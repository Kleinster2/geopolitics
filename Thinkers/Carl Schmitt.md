# Carl Schmitt

## Overview
German jurist and political theorist (1888-1985), widely considered one of the most important -- and most controversial -- political thinkers of the 20th century. His concepts of sovereignty, the friend-enemy distinction, the state of exception, and the Grossraum (great-space) theory remain foundational in political science, international law, and [[Geopolitics]]. He joined the Nazi Party in May 1933 and served as its chief legal theorist before falling from favor in 1936. His intellectual legacy is inseparable from his political complicity -- a tension that defines how his work is received today.

## Biography

### Early Life and Education
- Born July 11, 1888, in Plettenberg, Westphalia
- Catholic family in predominantly Protestant Prussia -- an outsider status that shaped his lifelong preoccupation with identity, belonging, and the political implications of homogeneity
- Studied law at Berlin, Munich, and Strasbourg
- Doctorate in law, 1910; habilitation, 1916

### Academic Career (Weimar Republic)
- Professor at University of Greifswald (1921)
- University of Bonn (1922-1928)
- Handelshochschule Berlin (1928-1933)
- University of Berlin (1933-1945) -- Germany's most prestigious chair in public law
- Published his most enduring works during the Weimar period: *Politische Theologie* (1922), *Die geistesgeschichtliche Lage des heutigen Parlamentarismus* (1923), *Der Begriff des Politischen* (1927/1932)
- Defended the Weimar constitution's emergency powers (Article 48) and served as legal adviser to President Hindenburg -- argued presidential dictatorship was constitutional

### Nazi Period (1933-1936)
- Joined the NSDAP on May 1, 1933 -- the same date as [[Martin Heidegger]]
- Quickly became the regime's leading legal theorist, earning the label "Crown Jurist of the Third Reich" (*Kronjurist des Dritten Reiches*)
- Provided legal justification for the Night of the Long Knives (June 30, 1934), writing "Der Fuhrer schutzt das Recht" ("The Fuhrer Protects the Law") -- argued Hitler's extrajudicial killings were themselves a form of law
- Organized the October 1936 conference "Das Judentum in der Rechtswissenschaft" ("German Jurisprudence in the Struggle Against the Jewish Spirit") at the University of Berlin, calling for the purging of Jewish influence from German legal scholarship
- His anti-Semitic writings from this period are unambiguous: he advocated marking Jewish authors' names in legal citations to identify their "racial" origin

### Fall from Favor (1936-1945)
- Attacked by the SS journal *Das Schwarze Korps* in late 1936, which questioned his ideological sincerity and noted his pre-1933 associations with Jewish intellectuals (including his friendship with [[Hugo Preuss]], the Jewish architect of the Weimar constitution)
- Lost institutional positions and Party influence but retained his Berlin professorship until 1945
- Published *Volkerrechtliche Grossraumordnung* (1939) and *Land und Meer* (1942) during this period

### Post-War (1945-1985)
- Detained by US forces, held at an internment camp in Berlin, 1945
- Interrogated at Nuremberg, 1947 -- as a potential witness and subject of investigation, not a defendant
- Robert Kempner, the American prosecutor, questioned Schmitt about his role in providing legal frameworks for Nazi expansion; Schmitt's interrogation transcripts (published in 2000 as *Antworten in Nurnberg*) reveal his defense: he was a theorist, not a decision-maker
- Refused to undergo denazification (*Entnazifizierung*), viewing the process as victor's justice and beneath his dignity
- Returned to Plettenberg, his birthplace, where he lived in intellectual exile for the remaining four decades of his life
- Continued writing prolifically: *Der Nomos der Erde* (1950), *Theorie des Partisanen* (1963), *Politische Theologie II* (1970)
- Received visitors and maintained extensive correspondence -- his Plettenberg home became an informal salon for a generation of European and international scholars, including [[Jacob Taubes]], [[Alexandre Kojeve]], and [[Ernst Jünger]]
- Died April 7, 1985, in Plettenberg, aged 96. Never expressed public remorse for his Nazi involvement

## Core Concepts

### The Friend-Enemy Distinction
*Der Begriff des Politischen* (*The Concept of the Political*, 1927; revised and expanded 1932)

The foundational claim:

> "The specific political distinction to which political actions and motives can be reduced is that between friend and enemy." (*Die spezifisch politische Unterscheidung, auf welche sich die politischen Handlungen und Motive zuruckfuhren lassen, ist die Unterscheidung von Freund und Feind.*)

Key arguments:
- The political is not a separate domain (like economics or aesthetics) but an intensity of association and dissociation -- any issue becomes political when it reaches the friend-enemy threshold
- The enemy is not a personal adversary (*inimicus*) but a public one (*hostis*) -- "the other, the stranger" whose existence represents a negation of one's own way of life
- A political community that cannot identify its enemy ceases to exist as a political entity
- Liberalism's attempt to dissolve the friend-enemy distinction into economic competition or moral discourse is, for Schmitt, a fundamental evasion of the political
- The extreme case -- the willingness to kill and die -- defines the political; war is not the aim but the ever-present possibility that gives politics its existential character

Relevance: Maps directly onto [[Realism]] in international relations. [[Hans Morgenthau]], the founder of modern IR realism, studied under Schmitt and acknowledged intellectual debts, though he later distanced himself. The friend-enemy concept anticipates the realist insistence that conflict is structural, not accidental.

### Sovereignty and the State of Exception
*Politische Theologie: Vier Kapitel zur Lehre von der Souveranitat* (*Political Theology: Four Chapters on the Concept of Sovereignty*, 1922)

The most quoted sentence in modern political theory:

> "Sovereign is he who decides on the exception." (*Souveran ist, wer uber den Ausnahmezustand entscheidet.*)

Key arguments:
- [[Sovereignty]] cannot be defined by normal operations of law -- it is revealed only in the moment when the legal order is suspended
- The "exception" (*Ausnahmezustand* -- state of exception, state of emergency) is the situation that cannot be anticipated by existing law and requires a sovereign decision
- All significant concepts of the modern theory of the state are "secularized theological concepts" -- sovereignty = God's omnipotence; the exception = the miracle; the legal order = the natural order
- The sovereign stands both inside and outside the legal order: inside because he upholds it, outside because he can suspend it
- This is not a theory of tyranny but of the irreducibility of decision: every legal system depends on a decision that precedes and grounds the law itself

Relevance: Directly applicable to emergency powers, martial law, executive overreach. [[Giorgio Agamben]] (Italian philosopher) built his entire *Homo Sacer* project (1995-2015) on extending Schmitt's state of exception into a theory of modern biopolitics, arguing that the exception has become the rule in contemporary governance. The concept resonates with debates over [[War Powers Resolution]], [[Insurrection Act]], and executive authority in wartime -- including the Trump administration's expansive use of [[IEEPA]] and emergency declarations.

### Critique of Liberalism and Parliamentarism
*Die geistesgeschichtliche Lage des heutigen Parlamentarismus* (*The Crisis of Parliamentary Democracy*, 1923)

Key arguments:
- Parliamentary democracy rests on two principles that are not identical and may be incompatible: liberalism (government through public discussion, transparency, separation of powers) and democracy (rule by the people, popular sovereignty, will of the majority)
- Liberalism requires endless deliberation, compromise, proceduralism; democracy requires decision, homogeneity, collective will
- Parliamentary government worked when the bourgeoisie that staffed it shared sufficient cultural and class homogeneity to make "discussion" productive
- Once mass politics arrives and genuine antagonisms enter parliament, deliberation becomes theater -- parties negotiate behind closed doors, and the parliamentary floor is a stage, not a forum
- Democracy, in its essence, requires homogeneity -- a people that recognizes itself as a political unity. This is what makes democracy compatible with dictatorship but incompatible with liberalism: a dictator can express the will of a homogeneous people more authentically than a liberal parliament divided by pluralism
- This homogeneity argument has been criticized as a proto-fascist rejection of pluralism

### Grossraum Theory
*Volkerrechtliche Grossraumordnung mit Interventionsverbot fur raumfremde Machte* (*The Grossraum Order of International Law with a Ban on Intervention for Spatially Foreign Powers*, 1939)

This is Schmitt's most geopolitically consequential concept and the one that most directly connects to 2026.

Key arguments:
- International law should be organized not around individual sovereign states (the Westphalian model) but around Grossraume -- "great spaces" defined by a leading power (*Raumtrager*, "space-bearer") that sets the political and legal norms for its region
- The model is explicitly the [[Monroe Doctrine]]: the United States declared the Western Hemisphere closed to European interference and established itself as the leading power that determines the region's political character
- Schmitt generalized this: if the US can claim a hemisphere, so can Germany, so can Japan, so can any power capable of organizing a great space
- Each Grossraum excludes intervention by "spatially foreign powers" (*raumfremde Machte*) -- a principle of non-interference that applies only to outsiders, not to the leading power within the space
- The Grossraum is not identical to Lebensraum (living space), which is a biological-racial concept; the Grossraum is a political-juridical concept about spatial ordering. Schmitt made this distinction, though the practical overlap in 1939 was obvious
- The concept was a sophisticated legal framework for German hegemony in Europe -- Schmitt was providing international law theory for what the Wehrmacht was doing on the ground

The Monroe Doctrine connection in detail:
- Schmitt's 1939 essay opens with an extended analysis of the Monroe Doctrine as the "first and to this day most successful example" of a Grossraum principle
- He argues that the Doctrine was originally a genuine spatial principle -- the Western Hemisphere as a politically distinct zone -- but that the US corrupted it by universalizing its claims (Wilsonianism, the League of Nations), turning a spatial principle into an ideological one
- Germany, he argued, should return to the spatial principle: a European Grossraum under German leadership, excluding Anglo-American interference
- The irony: Trump's 2026 [[Greater North America]] doctrine and the [[Donroe Doctrine]] revive precisely the Monroe Doctrine structure that Schmitt theorized -- a leading power defining a regional space and excluding outside interference. Trump's explicit invocation of the Monroe Doctrine, the Pentagon's "immediate security perimeter" from Greenland to Ecuador, and the ban on Chinese/Russian influence in the hemisphere all echo the Grossraum logic, though without the theoretical apparatus

### The Nomos of the Earth
*Der Nomos der Erde im Volkerrecht des Jus Publicum Europaeum* (*The Nomos of the Earth in the International Law of the Jus Publicum Europaeum*, 1950)

Schmitt's magnum opus, written during the war but published after.

Key arguments:
- "Nomos" means more than law -- it is the foundational act of spatial ordering, the original appropriation (Landnahme) of land that establishes a legal and political order
- The European international law system (*jus publicum Europaeum*) that lasted from roughly 1648 to 1914 was built on a specific nomos: the spatial division between European soil (where states were sovereign and war was "bracketed" -- limited, rule-governed, between recognized combatants) and the non-European world (open for colonization, where no rules applied)
- This "bracketing of war" (*Hegung des Krieges*) was the great achievement of European international law: war was not abolished but civilized -- fought between uniformed armies, governed by rules, terminated by peace treaties. The combatant/civilian distinction held
- The collapse of the *jus publicum Europaeum* began with the universalization of international law (League of Nations, UN) and the criminalization of war (aggression as a crime, war crimes tribunals). By making war illegal rather than regulated, the new system paradoxically made wars more total -- since the enemy is now a criminal, not a legitimate combatant, the restraints dissolve
- The post-1945 order, dominated by the US, lacks a spatial nomos: it claims to be universal but is actually a disguised American hegemony. A new nomos has not yet emerged

### Land and Sea
*Land und Meer: Eine weltgeschichtliche Betrachtung* (*Land and Sea: A World-Historical Meditation*, 1942)

A short book, written for Schmitt's daughter Anima:
- World history is shaped by the fundamental opposition between telluric (land-based) and thalassocratic (sea-based) civilizations
- Land powers (Rome, Russia, Germany) think in terms of territory, borders, fixed spatial orders
- Sea powers (Athens, Britain, the US) think in terms of movement, commerce, global reach, control of chokepoints
- The discovery of the New World and British naval supremacy created a maritime age that displaced the land-based nomos of Europe
- The US inherited Britain's maritime character and transformed it into a global spatial order based on air power and technological dominance
- [[Alexander Dugin]], the Russian geopolitical theorist, explicitly draws on Schmitt's land/sea distinction in his neo-Eurasianist framework

### Theory of the Partisan
*Theorie des Partisanen: Zwischenbemerkung zum Begriff des Politischen* (*Theory of the Partisan: Intermediate Commentary on the Concept of the Political*, 1963)

A late work that updates the friend-enemy concept for irregular warfare:
- The partisan (guerrilla, insurgent) is defined by four characteristics: irregularity, political engagement, mobility, and "telluric character" -- rootedness in a specific territory being defended
- The telluric partisan (Mao, the Spanish guerrillas against Napoleon) is fundamentally defensive: fighting for a homeland
- The danger is the "world-revolutionary partisan" (Lenin's concept) who fights not for territory but for an ideology, and therefore recognizes no spatial or legal limits
- The partisan destroys the bracketing of war: no uniforms, no front lines, no distinction between combatant and civilian
- Directly relevant to asymmetric warfare, counterinsurgency, and the post-9/11 "War on Terror"
- The IRGC's adoption of "partisan warfare tactics" in the [[2026 Iran conflict]] -- shifting from conventional military resistance to dispersed, irregular operations after their conventional capacity was degraded -- exemplifies the dynamic Schmitt analyzed

### The Katechon
From *Der Nomos der Erde* and *Politische Theologie*:
- Drawn from 2 Thessalonians 2:6-7 -- the "restrainer" (*ho katechon*) who holds back the coming of the Antichrist and the end of the world
- Schmitt secularizes this: every political order depends on a katechontic force that holds back chaos, dissolution, the collapse of order
- The Holy Roman Empire functioned as the medieval katechon; the *jus publicum Europaeum* was its modern successor
- When the katechon fails, the result is not freedom but anomie -- the dissolution of all political form
- This theological-political vision undergirds Schmitt's conservatism: political order is not natural but requires active maintenance against the forces of dissolution

## Influence on Contemporary Thought

### Left Schmittians
- [[Chantal Mouffe]] (Belgian political theorist): Built "agonistic pluralism" on Schmitt's critique of liberalism -- accepts the friend-enemy distinction but argues democratic politics should channel antagonism into agonism (adversaries rather than enemies). Her *The Challenge of Carl Schmitt* (1999) and *On the Political* (2005) are key texts
- [[Giorgio Agamben]] (Italian philosopher): Extended the state of exception into a theory of modern sovereignty in *Homo Sacer* (1995) and *State of Exception* (2003). Argues that the camp (concentration camp, refugee camp, Guantanamo) is the nomos of modernity -- the space where the exception becomes permanent. The most influential contemporary extension of Schmitt
- [[Jacques Derrida]]: Engaged with Schmitt's friend-enemy concept in *Politics of Friendship* (1994), deconstructing the binary and exploring what political thought looks like without stable identity categories

### Right Schmittians and the Leo Strauss Exchange
- [[Leo Strauss]] (German-American political philosopher): Wrote a famous review of *The Concept of the Political* in 1932 that Schmitt himself acknowledged as the sharpest critique. Strauss argued that Schmitt, despite wanting to affirm the political against liberalism, remained trapped in a liberal horizon -- his critique was parasitic on liberalism. Their exchange is one of the defining intellectual encounters of the 20th century. Strauss left Germany (with Schmitt's help on a Rockefeller fellowship recommendation) and founded the Straussian school at the University of Chicago
- [[Alexander Dugin]] (Russian political theorist): Explicitly uses Schmitt's land/sea distinction and Grossraum theory in his neo-Eurasianist geopolitics. Dugin's concept of a Eurasian Grossraum under Russian leadership, opposing the Atlantic (US-British) thalassocracy, is a direct application of Schmitt. Dugin has called Schmitt "the most brilliant jurist and political philosopher of the 20th century"
- The European New Right (Alain de Benoist, Guillaume Faye): Drew heavily on Schmitt's critique of liberalism, his friend-enemy distinction, and his Grossraum theory as alternatives to both liberal universalism and crude nationalism

### IR Theory Connections
- [[Hans Morgenthau]]: Studied with Schmitt in the early 1930s, absorbed the friend-enemy framework, and translated it into the realist tradition in American IR. His *Politics Among Nations* (1948) reflects Schmitt's insistence that politics is fundamentally about power and conflict, not law and morality
- The "realist" tradition in IR (Morgenthau, [[John Mearsheimer]], [[Stephen Walt]]) operates in a conceptual space that Schmitt helped define, even when practitioners are unaware of the genealogy

### Chinese Reception
- Schmitt is widely read in Chinese political theory and legal scholarship, particularly at Peking University and Tsinghua University
- Chinese scholars have found Schmitt useful for theorizing strong executive authority, the primacy of the political over the legal, and the legitimacy of a homogeneous political order -- all relevant to [[Xi Jinping]]'s governance model
- The Grossraum concept resonates with China's own claims to regional preeminence in East Asia -- a Chinese Monroe Doctrine that excludes "spatially foreign" (i.e., American) interference
- Key Chinese Schmitt scholars include Liu Xiaofeng (Sun Yat-sen University) and Chen Duanhong (Peking University Law)

## Controversies and Moral Complexity

### The Central Tension
Schmitt was simultaneously:
1. A genuine intellectual giant whose concepts remain analytically indispensable -- sovereignty, exception, friend-enemy, nomos, Grossraum, partisan theory are part of the permanent vocabulary of political thought
2. A committed Nazi who provided legal justifications for the regime, organized anti-Semitic purges of legal scholarship, and never expressed remorse

This is not a case of a thinker whose politics can be separated from his theory. Schmitt's authoritarianism, his contempt for liberal proceduralism, his insistence on decision over deliberation -- these are features of his thought, not biographical accidents. The question is whether the analytical power of the concepts survives their political origin.

### The Heidegger Comparison
Schmitt's case parallels that of [[Martin Heidegger]] -- another German intellectual giant who joined the Nazi Party on the same date (May 1, 1933) and whose philosophical legacy is entangled with his political commitments. Both refused genuine reckoning after the war. Both remained enormously influential despite (and sometimes because of) the controversy. The key difference: Heidegger's philosophy is less directly political than Schmitt's legal theory, making the entanglement harder to disentangle for Schmitt.

### How Scholars Navigate This
- Most political scientists and legal theorists treat Schmitt the way physicists treat Werner von Braun -- use the work, acknowledge the history, refuse to pretend the two are unrelated
- Some (notably the Frankfurt School's Jurgen Habermas) argue that Schmitt's concepts are inherently authoritarian and should be opposed rather than appropriated
- Others (Mouffe, Agamben) argue that the concepts can be turned against Schmitt's own politics -- that understanding the friend-enemy distinction is essential precisely in order to prevent its worst applications
- The pragmatic consensus: his work is taught in every serious political science and law program because the concepts are too powerful to ignore, but always with the biographical and political context foregrounded
