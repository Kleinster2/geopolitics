# Jack Goldsmith

## Overview
Harvard Law professor and former Assistant Attorney General (Office of Legal Counsel, 2003-2004). Leading scholar on internet governance, national security law, and the limits of international law. Co-authored influential critique of "internet freedom" ideology, arguing cyberspace is increasingly shaped by national borders and state power.

## Key Contributions

### The Internet and State Power
With [[Tim Wu]], wrote "Who Controls the Internet?" (2006), challenging early "cyberspace exceptionalism." Key arguments:
- Governments can and do control internet activity within borders
- Territorial law applies to cyberspace
- The "borderless internet" was always an illusion
- States have become effective at internet regulation

### Splinternet Thesis
Early articulator of internet fragmentation:
- China's Great Firewall proves territorial control possible
- Other states following with national internet controls
- Technical architecture increasingly reflects political borders
- Global internet giving way to national/regional internets

### National Security Law
Beyond internet governance, Goldsmith is influential on:
- Executive power and war on terror
- Resigned from OLC over torture memos (withdrew some)
- Surveillance law and Fourth Amendment
- Foreign interference and election security

## Contemporary Relevance

### Internet Sovereignty Debate
Goldsmith's work prefigured current debates:
- US vs. China models of internet governance
- EU's digital sovereignty push
- Russia's "sovereign internet" law
- Debate over encryption and law enforcement access

### Platform Regulation
His territorial law arguments inform debates over:
- Content moderation jurisdiction
- Data localization requirements
- Cross-border data flows
- Tech company liability
