# Richard Haass

## Overview

Former CFR president (2003-2023), senior Bush 41 and Bush 43 official, and architect of the "war of choice vs. war of necessity" framework. His critique of the 2026 Iran war carries particular weight because he is a centrist establishment figure — not a restrainer or dove — who supported the 1991 Gulf War and served in Republican administrations. He calls the Iran war a "preventive war" that weakens global norms.

## Key Framework: Wars of Choice vs. Wars of Necessity

From *War of Necessity, War of Choice* (2009):

- **Wars of necessity**: Vital national interests under imminent threat, diplomatic alternatives exhausted. Example: 1991 Gulf War (Kuwait invasion, coalition assembled, UN mandate).
- **Wars of choice**: Interests involved but threats are not imminent, alternatives exist. Example: 2003 Iraq invasion (no imminent WMD threat, inspectors could have continued, coalition thinner).
- **Preventive vs. preemptive**: Preemptive war (against imminent attack) has some international law support; preventive war (against a future possibility) does not.

This framework is the lens through which Haass evaluates the 2026 Iran war.

## 2026 Iran War Commentary

### "A Questionable War of Choice" (Substack, February 28, 2026)

Published the day of the strikes. Called it a war of choice, not necessity: Iran posed no imminent threat, was not on the verge of becoming a nuclear weapons state. Critiqued the regime change objective as political rather than military — military force "can destroy and kill but cannot bring about regime change on its own." The political opposition was not united or functioning as a government-in-waiting.

### "Epic Folly" (Substack, March 12, 2026)

Major two-week assessment. The war:
1. Weakens the global norm against preventive war
2. Weakens the norm against political assassination as a tool of national security
3. Costs far outweigh benefits

Iran still has ability to produce drones, launch missiles, and lay mines, bringing Strait of Hormuz shipping to a near halt. Both rationales for continuing (degrading capabilities, regime change) are infeasible. Called for reviving diplomacy with a phased approach (ceasefire then formal accord).

## Significance for the Entrapment Debate

Haass's critique matters because of who he is: a former NSC senior director who supported the 1991 Gulf War as necessary, served in two Republican administrations, and ran the CFR for 20 years. When Pillar or Parsi call the war unnecessary, hawks can dismiss them as restrainers. When Haass calls it "Epic Folly" using the same framework he used to support Desert Storm, the critique is harder to dismiss.
