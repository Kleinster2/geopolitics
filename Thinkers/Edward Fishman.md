# Edward Fishman

## Overview
Scholar and former government official specializing in economic statecraft — sanctions, export controls, and the weaponization of global economic networks. Author of *Chokepoints: American Power in the Age of Economic Warfare* (2025), a defining work on how the US wields control over financial, technological, and energy chokepoints to confront rivals. Now directs CFR's geoeconomics center.

## Key Facts
- **Position**: Senior Fellow and Director, Maurice R. Greenberg Center for Geoeconomic Studies, [[Council on Foreign Relations]] (appointed January 2026)
- **Academic**: Adjunct Professor, Columbia University SIPA; Senior Research Scholar, Center on Global Energy Policy, Columbia
- **Previous**: Adjunct Senior Fellow, [[CNAS]]
- **Education**: B.A. History, Yale (Phi Beta Kappa); M.Phil. International Relations, Cambridge; M.B.A., Stanford (Arjay Miller Scholar)

## Major Works

### *Chokepoints: American Power in the Age of Economic Warfare* (2025)
Published by Penguin Random House (February 2025). Examines how the US and allies wield control over economic chokepoints — the dollar, advanced semiconductors, energy supply chain nodes — to project power against Russia, China, and Iran.

## Analytical Framework

### Economic Chokepoints as Power
Fishman's central argument: the US derives coercive power not primarily from military dominance but from controlling nodes in global economic networks — dollar clearing, semiconductor supply chains, SWIFT, energy infrastructure. These "chokepoints" allow Washington to surveil, sanction, and exclude adversaries from systems they depend on.

### Tensions He Identifies
- Overuse of economic weapons drives de-dollarization and alternative system development
- Sanctions effectiveness degrades as targets adapt
- Technology denial accelerates adversary self-sufficiency efforts
- Weaponization risks fragmenting the very networks that give the US leverage

## Teaching
Teaches "Geoeconomic Fragmentation" seminar at Columbia SIPA — examines how sanctions, tariffs, export controls, and industrial policies are reshaping global finance, energy markets, supply chains, and the technology industry.
