# Stephen Walt

## Overview
Leading American international relations scholar and neorealist theorist. Professor at Harvard Kennedy School. Known for "balance of threat" theory and critique of US foreign policy, particularly the Israel lobby's influence. In 2026, coined "predatory hegemony" to describe Trump's approach to allies.

## Key Facts
- **Position**: Robert and Renée Belfer Professor of International Affairs, Harvard Kennedy School
- **Education**: PhD, UC Berkeley
- **School**: Neorealism / Defensive Realism
- **Orientation**: Realist, restraint advocate

## Major Works

### Books
- *The Origins of Alliances* (1987) - Balance of threat theory
- *Revolution and War* (1996)
- *Taming American Power* (2005)
- *The Israel Lobby and U.S. Foreign Policy* (2007, with John Mearsheimer) - Controversial critique
- *The Hell of Good Intentions* (2018) - Critique of liberal hegemony

### Key Concepts

**Balance of Threat Theory**
- States balance against threats, not just power
- Threat = power + proximity + offensive capability + aggressive intent
- Explains alliance formation better than pure balance of power

**Offshore Balancing**
- US should avoid direct military presence in Eurasia
- Let regional powers balance each other
- Intervene only when necessary to prevent hegemony
- Less costly, more sustainable

## On Iran and the Middle East

### JCPOA defense

Walt has been a consistent defender of the JCPOA and diplomatic engagement with Iran:

- "The Case Against the Iranian Nuclear Deal Is One Big Lie" (*Foreign Policy*, September 2017) — used a thought experiment to argue the opposition case was intellectually dishonest
- On Trump's 2018 withdrawal: called it "the most consequential foreign-policy blunder yet" and warned "the United States cannot be trusted to keep its promises" (Belfer Center/HKS reaction, May 2018)
- In *The Hell of Good Intentions* (2018): Threatening regime change for 25 years "is probably not the best way to convince them they don't need a nuclear deterrent"

### 2025: Israel and the Middle East

- "Israel Can't Be the Middle East's Hegemon" (*Foreign Policy*, June 16, 2025) — argued Israeli military dominance cannot substitute for political settlement; Israel "has long opposed US efforts to improve relations with Iran and hoped to convince the US to support regime change goals"
- "The Middle East Looks Toward a Grim 2026" (*Foreign Policy*, January 1, 2026) — warned Hamas, Hezbollah, and Iran were "down but not out"; Israel continuing to use military power preemptively
- "How the World Changed After Oct. 7" (*Foreign Policy*, October 6, 2025) — one-year retrospective; referenced Jake Sullivan's pre-Oct 7 claim the Middle East was "quieter than it has been for decades"

### 2026 Iran war commentary

**"Trump's Iran War Shows the United States Is Still Addicted to Military Conflict"** (*Foreign Policy*, March 2, 2026): Called the Iran war "the least necessary shedding of blood by the U.S. military since the invasion of Iraq in 2003."

**The addiction thesis**: Walt traces the pattern across every post-Cold War president:
- **Clinton** (1992): Won saying "it's the economy, stupid." In office: missile strikes, no-fly zones, Kosovo.
- **Bush** (2000): Promised a "humble foreign policy." In office: Afghanistan, Iraq, GWOT.
- **Obama** (2008): Promised to end "foolish wars." In office: Libya, drone campaigns, Afghanistan surge.
- **Trump** (2016): Called US foreign policy a "complete and total disaster." In office: did the opposite.

Walt: "Every one of the last four presidents ran for office promising to correct the excesses of their predecessor and to focus on problems at home... Yet once in office, every one of these presidents did the opposite of what they had promised."

**Structural explanation**: This happens "because we can" — the US has such overwhelming military capability that "the use of force has become too easy and seemingly risk-free." The foreign policy establishment ("the blob") consistently pushes toward intervention regardless of party.

**On regime change skepticism** (NPR, March 2026): "I don't think there's ever been a successful regime change solely accomplished with airpower. It always requires some kind of ground force involvement."

**On the Israel Lobby connection**: Walt has been more cautious than Mearsheimer in applying the Israel Lobby framework directly to the 2026 war. His March 2 column frames the war through the "addiction to force" lens rather than the lobby lens. The Washington Institute noted Walt "is walking back his Israel Lobby theory beyond recognition" — suggesting he is presenting the argument more carefully in the current environment.

## 2026: "Predatory Hegemony" Concept

### Definition
From GZERO World interview (January 2026):

> "All great powers are predators to some degree, but they usually confine their acts of predation to their adversaries... What Trump has done is he's applying that to our allies as well. In all bilateral relationships, his view is: what's mine is mine, what's yours is negotiable."

### Foreign Affairs essay

"The Predatory Hegemon: How Trump Wields American Power" (*Foreign Affairs*, March/April 2026): Defines predatory hegemony as "a dominant great power that views all bilateral relations as inherently zero-sum and seeks to extract the greatest possible benefits from each one." Draws historical parallels to Athens' treatment of Delian League allies, European colonialism, Soviet exploitation of Eastern Europe. Argues this strategy "will weaken the United States and its allies alike, generate growing global resentment, create tempting opportunities for Washington's main rivals."

### Key Features
- Predation against **allies**, not just adversaries
- Extracting maximum concessions from everyone
- More effective against dependent allies (short term)
- Long-term loser as allies diversify

### Examples Cited
- South Korea: Tariff threats → forced investment concessions
- Greenland: No security need, about "marking territory"
- Venezuela: Not about drugs/democracy, about hemispheric control

### Allied Response
Countries that pushed back (Brazil, China) did better than accommodators.

## On Trump's Foreign Policy

### Speed vs. Direction
> "I'm surprised not by the direction things have gone, but by the sort of speed and scope by which things have changed."

### Use of Force
- Trump prefers "short, sharp, discrete uses of force, primarily air power"
- Picks on weak countries that can't retaliate
- Not averse to force, averse to forever wars

### Greenland Assessment
> "There's no economic or security reason why we have to [control Greenland]. The current treaty arrangements give the United States as much access as it might ever want. This is all about essentially marking territory."

### Venezuela Assessment
- Not about narcotics (Venezuela not major drug source)
- Not about democracy (old regime left in place)
- Not about security threat
- Really about putting Latin America on notice: "This is our hemisphere and we're going to run it"

### Long-term Damage
Most concerned about: Gutting of research and development, assault on universities
> "The next several presidents are going to deal with the consequences of the damage that's been done to our research and development capabilities."
