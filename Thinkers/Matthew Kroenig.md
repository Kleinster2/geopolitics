# Matthew Kroenig

## Overview

Nuclear strategist and one of Washington's most prominent Iran hawks. Has advocated military strikes on Iran since 2012 — the 2026 war represents the policy he has pushed for over a decade. Combines academic work at Georgetown with a senior leadership role at the Atlantic Council's Scowcroft Center.

## On Iran

### The decade-long case for strikes

Kroenig has advocated US military strikes on Iran since his 2012 *Foreign Affairs* article. His time as Special Advisor on Iran Policy at the Pentagon (2010-2011) shaped his assessment of military options. Critics charged that he combined "alarmist worst case of the Iranian nuclear threat with the rosiest best case of America's ability to make things better."

His framework: a nuclear-armed Iran would (1) provide a nuclear umbrella for proxies like Hezbollah and Hamas, (2) trigger cascading proliferation in Saudi Arabia, Turkey, and Egypt, (3) damage the NPT regime permanently. Military action is therefore the "least bad" option compared to deterrence or containment.

### 2026 Iran war commentary

**"Trump's Decision Will Bring Security and Possibly Democracy"** (*Foreign Policy*, March 1, 2026): Praised the strikes as a historic moment. Argued they remove one member of the "axis of aggressors" and strengthen the US position vis-a-vis Russia and China. The constant Iran threat had prevented the US from pivoting to Asia.

**Foreign Policy debate with [[Trita Parsi]]** (March 12, 2026): Argued Iran "violated key principles of the rules-based international order for decades" — threatening neighbors, sponsoring terrorism, pursuing nuclear weapons. "It falls on the United States to enforce the rules."

**Atlantic Council "Twenty Questions" on the Iran War** (March 11, 2026): Described Trump's approach as "peace through strength doctrine" — "quite willing to use force in short, sharp, decisive ways."

**NPR**: Said he would have advocated "a more limited strike" to induce "token retaliation" and hope for de-escalation — suggesting even Kroenig considered the actual operation's scope excessive.
