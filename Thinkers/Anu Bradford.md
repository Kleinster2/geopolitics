# Anu Bradford

Finnish-American legal scholar at Columbia Law School, known for two frameworks that explain how regulatory power is exercised globally without military or market dominance.

## The Brussels Effect

In *The Brussels Effect: How the European Union Rules the World* (2020), Bradford argues that the EU exports its rules worldwide not by force but by the gravity of its single market. Because the EU market is too large to forgo and it is usually cheaper for multinationals to apply one (the strictest) standard globally than to maintain separate product lines, EU rules become de facto global standards. She distinguishes:

- **De facto Brussels Effect** — firms voluntarily globalize EU compliance for efficiency.
- **De jure Brussels Effect** — other governments copy EU rules into their own law.

GDPR is the canonical case; the [[AI Act]] and EU Data Act are the EU's attempt to repeat it for AI and data. The Brussels Effect is the EU's substitute for de facto standard-setting power — it cannot make the dominant platforms, so it writes the rules they must follow.

## Digital Empires — the three regulatory models

In *Digital Empires: The Global Battle to Regulate Technology* (2023), Bradford maps the contest between three regulatory models:

| Model | Power | Logic |
|-------|-------|-------|
| United States | Market-driven | Light regulation; private firms and innovation set the terms |
| China | State-driven | The state directs technology toward national and party goals |
| European Union | Rights-driven | Regulation protects fundamental rights and exports norms (the Brussels Effect) |

This maps closely onto the three models of standard-setting in [[Technical Standards]] (US firm-led, China state-led, EU regulation-led).

## Vault use

The reference for how the EU projects power through rule-writing rather than invention or coercion — central to [[Digital Sovereignty]] and the EU column of the standards-competition story.
