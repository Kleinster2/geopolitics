# Robert Pape

## Overview
Robert Anthony Pape (b. 1960) is Professor of Political Science at the University of Chicago and founder/director of the Chicago Project on Security and Threats (CPOST). One of the most prominent academic voices on airpower effectiveness and the strategic logic of asymmetric conflict. Advised the Obama campaign (2008) and briefly Ron Paul on Iraq policy. Has become a central voice on the [[2026 Iran conflict]], arguing that the US has walked into an "escalation trap" where each attempt to control the war creates pressures for further escalation.

## Background
- BA and MA, University of Pittsburgh (1982, summa cum laude, Phi Beta Kappa, Truman Scholar)
- PhD, University of Chicago (1988)
- Taught at US Air Force School of Advanced Airpower Studies (1991-1994)
- Dartmouth College (1994-1999)
- University of Chicago (1999-present, tenured)
- Director of graduate studies, Political Science; former chair, Committee on International Relations

## Key Works

### *Bombing to Win: Air Power and Coercion in War* (1996)
Foundational challenge to the conventional wisdom that coercive airpower is both effective and cheap. Pape argues that bombing campaigns intended to motivate citizens to rise against their government typically backfire, producing greater resilience and loyalty. Advocates integrating air and land power ("hammer and anvil") rather than relying on airpower alone.

### *Dying to Win: The Strategic Logic of Suicide Terrorism* (2005)
Challenged the assumption that suicide terrorism is irrational or driven primarily by religious fundamentalism. Argued there is a strategic logic: suicide terrorism is an effective method for compelling democracies to withdraw forces from territory the terrorists consider homeland. Found that most suicide attackers come from middle-class, educated backgrounds — not poverty. Controversial: critics (Ashworth et al., Horowitz) challenged the research design for sampling on the dependent variable.

### *Cutting the Fuse* (2010, with James K. Feldman)
Extended the suicide terrorism analysis to 2,100+ attacks. Recommended avoiding troop deployments perceived as occupation, minimizing cultural threat, treating collateral damage with great sensitivity.

### Economic sanctions research (1997-1998)
Contested the effectiveness of international economic sanctions, arguing only 5% achieve policy goals (vs. 34% claimed by other scholars). Directly relevant to the debate over Iran sanctions regimes.

## Current Work: The Escalation Trap

Pape has become the most systematic academic critic of US strategy in the [[2026 Iran conflict]], publishing across Foreign Affairs, CNBC, and his own Substack (escalationtrap.substack.com). His framework centers on three concepts:

### Horizontal Escalation
See [[Horizontal Escalation]] for the full concept note. Pape's core argument: Iran cannot defeat the US or Israel in a conventional contest and does not need to. Instead, it widens the geographic and political scope of the conflict — striking across nine countries, disrupting energy markets, politicizing Gulf basing arrangements — to transform the war from a military contest into one of political endurance. This is not flailing retaliation; it is a recognizable strategic pattern with historical precedent.

### The Escalation Trap
Pape's original contribution: the dynamic where efforts to *control* a conflict create pressures that make *major escalation* more likely. The US launched precision decapitation strikes expecting to produce quick political collapse. When that failed, each subsequent move to "win" created new incentives for Iran to widen and deepen the conflict. "The search for control produced the loss of control."

### Disruption vs. Damage Threshold
Pape's March 20, 2026 Substack post formalizes a critical distinction: the current phase of the war is characterized by *disruption* (shipping delays, insurance shocks, temporary flow reductions) that is reversible if violence stops. The next phase — ground operations and reciprocal infrastructure attacks — will produce *damage* (destroyed export terminals, refineries, offshore platforms) that removes physical capacity for months or longer. "Disruption delays flows. Damage removes them." This is the threshold between a contained episode and a global economic crisis.

### Historical Analogies
Pape draws on two cases to illustrate horizontal escalation:

**Vietnam (1965-1968):** Operation Rolling Thunder dropped three times the WWII tonnage on North Vietnam by 1967. Appeared to devastate all critical infrastructure. Yet the January 1968 Tet Offensive — coordinated attacks on 100+ cities — shattered perceptions of imminent victory and broke LBJ's presidency. "The United States never lost a battle — but it still lost a war."

**Kosovo (1999):** Operation Allied Force was planned as a 3-day, 51-target precision campaign. Instead, Serbia ordered 30,000 troops into Kosovo, expelled a million Albanian civilians, and forced NATO into 78 days of sustained crisis requiring 40,000 ground troops. Precision strikes shocked but did not determine political outcomes.

### Policy Prescription
Pape frames the US as facing a "strategic fork": double down (permanent aerial containment, risking a repeat of the Iraq trajectory from no-fly zones to 2003 ground invasion) or stand down (declare objectives met, accept short-term political cost, avoid compounding losses). He argues the latter is wiser: "accept a limited loss now rather than risk compounding losses later."

### Oil-price normalization as the Iran deal test (May 2026)

Pape's May 27 Escalation Trap post was paid beyond the public preview, but the preview gives a clean late-war diagnostic: if oil remains above $90 after [[Strait of Hormuz]] is nominally reopened, the strategic result is no longer just the physical ability to move ships. It is the market's belief that Gulf passage has become structurally dangerous.

That matters for the statecraft file because it shifts the center of negotiation from uranium alone to the operating regime of the strait. A US-preferred deal that removes mines, prohibits tolls, and restores unrestricted passage is a different settlement from an Iran-preferred arrangement where Tehran regulates traffic and levies fees. The first tries to restore the pre-war commons; the second converts Hormuz into a permissioned chokepoint under Iranian leverage.

Reuters' May 29 oil tape showed the test in real time: Brent was down sharply on ceasefire-extension hopes but still around $91 while traffic remained only a small fraction of pre-war levels. Pape's useful contribution is the distinction between reopening and normalization.

## Assessment

Pape's work on airpower effectiveness is foundational — *Bombing to Win* is one of the most cited books in the field. His suicide terrorism research is more contested methodologically but influential. His current escalation trap framework synthesizes his career-long skepticism of airpower-centric strategies into a real-time analytical lens on the Iran war.

**Strengths:** Empirically grounded, historically informed, willing to make specific predictions. His disruption-to-damage threshold framework provides concrete observable indicators rather than abstract theorizing.

**Limitations:** His framework may underweight the possibility that Iran's horizontal escalation strategy fails — Gulf states could absorb the pressure and rally *against* Iran (as Ryan Bohl argues). The Vietnam and Kosovo analogies, while instructive, are imperfect: Iran's conventional capacity is far more degraded than North Vietnam's was in 1968, and the regional coalition opposing it is more cohesive than Pape's analysis suggests.
