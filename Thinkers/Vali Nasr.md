# Vali Nasr

## Overview
Iranian-American scholar and former US government official specializing in Iran, political Islam, and Middle East politics. Professor at Johns Hopkins SAIS and Senior Advisor at CSIS. Served in the Obama administration State Department.

## Key Facts
- **Position**: Majid Khadduri Professor of International Affairs and Middle East Studies, Johns Hopkins University SAIS
- **Former SAIS Dean**: 2012-2019
- **Other roles**: Senior Advisor, Center for Strategic and International Studies (CSIS)
- **Government experience**: Senior Advisor to US Special Representative for Afghanistan and Pakistan (Holbrooke), 2009-2011
- **Education**: PhD Political Science, MIT; MA Fletcher School (international economics, Middle East studies)
- **Background**: Iranian-American
- **Key books**: *The Shia Revival* (2006), *The Dispensable Nation* (2013), *Iran's Grand Strategy: A Political History* (Princeton, 2025)

## Analysis: Iran Protests (January 2026)

From PBS "Compass Points" panel discussion:

### On Regime Vulnerability
- Regime is "very vulnerable" and has been "on its death march for some time"
- But argues this point was NOT when it would collapse
- People looking to outside intervention means they don't feel capable of bringing regime down themselves
- "Balance of force still lies with the regime"

### On Regime Structure
- Islamic Republic is "not one man" - differs from 1979 Shah scenario
- It's a system, a regime - perhaps 10% of population dedicated to it
- "Designed to survive, not be popular"
- "At war with the US for 40 years, at war with its own population increasingly"
- Multiple nodes of power = resilience
- That diversity is "one of the reasons it's resilient"

### On IRGC Cohesion
- No evidence of Revolutionary Guard fracturing
- No garrison went to the people
- No soldiers refusing to kill
- The shock of the killing itself shows regime resolve

### On US Options
- Questions what US is actually offering - "If it's in terms of surrender, then..."
- Witkoff's 4 demands could potentially be "finagled" with creative language
- Points to past consortium arrangements where Iran could claim enrichment without domestic enrichment
- "We're not talking about negotiations" - more like "Emperor Hirohito meeting General MacArthur"
- US hasn't laid out clear objectives - shifted from regime change to leadership change to "come to the table please"
- Administration hasn't laid out day-after plan
- "You can always start a war... but it may not pan out"
- Israeli 12-day war was militarily successful but "didn't get actually what it went for"

### On Risk of Escalation
- Danger is Iranian calculation "might be different" this time
- May decide they need to "draw blood" - not symbolic responses
- Cites Beirut barracks attack as example of Iran's capability for carnage. **[Vault note: Nasr said "1985" and "500 Marines" — the actual event was October 23, 1983, killing 241 US service members and 58 French paratroops. Verbal error, not analytical.]**

## Analysis: 2026 Iran War — Day 4 (March 4, 2026)

From Al Jazeera's The Bottom Line with Steve Clemons, March 4, 2026.

### On performative diplomacy
- Argues war was predictable: Trump had "no agenda for the negotiations," sent envoys "for a two-hour meeting unprepared with no details, no real offers."
- Omani FM's Washington visit (Feb 27) was "an act of desperation" — only VP Vance saw him; Trump in Texas, said he was "not happy" with Iran.
- "We ultimately learned that the planning for the war had been done much earlier between Israel and the United States and the diplomacy was just performative."

### On US loss of control
- "The United States has lost control of this war" — Iran surprised the US with its will to fight, the system's refusal to collapse, and IRGC not seeking a deal.
- Trump expected killing Khamenei would lead to a corrupt IRGC faction making a deal. "He didn't quite understand what he was biting into."
- Iran weaponizing energy prices and casting "a shadow of risk over the Gulf" — impacting markets, airlines, technology companies globally.

### On Israel's vs. America's war goals
- "Making Iran powerless makes sense for Israel as a war goal. But it's not clear to me why these are America's goals."
- If nuclear program already "obliterated" in June 2025, missiles can't reach US, and proxies are defanged — "what is so urgent?"
- "We're marketing what are Israel's war objectives essentially as urgent national security issues for the United States."

### On Netanyahu's "Sunni axis" and Israel's broader ambitions
- Netanyahu's February 22 "radical Sunni axis" statement signals Israel's next targets: Turkey, Qatar, Pakistan, Saudi Arabia.
- Bennett's "Turkey is the new Iran" (Feb 17) is "even sharper."
- Nasr: "Israel has come out of the Gaza war as triumphant, seeing itself as a military superpower... it wants to shape the Middle East in the way that it wants and doesn't want any rivals."
- If Rubio's admission means the US follows Israel's lead, "that makes the region very nervous."

### On decapitation as norm
- Warns of dangerous precedent: "Every country can vilify an opponent. Russia can say Zelensky is a Nazi terrorist. Why don't we decapitate him?"
- "When decapitation becomes routinized as an instrument of war, you have to worry about the international order going forward."

### On China/Russia
- Iran is "alone" — China and Russia help quietly but won't intervene openly.
- Their calculus: "Why get involved? Let America boil in its own stew."
- But "they're not doing anything to restrain Iran's attack on the Gulf" — understanding it's existential for Iran.

## Analysis: 2026 Iran War — Day 18 (March 17, 2026)

From Bloomberg's *The Mishal Husain Show*, interview with Mishal Husain. Edited for length and clarity; extended version available as podcast.

### On Iran's "last war" thesis

- Iran is "not ready to quit. They've put their teeth into the United States and they're not ready to let go."
- The strategic logic: "Their thinking is that this has to be the last war. Either they go down or the United States and Israel will abandon the idea that they could go into Iran and have a war every six months or at will — this idea of 'mowing the lawn.'"
- Pain threshold framing: "Their calculation is that this is about who has a higher threshold of pain. They think the United States and Israel can dash a lot faster, but they're not really long-distance runners."
- Iran's war aims (per Nasr): sanctions lifted, Israel withdrawal from Lebanon, US bases out of the region. "They are saying very openly that they want the United States to abandon its bases in the region."
- Gulf strategy: Iran is "following a very clever strategy of convincing Gulf states that the US bases are not really there to protect them; they're there to wage war against Iran and invite war on the Gulf. They're hoping that when this war ends, it will problematize the US presence in the region."
- "Final stand" framing: "Either resistance collapses, or resistance manages to change the rules of the game — that's what they're betting on."

### On Mojtaba Khamenei

- Earned legitimacy through suffering: family killed in strikes gives him "a particular charisma. He replicates Shia saints or mythical Iranian heroes."
- Tight IRGC relationship: "very intimately involved in the whole process of shaping the Revolutionary Guard over the past 25 years"
- No learning curve: sat next to his father for three decades
- New IRGC generation: younger commanders "who fought against the US and ISIS in Syria and Iraq" have come to the fore — different from Iran-Iraq War generation
- Dynasty doesn't disqualify: "Neither had his father" the expected religious credentials. The office has been metamorphosing.

### On rally-around-flag dynamics

- "There is still a huge amount of anger and dislike of the regime. Nobody wants to live under theocracy or economic isolation."
- But the war has created a new political axis: **"Are you for war or are you against war?" has replaced "Are you for the regime or against the regime?"**
- "Increasingly, anti-regime Iranians are saying: At this particular moment, it's not the time to have our internal fight over politics, but to support the country."
- Global South dimension: "I was in India recently and it's very clear that outside the United States and the West, they're enjoying huge amounts of support for standing up to Donald Trump."
- If Trump was "looking for a quick political uprising in Iran, it won't happen until the dust of this war settles."

### On nuclear weapons

- "The door is open." Khamenei's anti-nuclear fatwa "is no longer valid" — in Shia Islam, followers don't follow dead ayatollahs.
- "It would be up to Mojtaba and clerics in Qom to decide whether they will renew that fatwa or not."
- National security logic now favors weaponization: Iran's strategy of an open, IAEA-monitored civilian program "was a mistake. They should have followed in the India-Pakistan model. They should have gone for a bomb, rather than a program."
- Future: "either give up the program altogether — which I don't think is likely — or the program would essentially become much more military and secret."

### On maximum pressure radicalization

- From *Iran's Grand Strategy* (2025): "The impact of maximum pressure on Iranian society and the economy cannot be overstated" — 14% contraction in per capita income (2018-2022), 11% rise in poverty.
- JCPOA counterfactual: President Rouhani estimated that if the deal had lasted 10 years, Iran's middle class would have grown 35%. Instead, "20% of Iran's middle class within the first two years fell under the poverty belt."
- Nasr's thesis: "A poorer society under economic pressure becomes more authoritarian and more radical." Maximum pressure didn't moderate Iran — it hardened it.
- "The more hardline, hard-headed leadership rose to the helm."

### On Iran's "global guerrilla strategy"

- "They cannot fight this in a conventional way. Nobody will help them."
- Strategy: "You build militias in Lebanon, Iraq and Syria in order to defend yourself farther away from your borders. You help Russia at its moment of need because you need a Russian veto at the UN. You try to needle the Arab countries in order to raise the cost of supporting the US."
- Acknowledges this "has often backfired. It won them the enmity of the Arab world."
- Current framing: "Now, this is the final battle — the final stand."

### On Reza Pahlavi

- "He did have a huge moment before this war" — chanted on streets during protests.
- Historical pattern: monarchy and clergy as Iran's two poles of power since the 1500s. "When the clergy are in power, and they abuse power, it's the monarchy that people see as the alternate institution."
- **Critical weakness**: "He never invested in building a ground game in Iran — an actual organization and movement. Popularity is not the same thing as having a political program."
- **Nationalism problem**: "He's allied very closely with both Israel and the United States, and this runs against the grain of Iranian nationalism. Unless he actually stands and represents Iran's desire for safety and security at this point, it will be very difficult for him to play a significant role."

### On Iranian-American fractures

- "Deep fractures within the Iranian-American community" — friendships broken, family members not speaking.
- "There are those abroad who say they're willing for Iran to be divided. To lose territory, or at any cost, for the regime to go."
- "The most difficult conversations we're having now are with our own kin."
- Parallels for Iranian-Americans to being Muslim-American after 9/11.

### Personal dimension

- Born in Tehran, family forced into exile in 1979 at age 18. "Our property was taken by the revolutionary government."
- Father: Professor Seyyed Hossein Nasr, renowned scholar of Islam, first Iranian undergraduate at MIT (1950), authored 50+ books. Fled due to government affiliation and representing a mystical interpretation of Islam at odds with revolutionary ideology.
- "For the majority of Iranians I think this is an incredibly painful moment, regardless of what they think about the Islamic Republic. The relationship of Iranians to Iran is cultural, patriotic and nationalistic. It goes above and beyond any one government."

## Analytical Framework

- Skeptical of maximalist US demands producing results
- Emphasizes gap between military capability and political outcomes
- Warns against assuming military success = strategic success
- Focuses on regime survival instincts and structural resilience
- Highlights Israel's expanding ambitions beyond Iran as structural driver of US entanglement
- **"Last war" thesis**: Iran's endgame is not just survival but permanently altering US cost calculus — raising the price of future intervention beyond what any administration would pay
- **Maximum pressure radicalization**: sanctions don't moderate — they impoverish, radicalize, and empower hardliners (the JCPOA constituency was the middle class, which maximum pressure destroyed)
- **War-vs-regime binary**: wars redirect internal dissent into nationalist solidarity, making regime change less likely the longer fighting continues
