# John Mearsheimer

## Overview

The most influential living realist in international relations. Creator of offensive realism — the theory that great powers are structurally compelled to maximize relative power, making conflict tragic but inevitable. Co-author (with [[Stephen Walt]]) of the Israel Lobby thesis, which he has applied directly to the 2026 Iran war: "the Trump administration was dragged into this war by Israel and its enormously powerful lobby in the US."

Mearsheimer is one of the most cited political scientists alive and one of the most controversial, having been attacked for his positions on Israel, Ukraine, and the limits of liberal internationalism.

## Core Framework: Offensive Realism

Developed in *The Tragedy of Great Power Politics* (2001, updated 2014). Key claims:

1. **The international system is anarchic** — no authority above states
2. **Great powers possess offensive military capability** — always able to harm each other
3. **States can never be certain of others' intentions** — today's partner is tomorrow's threat
4. **Survival is the primary goal** — states that don't survive can't pursue anything else
5. **States are rational actors** — they think strategically about how to survive

**The conclusion**: The best way to ensure survival is to be the most powerful state — ideally, a regional hegemon. Great powers are structurally compelled to maximize relative power. Conflict is not caused by evil leaders or bad regimes but by the system itself. Hence "tragedy" — rational actors pursuing survival produce devastating wars.

**Key distinction from Waltz's defensive realism**: Kenneth Waltz argued states seek *enough* power to be secure (balancing). Mearsheimer argues they seek *maximum* power (hegemony). Waltz says the system punishes aggressors; Mearsheimer says the system rewards them — until they overreach.

**On regional hegemony**: No state can achieve global hegemony (the "stopping power of water"). The US is the only regional hegemon in modern history. Its grand strategic imperative is to prevent peer competitors from achieving regional hegemony in Europe or Asia — which explains why the US fought Germany (twice), Japan, the Soviet Union, and now confronts China.

## The Israel Lobby

### The original thesis

Co-authored with [[Stephen Walt]]. First published as "The Israel Lobby" in the *London Review of Books* (March 2006, after being rejected by *The Atlantic*). Expanded into a book: *The Israel Lobby and U.S. Foreign Policy* (FSG, 2007).

**Core argument**: A loose coalition of individuals and organizations — AIPAC, neoconservative think tanks, Christian Zionists, major donors — exerts disproportionate influence on US Middle East policy, pushing it in directions that serve Israeli interests at the expense of American interests. The lobby is not a conspiracy but an interest group operating through normal democratic channels — just unusually effective.

**On Iraq**: The book argued the Israel lobby played a significant role in pushing the US toward the 2003 Iraq invasion, alongside neoconservatives who saw Saddam's removal as benefiting Israeli security.

**Backlash**: The thesis was denounced as antisemitic by many critics. Alan Dershowitz called it "one-sided" and "misleading." The ADL condemned it. Defenders argued the critique was about policy influence, not Jewish identity, and that conflating the two was itself a tactic to shut down debate.

### Applied to the 2026 Iran war

The 20th anniversary of the LRB article fell in March 2026 — during the war itself. Mearsheimer drew the parallel explicitly:

**Grayzone interview** (March 12, 2026, with Blumenthal/Mate): "There is absolutely no question that Israel and the lobby played a central role" in both the Iraq War and the Iran war. Drew direct parallels to the 2007 book's Iraq chapter.

**"Judging Freedom"** (~March 3, 2026): "The Trump administration was dragged into this war by Israel and its enormously powerful lobby in the US."

## On the 2026 Iran War

### "Big Trouble Ahead in Iran" (Substack, ~March 3, 2026)

Published in the war's first days. Core argument: the US was pulled into a war it didn't need by Israeli influence and the lobby. Warned the war would be far more difficult than the administration anticipated.

### "Bound to Lose" (Substack, ~March 11, 2026)

"The United States has already lost the war against Iran, and there is no credible path to victory." "Nobody can tell a plausible story about how this war ends." Argued the war was unwinnable through airpower alone, and that the political objectives (regime change) were unachievable without a ground invasion that was politically impossible.

### "The Unfolding Disaster in the Gulf" (Substack)

Warned Iran can inflict "massive damage" on Gulf states. Cited one strike on Riyadh's desalination plant as capable of eliminating 90% of the city's water supply. Argued the war's costs to US allies exceeded any conceivable benefit.

### Chris Hedges Report

"Why America is Losing the War With Iran" — interview extending the "Bound to Lose" argument.

## On Ukraine

Mearsheimer's most publicly visible — and most controversial — position since 2014.

**Core argument**: NATO expansion caused the Ukraine crisis. The West's decision to push NATO eastward and integrate Ukraine into Western institutions constituted an existential threat to Russia, which responded predictably. The February 2022 invasion was a consequence of Western policy choices, not Putin's aggression alone.

**"Why is Ukraine the West's Fault?"** (2015 lecture at the University of Chicago): 30M+ YouTube views. Laid out the case before the 2022 invasion.

**Post-2022**: Maintained his position after the invasion, arguing the war confirmed his predictions and that continued Western support would prolong the conflict without changing the outcome. This made him a pariah in much of the Western foreign policy establishment and simultaneously one of the most-sought voices in non-Western media.

**The controversy**: Critics argue he provides intellectual cover for Russian aggression and denies Ukrainian agency. Defenders argue he is applying the same realist logic consistently — the US would react the same way if Mexico joined a hostile military alliance.

## On China

Offensive realism predicts that a rising China will seek regional hegemony in Asia, and the US will attempt to prevent it — making US-China competition the defining geopolitical contest of the 21st century.

Mearsheimer has argued the US should focus its strategic resources on containing China rather than dissipating them in the Middle East or Eastern Europe. The 2026 Iran war, in his view, represents exactly the kind of strategic distraction that weakens the US position vis-a-vis its only peer competitor.
