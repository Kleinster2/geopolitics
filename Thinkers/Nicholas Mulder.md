# Nicholas Mulder

## Background

Assistant professor of history at Cornell University. Author of *The Economic Weapon: The Rise of Sanctions as a Tool of Modern War* (2022), a history of how sanctions emerged as a central instrument of international politics during the interwar period (1919-1945).

Specializes in the history of economic coercion, sanctions policy, and the intersection of economic and military statecraft. One of the leading academic voices on how sanctions function — and fail — as instruments of foreign policy.

## Key Arguments

### End of US Sanctions Monopoly (2026)

Mulder argues (FT, March 17, 2026) that the post-Cold War era of US dominance in economic warfare is over. Two developments mark the shift:

1. **China's rare earth retaliation** — Beijing responded to US export controls with its own controls on refined rare earth exports, striking US defense, aerospace, and automobile industries. The disruption contributed to the US-China de-escalation culminating in the Busan meeting and January 2026 trade truce.

2. **Iran's Hormuz closure** — Iran "replicated a tactic that America has long practised in its use of sanctions: it has turned a key chokepoint in the world economy into a weapon to compel its adversary to de-escalate." The blockade cut ~20% of global oil and gas flows and one-third of global fertilizer trade.

### Cascading Sanctions Effects

The Iran energy shock forced the US to temporarily relent on Russian oil sanctions — "warding off the energy weapon used by Tehran has thus necessitated a dialling down of the economic war on Moscow." The EU, which had been pursuing decoupling from Russian gas, may need to continue purchasing Russian energy (Mulder, FT, March 17, 2026).

### Diminishing Returns

Sustained economic coercion prompts targeted states to diversify and adapt:
- Russia reoriented trade to Asian economies after 2022
- Chinese companies shifted production abroad and accelerated domestic chip innovation
- "In today's sanctions-clogged world economy, more pressure can mean diminishing returns"

### Sanctions as Pathway to War

Mulder challenges the traditional argument that sanctions are a preferable alternative to military action: "Instead of averting military action, sanctions are now just as frequently paving the way to violent escalation. A world of persistent economic wars will, sooner or later, spiral into actual warfare."

This directly applies to the [[2026 Iran conflict]], where the US moved from "maximum pressure" sanctions to open war.
