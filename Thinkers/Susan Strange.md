# Susan Strange

British scholar (1923–1998) regarded as the founder of **International Political Economy** (IPE) as a field in Britain. A former journalist (*The Economist*, *The Observer*) turned academic at the LSE and Warwick, she insisted that politics and economics could not be studied separately and that the study of power had to include markets, firms, and non-state actors — not just states.

## Core framework — structural power

Strange's most influential contribution, developed in *States and Markets* (1988), is the distinction between **relational power** (A getting B to do what B otherwise would not) and **structural power** (the power to shape the frameworks, rules, and structures within which states, firms, and people operate). Structural power is more durable and often less visible — it sets the terms others must accept.

She identified four structural powers:

| Structure | Control over |
|-----------|--------------|
| Security | Protection from violence |
| Production | What is produced, by whom, and how |
| Finance | Credit creation and the terms of money |
| Knowledge | What is known, believed, and how it is communicated |

The **knowledge structure** is where standard-setting lives: whoever controls how information is encoded, who sets the technical and informational rules, exercises a quiet, system-shaping form of power. This is the framework the [[Technical Standards]] note draws on — setting a standard is an exercise of structural power, not just commercial advantage.

## Other work

- *Casino Capitalism* (1986) and *Mad Money* (1998) — warnings about the destabilizing growth of speculative global finance.
- *The Retreat of the State* (1996) — her "diffusion of power" thesis: authority is leaking from states to markets, firms, and other non-state actors (she coined "Westfailure" for the failures of the Westphalian state system).
