# Robert Kaplan

## Overview

American author and foreign policy thinker known for emphasizing geography's role in shaping international politics. Prolific writer whose work spans conflict zones, strategic geography, and the future of American power. Combines reportage with geopolitical analysis.

## Key Facts
- **Born**: 1952, New York
- **Affiliations**: Center for New American Security (Senior Fellow), The Atlantic (correspondent)
- **Education**: University of Connecticut
- **Background**: Foreign correspondent, extensive travel in conflict zones

## Major Works

### Books
- *The Revenge of Geography* (2012) - Core work arguing geography shapes history and strategy
- *Monsoon: The Indian Ocean and the Future of American Power* (2010) - Indian Ocean as 21st century strategic center
- *The Coming Anarchy* (1994/2000) - Environmental scarcity, tribalism, and state breakdown
- *Balkan Ghosts* (1993) - Reportedly influenced Clinton's Bosnia hesitation
- *Asia's Cauldron* (2014) - South China Sea tensions
- *Adriatic* (2022) - Mediterranean history and geopolitics
- *Earning the Rockies* (2017) - American geography and national character

### Key Themes
- Geographic determinism tempered by human agency
- Tragedy and limits in foreign policy
- Regional deep dives (Balkans, Middle East, Indian Ocean, South China Sea)
- Decline of state authority
- Environmental factors in conflict

## Analytical Framework

### The Revenge of Geography

Core argument: Geography, temporarily eclipsed by technology and ideology, is reasserting itself in international politics.

Key points:
- Physical environment constrains and channels political possibilities
- Mountains, rivers, coastlines create recurring strategic patterns
- Maps reveal what elites often miss
- Technology changes geography's meaning but doesn't eliminate it

### On China

Kaplan emphasizes China's **continental-maritime duality**:
- Vast land borders creating security anxieties
- Long coastline enabling Pacific ambitions
- South China Sea as "Asia's Mediterranean" - whoever dominates it controls regional trade
- China's geography both enables expansion and invites encirclement

This analysis parallels [[Hal Brands]]' "amphibious geography" argument (February 2026).

## Influence

### Policy Impact
- Read by policymakers and military planners
- *Balkan Ghosts* allegedly reinforced Clinton administration's intervention hesitancy
- Advises military and intelligence communities
- Accessible style reaches beyond academia

### Criticism
- Accused of geographic determinism
- Some academics find analysis impressionistic
- *Balkan Ghosts* criticized for fatalism about ethnic conflict
- Environmental determinism concerns
