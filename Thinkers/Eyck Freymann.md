# Eyck Freymann

## Overview

Fellow at the [[Hoover Institution]] at Stanford University. Author of the forthcoming book *Defending Taiwan: A Strategy to Prevent War with China*. Focuses on Taiwan defense strategy, Chinese coercion, and the intersection of economic warfare and military planning in the Indo-Pacific.

## Key Arguments

### Hormuz-Taiwan Template (April 2026)

Freymann argues that Iran's [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure provides a directly replicable playbook for [[China]] against [[Taiwan]] — and that the Taiwan scenario would be far more damaging.

**The mechanism — coercion without combat:**
- China would not need to sink merchant ships or invade Taiwan to achieve strategic objectives
- A unilateral legal declaration claiming the right to control who and what enters/exits Taiwan, combined with missile demonstrations and "exclusion zones," would be sufficient
- The standard [[Five Powers Clause]] in commercial war-risk insurance automatically terminates coverage in any conflict between the US and China
- Private carriers and insurers would refuse Taiwan-bound traffic — the same dynamic that shut Hormuz without Iran sinking a single tanker

**Why Taiwan would be worse than Hormuz:**
- [[TSMC]] produces over 90% of the world's most advanced chips — no strategic reserve exists (unlike oil SPRs)
- TSMC's Arizona fab cannot substitute for lost Taiwan production
- Taiwan, [[Japan]], and [[South Korea]] could face energy shortages with no available delivery infrastructure
- A semiconductor supply shock would compound the energy shock, triggering cascading financial contagion — particularly in US tech equities
- Taiwan might curtail chip production deliberately to pressure the world into resupplying it

**The stockpiling asymmetry:**
- China is building vast reserves of oil, chips, grain, and commodities — a "shadow fortress economy" designed to outlast democratic coalitions in a crisis of attrition
- The US and allies lack equivalent joint stockpiling arrangements, pre-positioned crisis logistics, or standing economic crisis management frameworks
- Beijing's strategy, like Tehran's, depends on persuading the US that an authoritarian regime with substantial stockpiles can outlast a coalition of democracies with smaller ones

**Policy prescription:**
- The first priority in any Taiwan crisis — before punishing China or decoupling supply chains — must be managing global economic fallout
- The US and allies need joint stockpiling, pre-positioned logistics, and standing crisis management frameworks — stress-tested before a crisis, not improvised during one
- "The Hormuz emergency has shown what improvisation looks like. A Taiwan crisis would not be so forgiving."
