# Executive Order 13959

The November 12, 2020 Trump executive order barring US persons from transacting in the publicly traded securities of designated "Communist Chinese Military Companies" (CCMC). The first US measure to weaponize **capital-markets access** — rather than trade, tariffs, or technology — as an instrument of China pressure. Its most visible casualties were the three Chinese telecom ADRs delisted from the NYSE in early 2021. Continued and expanded by the Biden administration via [[#Executive Order 14032|EO 14032]], making it a rare bipartisan-sustained decoupling tool.

## What it did

EO 13959 prohibited US persons from purchasing or selling publicly traded securities (and derivatives thereof) of companies the US government designated as owned or controlled by, or affiliated with, the Chinese military. The mechanism was novel: not an export ban, not a tariff, but a prohibition on **investment** — cutting designated Chinese firms off from US capital pools (index funds, pension funds, retail brokerages).

- Designation authority initially sat with the **Department of Defense**, which maintained the Non-SDN Communist Chinese Military Companies (NS-CCMC) List under a long-dormant 1999 NDAA provision.
- US investors were given wind-down periods to divest holdings in listed firms.

## The telecom delistings (Jan–May 2021)

The order's signature effect was the forced removal of China's three big state telecoms from the New York Stock Exchange:

| Company | ADR | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| [[China Mobile]] | CHL | Delisted Jan 2021 |
| China Unicom | CHU | Delisted Jan–May 2021 |
| China Telecom | CHA | Delisted 2021 |

The NYSE reversed its delisting decision twice in early January 2021 before proceeding — a vivid illustration of exchanges caught between regulatory compulsion and commercial interest. The National Securities Clearing Corporation withdrew clearing services as of January 11, 2021; without clearing, trading could not occur. The three telecoms now trade only in Hong Kong and on mainland exchanges. (confirmed)

## Executive Order 14032 (Biden, June 3, 2021)

Rather than reverse the Trump order, the Biden administration **expanded** it. EO 14032 amended and recast EO 13959:

- Created the **Non-SDN Chinese Military-Industrial Complex Companies (NS-CMIC) List**, replacing the NS-CCMC List.
- Shifted designation authority from the **Department of Defense to the Treasury Department** (OFAC) — a more flexible sanctions apparatus.
- Broadened the criteria to include companies operating in the **surveillance-technology** sector, not just the defense-industrial base.
- Listed 59 entities, with prohibitions effective August 2, 2021.

Notably, [[Xiaomi]] was **removed** from the restrictions after it successfully challenged its designation in US federal court (the court found the DoD designation arbitrary) — a reminder that even capital-markets sanctions face due-process limits. Luokung and Gowin Semiconductor were also dropped.

## Why it matters

EO 13959 is a landmark in the [[Weaponized Interdependence]] toolkit: the United States demonstrated it could use its position at the center of global capital markets — the depth and liquidity that make US listings valuable — as a coercive chokepoint, the financial analogue to [[Semiconductors|semiconductor export controls]]. Three observations:

1. **Capital-markets decoupling is now a live instrument.** Before 2020, US-China decoupling ran through trade and technology. EO 13959 opened the financial channel, and its bipartisan continuation (Trump → Biden) signaled durability rather than a one-administration aberration.
2. **It accelerated listing repatriation.** The delistings, combined with the audit-driven [[#Relationship to HFCAA|HFCAA]] threat, pushed Chinese champions toward Hong Kong and mainland home listings. [[China Telecom]] returned to Shanghai (Aug 2021) and [[China Mobile]] followed (Jan 2022) — multi-billion-dollar A-share IPOs that brought the telecoms' primary listings home.
3. **The chokepoint cuts both ways.** Excluding large Chinese firms from US indices removes them from American portfolios but also cedes a measure of US financial-market centrality — part of the broader fragmentation [[Digital Sovereignty]] and [[Splinternet]] describe in the information domain.

## Relationship to HFCAA

EO 13959 is often conflated with the **Holding Foreign Companies Accountable Act** (HFCAA, December 2020), but they are distinct decoupling vectors:

- **EO 13959** — *ownership/military-link* based. Bars investment in designated firms regardless of their financial disclosures.
- **HFCAA** — *audit-transparency* based. Threatens delisting of any foreign issuer whose auditor the PCAOB cannot inspect for three consecutive years. A 2022 US-China audit agreement granted PCAOB access, easing the immediate threat — but it remains a standing lever.

Together they form a two-track capital-markets pressure system: one keyed to security ties, one to disclosure.
