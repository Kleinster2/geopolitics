# 2026 White House Correspondents' Dinner shooting

A lone gunman, [[Cole Allen]] (31, of Torrance, California), opened fire at the security checkpoint of the [[White House Correspondents' Association|White House Correspondents' Dinner]] at the Washington Hilton on the evening of April 25, 2026. President [[Donald Trump]], First Lady Melania Trump, Vice President [[JD Vance]], and Cabinet members were evacuated by the [[US Secret Service]]. Allen was charged with attempting to assassinate the president; faces potential life in prison if convicted.

---

## What happened

Just after the dinner began around 8:00 p.m. ET on Saturday April 25, 2026, Allen allegedly attempted to breach a security barrier inside the Washington Hilton near the main ballroom. Armed with a shotgun and other weapons, he exchanged gunfire with law enforcement at the security screening area. The Secret Service evacuated Trump, Melania, Vance, and Cabinet members. The dinner was disrupted; CBS White House correspondent Weijia Jiang, who had been on stage when the shooting began, gave a first-person account of the evacuation.

## The suspect

Cole Allen, 31, was identified by law enforcement sources as the suspect. Profile per public reporting (Fox News, NPR, CBS News, CNN, Wikipedia):

- Resident of Torrance, California
- Working as a tutor at the time of the attack
- Mechanical engineering degree, [[California Institute of Technology|Caltech]], 2017
- Master's in computer science, California State University, Dominguez Hills, 2025
- FBI secured his California home Apr 26 as part of the investigation

## Charges

DOJ unveiled charges April 27. Includes attempting to assassinate the president; the White House stated the suspect "wanted to target Trump officials." Federal explosives / weapons charges layered on top. Potential life sentence if convicted.

## Pattern with prior Trump-administration / AI-tech political violence

The April 25 incident follows a sequence of high-profile physical-violence attacks in 2026:

- **April 10, 2026** — Daniel Moreno-Gama (20, of Texas) charged with attempted murder, attempted arson, and federal explosives charges after a Molotov cocktail attack targeting [[Sam Altman]]; suspect carried a document with anti-AI views and a target list of AI executives and investors (per NPR, CNN, ABC News, CNBC, Fox News, SF Standard, Al Jazeera coverage).
- **April 12, 2026** — gunfire incident near Sam Altman.
- **April 25, 2026** — Cole Allen attack at WHCD.

Whether the WHCD attack connects to the anti-AI / anti-tech-establishment violence track or to a distinct anti-Trump motive is, at time of writing, unresolved. The DOJ filing on April 27 will determine whether the framing is general political violence or a specific threat type.

## Apr 28, 2026 — Federal court appearance, manifesto details, Kimmel monologue context

Per Bloomberg "The China Show" 4/28/2026 broadcast (US/global news segment):

- **Federal court appearance.** Cole Allen made his first federal court appearance Apr 28. Charged with attempted assassination of the president and gun offenses; potential life sentence if convicted.
- **Manifesto.** Allen sent a manifesto to family members before the attack; the document was included in charging filings. The manifesto did not name Trump explicitly but appeared to refer to him. The motive characterization remains in flux pending DOJ disclosure of the full document.
- **Jimmy Kimmel monologue context (Apr 23).** Two days before the WHCD shooting, [[Jimmy Kimmel]] (ABC late-night host) staged a parody of the WHCD that referred to first lady [[Melania Trump]] as an "expectant widow" — phrasing that, in retrospect after the attack, Trump and Melania characterized as an incitement to violence.
- **Trump's Apr 28 demand.** Trump publicly demanded that [[ABC]] and parent [[Walt Disney]] immediately fire Kimmel over the broadcast, calling it a "despicable call to violence." [[Melania Trump]] also publicly urged ABC to take a stand, calling the monologue "hateful and violent."
- **Disney / ABC posture.** As of the Apr 28 broadcast, Disney/ABC had not announced any action on Kimmel. The corporate-First-Amendment frame is the binding tension: Disney faces presidential pressure to discipline a satirical monologue that, while close to the line of taste, was scheduled before any attack and is not under any legal incitement standard. The decision becomes the canonical 2026 test of late-night-host job-security under presidential public pressure.

The Kimmel-monologue thread layers the attack onto the broader question of the relationship between political-comedy speech and political-violence outcomes. Trump's call to fire Kimmel is the political-instrumentalization side of the same incident; Allen's manifesto is the operational side. Whether the two threads connect substantively (i.e. whether the Kimmel monologue motivated or contextualized Allen's attack) versus politically (i.e. Trump using the attack to pressure ABC on a Kimmel he'd long objected to) is an open empirical question.
