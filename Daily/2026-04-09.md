## Notes Created
- [[Suez Moment]] (Concepts) — Full concept note. Defines the term as a diagnostic: an event in which a great power's pretensions collide irreversibly with its actual capacity, visibly to allies and adversaries. Covers the 1956 Suez Crisis (sequence, financial/oil/UN mechanism of British capitulation, Macmillan's "first in, first out" flip, sterling run of ~$45M, what it revealed), the seven-feature diagnostic framework (overreach / ally veto / adversary triumph / exposed dependency / retreat under duress / legitimacy collapse / successor visible), and contemporary candidates (Iraq 2003, GFC 2008, Afghanistan 2021, Ukraine, 2026 Iran war). Adds Acheson's Dec 5, 1962 "lost an empire" quote as canonical epitaph. Argues the 2026 Iran war is the most structurally Suez-like candidate to date for the US; documents multiple direct invocations of "Suez moment" by analysts this cycle — [[Fawaz Gerges]] (LSE) to MEE: "I think we are witnessing a Suez Crisis moment for the US"; MEE opinion; Modern Diplomacy (Mar 30); Seoul Economic Daily (Mar 29); Breitbart Business Digest (Apr 8, contrarian). Notes [[Seth Cropsey]]'s WSJ (Mar 22–23) inverse framing. The Feigenbaum observation — Trump "begging Beijing for an expeditionary naval deployment" — framed as an explicit inversion of the 1956 Eisenhower-to-Eden relationship. Flags disanalogies (reserve-currency issuer, deeper alliance system, nuclear calculus, no ready successor) and the "no successor" problem pointing to [[G-Zero]] rather than clean handoff. Clarifies that Paul Kennedy popularized "imperial overstretch," not "Suez moment" per se.
