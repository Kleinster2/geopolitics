# Japan

## Overview
World's third-largest economy and key US ally in Asia. Historically pacifist post-WWII, now undergoing significant remilitarization in response to China's rise and regional threats.

## Key Facts
- **Capital**: Tokyo
- **Population**: ~125 million (declining)
- **GDP**: ~$4.2 trillion
- **Military Budget**: ~$50 billion (increasing to 2% GDP)
- **Emperor**: Naruhito (ceremonial)
- **Prime Minister**: Sanae Takaichi (2025-)

## Geopolitical Position

### Strategic Shift
Post-WWII pacifism giving way to "normal" military posture:
- 2022 National Security Strategy: historic shift
- Doubling defense budget (1% to 2% GDP)
- Counter-strike capabilities
- Acquiring Tomahawk missiles

### US Alliance
- Cornerstone of regional security
- US bases in Japan (~54,000 troops)
- Nuclear umbrella
- Integrated operations

## Security Concerns
1. **China**: Senkaku Islands, Taiwan contingency, military modernization
2. **North Korea**: Missiles over/near Japan, nuclear threat
3. **Russia**: Northern Territories dispute
4. **Taiwan**: Critical sea lanes, potential conflict zone

## Fiscal-financial state-capacity constraint (May 2026)

Japan's debt/yield/yen pressure is primarily an investing-vault topic, but it has a geopolitical read-through because fiscal capacity is strategic capacity. Sanae Takaichi is trying to expand defense, harden supply chains, fund energy security, and manage China pressure while the JGB market is demanding a higher term premium and the yen is weakening in real effective terms.

Robin J. Brooks' May 28 2026 debt-trap frame is that ongoing BOJ purchases keep long-end yields below full market-clearing levels, while the missing risk premium leaks into the yen. IMF's 2026 Japan Article IV complicates Brooks' proposed asset-sale escape route: Japan has large public-sector financial assets, but many are encumbered, operationally independent, or held by social security funds for future benefits. The geopolitical implication is that Tokyo's balance-sheet problem cannot be separated from alliance policy. A government that needs to stabilize the currency and bond market has less room for open-ended defense-industrial commitments.

## China Pressure: Taiwan and Rare Earths (May 2026)

Japan's Taiwan language under Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi has become a pressure point in China-Japan relations. Jiji/Nippon reported that Xi Jinping criticized Takaichi's Taiwan remarks in a call with Donald Trump, while Reuters-linked customs analysis showed China had restricted shipments of several heavy rare earths and gallium to Japan for roughly four months. The combination matters because it fuses diplomatic pressure with an industrial chokepoint.

The strategic echo is 2010: China can use rare-earth access to remind Japan that its defense and manufacturing base depends on Chinese-controlled processing. The difference in 2026 is that Japan has more allied diversification channels, but the near-term leverage still sits with Beijing.

Sources: Jiji/Nippon.com, May 25 2026; Reuters-linked rare-earth customs analysis via OE Digital / Energy News, May 22 2026.
