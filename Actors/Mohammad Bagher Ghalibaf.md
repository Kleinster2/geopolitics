# Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf

## Overview

Speaker of the Iranian parliament ([[Majles]]) since 2020 and a former senior commander in the [[IRGC]]. After the killing of [[Ali Larijani]] (national security adviser) in an Israeli strike on March 18, 2026, Ghalibaf became the dominant civilian war leader of the Iranian regime and — following Trump's April 7 ceasefire announcement — the senior Iranian interlocutor on the direct diplomatic track. Described by [[Ali Vaez]] and others as closer to the IRGC hardline than Larijani; [[Ian Bremmer]] assessed him as "not as capable" as Larijani, though he "speaks with authority."

## Key Facts

- **Born**: August 23, 1961, Torqabeh, Iran (age 64 as of April 2026)
- **Position**: Speaker of the Islamic Consultative Assembly ([[Majles]]) since May 28, 2020
- **Prior positions**: Mayor of Tehran (2005-2017, three terms); Chief of the Law Enforcement Force (Iran's national police), 2000-2005; IRGC Air Force Commander, 1997-2000; former commander of IRGC forces during the Iran-Iraq War
- **Presidential runs**: Three unsuccessful attempts (2005, 2013, 2017) — finished second to Hassan Rouhani in 2013
- **Faction**: Principlist (conservative); close to IRGC senior command
- **Military background**: Former IRGC Air Force commander; fought in the Iran-Iraq War

## Role in the 2026 Iran conflict

### Initial wartime role (March 8-18)

Following the March 8 elevation of [[Mojtaba Khamenei]] as Supreme Leader, [[Ali Vaez]] (International Crisis Group) identified Ghalibaf and [[Ali Larijani]] as the two figures actually running the Iranian state:
- **Ali Larijani** — national security adviser; day-to-day state affairs and strategic direction (pragmatist)
- **Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf** — parliament speaker; war effort (closer to IRGC hardline)

Both are former IRGC commanders; Mojtaba Khamenei was not operationally present.

### Ceasefire terms (March 18)

After Larijani's assassination by Israel on March 18, 2026, Ghalibaf became the senior civilian war leader. He laid out Iran's demand the same week:

> "There must be no threat of war anymore [before Iran stops]. This is our clear and definite demand. We don't accept a ceasefire and then go back to war in five months' time." (Iranian state TV, confirmed, FT)

### March 23 — denial of Trump talks

When Trump claimed "very good and productive conversations" with Iran on March 23 (announcing a five-day postponement of strikes on Iranian power plants), Ghalibaf categorically denied any negotiations:

> "No negotiations have been held with the US." He accused Trump of trying to "manipulate the financial and oil markets and escape the quagmire in which the US and Israel are trapped." (confirmed, Al Jazeera)

### April 7-8 — ceasefire and diplomatic track

With the April 7 Trump-announced two-week ceasefire, Ghalibaf was identified as the senior Iranian participant in the direct diplomatic track — facilitated by [[Pakistan]] and [[Oman]].

[[Ian Bremmer]] assessment (April 8, 2026):
> "My understanding is that Ghalibaf, the speaker of the parliament, is going to participate and he's not as capable as Larijani was, but he certainly has authority. He speaks with authority and that's useful. It was less useful when the Israelis assassinated Larijani."

## Context: "less capable" than Larijani

Larijani's elimination is widely seen as removing "the regime's most prominent pragmatic interlocutor" (FT, March 19). A regional diplomat told the FT that Larijani "would have been integral to any diplomatic process — his death is expected to further set back talks."

Ghalibaf's profile:
- Stronger ties to [[IRGC]] senior command (he was one of them)
- Longer hardline track record than Larijani
- Political ambition (three presidential runs)
- Parliament speaker gives him authority to bind the Iranian legislature to any deal
- Does not carry Larijani's reputation as a cross-factional bridge-builder

## Pre-war political profile

- Long-running member of Iran's conservative establishment
- Reformist critics accused his Tehran mayoralty of corruption
- Generally supported the [[JCPOA]] diplomatic track in his Majles role (2020-present)
- Viewed as more hawkish than Larijani but more administratively capable than the more ideological wings of the Principlist faction
