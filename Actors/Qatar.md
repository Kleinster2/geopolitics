# Qatar

## Overview
World's largest LNG exporter and per-capita richest nation. Under Emir Tamim bin Hamad Al Thani, Qatar punches far above its weight through gas wealth, diplomatic mediation, and the $524 billion QIA sovereign fund. Despite hosting ~10,000 US troops at Al Udeid Air Base, Qatar maintains working relations with Iran and has emerged as the Gulf's key diplomatic broker.

## Key Facts
- **Capital**: Doha
- **Population**: ~3.1 million (91% expatriate)
- **GDP**: ~$222 billion (2026 est.)
- **GDP per capita**: ~$80,000+ (nationals only)
- **Emir**: Tamim bin Hamad Al Thani (2013-)
- **LNG exports**: 77 mtpa (20%+ of global trade)
- **Currency**: Qatari Riyal, pegged to USD at 3.64

## Geopolitical Position

### Energy Superpower
Qatar sits on the world's largest natural gas field (North Field), shared with Iran's South Pars. QatarEnergy operates the North Field expansion—an $80+ billion project to nearly double LNG capacity to 142 mtpa by 2030. The March 2026 Iranian missile strike on Ras Laffan terminal has raised fundamental questions about Qatar's energy security and forced majeure on LNG shipments (reported).

### Military and Security
**Al Udeid Air Base** hosts ~10,000 US troops and serves as forward headquarters for US Central Command (CENTCOM). Iranian strikes on Al Udeid in June 2025 and March 2026 have challenged the premise that the base protects Qatar versus making it a target. Qatar operates under US security umbrella while maintaining independent foreign policy.

### Diplomatic Mediation
Qatar's core strategic asset is its role as mediator between adversaries:
- **Hamas-Israel negotiations**: Brokered Gaza ceasefire (January 2025) and second peace agreement (October 2025)
- **Taliban talks**: Hosted negotiations leading to 2021 US withdrawal from Afghanistan
- **Regional conflicts**: Emir Tamim was first head of state to visit post-Assad Damascus (January 2025)

### Iran Triangulation
Qatar shares the North Field/South Pars gas reservoir with Iran—the source of its wealth and its strategic vulnerability. Maintains diplomatic relations with Tehran while hosting US military presence. This balancing act defines Qatar's foreign policy challenge and was severely tested during the 2026 Iran conflict when Iranian missiles struck Qatari energy infrastructure.

### Turkey Alliance
Strong partnership with Turkey, including Turkish military base in Qatar (~3,000 personnel as of 2026). Turkey provided crucial support during the 2017 GCC blockade, including food supplies and diplomatic backing. This alliance has survived regional realignments and represents Qatar's hedge against over-dependence on US protection.

## Major Crises and Responses

### 2017 GCC Blockade
Saudi Arabia, UAE, Bahrain, and Egypt severed diplomatic ties and imposed land, sea, and air blockade (June 5, 2017 - January 5, 2021). The quartet demanded Qatar meet 13 conditions including:
- Shut down Al Jazeera network
- Close Turkish military base
- Cut ties with Iran and Muslim Brotherhood
- Pay compensation for alleged terrorism support

**Qatar's response**: Refused all 13 demands. Resolved at Al-Ula summit (January 5, 2021) with zero concessions required from Qatar. The blockade ultimately strengthened Qatar's independence and diversified its relationships.

### Abraham Accords Position
Qatar did NOT join the Abraham Accords normalizing relations with Israel, unlike Bahrain and UAE. This reflects Qatar's different strategic calculus—maintaining its mediator role requires relationships with all parties, including Hamas leadership based in Doha.

### 2026 Iran Conflict Impact
Iran struck Qatar directly during Operation Epic Fury retaliation, targeting:
- **Al Udeid Air Base** (June 2025 and March 2026): Iranian ballistic missiles hit US CENTCOM headquarters
- **Ras Laffan LNG terminal** (March 18, 2026): Physical damage to liquefaction trains and force majeure declaration
- **Economic impact**: QatarEnergy CEO estimates ~17% of LNG capacity offline for 3-5 years, $20 billion annual revenue loss

Qatar expelled Iranian military attachés and declared Iran's actions a "direct threat to national security"—the strongest language from Doha since the war began. The crisis revealed the limits of Qatar's neutrality when its energy infrastructure becomes a battlefield.

## Economic Foundations

### Sovereign Wealth
**Qatar Investment Authority (QIA)** manages ~$524 billion in assets (2026 est.), making it one of the world's largest sovereign wealth funds. QIA serves as diplomatic tool through strategic investments in global tech (Anthropic, xAI), infrastructure, and sports properties.

### Soft Power Assets
- **Al Jazeera**: Arabic and English news networks, diplomatic influence tool
- **Sports**: Hosted FIFA World Cup 2022, owns Paris Saint-Germain via QIA subsidiary
- **Cultural diplomacy**: Qatar Museums, Education City partnerships with US universities

## Current Strategic Issues

### Post-War Regional Realignment
The 2026 Iran conflict has frozen pre-war GCC dynamics where Saudi Arabia was building a new alliance (Turkey, Qatar, Egypt, Pakistan) against the UAE-Israel axis ([[Marc Lynch]], *Foreign Policy*). Iran's attacks on all Gulf states temporarily reunified the GCC under existential threat, but underlying tensions remain unresolved.

### Energy Diversification Challenge
Despite massive gas reserves, Qatar faces the same hydrocarbon dependency as other Gulf states. Vision 2030 focuses on:
- Financial services hub
- Technology and innovation
- Sports and entertainment
- Sustainable development

### Security Dilemma
Al Udeid's targeting by Iran exposed Qatar's security paradox: the US base that supposedly provides protection has made Qatar a direct target in regional conflicts. This challenges the fundamental assumption of the US-Qatar security relationship.
