# US-Israel Relations

## Overview
Often described as America's most important Middle East relationship. Characterized by deep military, intelligence, and political cooperation. Under Trump 2.0 (2025-), relations have become even closer with unprecedented alignment on Iran policy.

## Foundation

### Strategic Value
- Democratic ally in volatile region
- Intelligence sharing (extensive)
- Military technology cooperation
- Counter-terrorism partnership
- Iran containment partner

### Domestic Politics
- Strong bipartisan Congressional support (historically)
- AIPAC and pro-Israel lobby influence
- Evangelical Christian support
- Jewish American community
- Growing partisan divide (Democratic left)

### Military Aid
- **$3.8 billion annually** (10-year MOU through 2028)
- Qualitative Military Edge (QME) commitment
- Joint missile defense (Iron Dome, Arrow, David's Sling)
- F-35 access
- Intelligence sharing

## Trump Administration 2.0 (2025-)

### Key Characteristics
- Highly aligned with Netanyahu government
- "Instrumental" rather than "emotional" approach
- Maximum pressure on Iran restored
- Abraham Accords expansion priority
- Less restraint on Israeli military action

### Iran Policy Coordination
- April 2025: Trump initiates direct talks with Iran
- 60-day deadline for nuclear deal
- Negotiations fail
- June 2025: Joint US-Israel strikes on Iran
- US directly attacks Iranian nuclear facilities
- First direct US military action against Iran's nuclear program

### 2026 Approach
- Trump encouraging Iranian protesters
- Threatening military intervention
- Giving Israel space on Iran decisions
- Focused on regime weakness opportunity

## Historical Context

### Pre-1967
- US initially cautious about Israel
- France was primary arms supplier
- Relationship grew during Cold War

### 1967 Transformation
- Six-Day War victory
- Israel as strategic asset vs. Soviet clients
- Arms relationship begins

### Key Milestones
| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1973 | US airlift during Yom Kippur War |
| 1979 | Camp David Accords (Carter) |
| 1981 | Strategic Cooperation Agreement |
| 1998 | Wye River Memorandum |
| 2016 | $38B MOU signed (Obama) |
| 2017-20 | Trump 1.0: Jerusalem, Golan, Abraham Accords |
| 2025 | Joint strikes on Iran |

## Points of Tension (Historical)

### Settlement Policy
- US officially opposes expansion (most administrations)
- Trump 1.0 broke with this
- Source of international friction

### Palestinian Issue
- Two-state solution (official US position, mostly)
- Trump: Less committed to two-state framework
- Gaza conflicts create diplomatic challenges

### Iran Approach
- Obama: JCPOA engagement
- Trump: Maximum pressure
- Biden: Attempted JCPOA revival
- Trump 2.0: Confrontation

### Arms Sales
- US balances Israel QME with Gulf sales
- Congressional oversight
- Generally resolved in Israel's favor

## Intelligence Relationship

### Depth
- Mossad-CIA cooperation
- NSA-Unit 8200 partnership
- Counter-terrorism sharing
- Iran intelligence coordination

### Occasional Friction
- Jonathan Pollard case (1985)
- Concerns about Israeli operations on US soil
- Generally very close

## Current Dynamics (2025-2026)

### Alignment on Iran
- Shared goal: Prevent nuclear Iran
- Joint military operations (June 2025)
- Coordinated pressure campaign
- Both watching protest developments

### Abraham Accords Expansion
- Trump sees as signature achievement
- Saudi normalization still pursued
- Israel-Gulf alignment deepening
- Iran weakness creates opportunity

### Gaza/Palestinian Issue
- Less US focus under Trump
- Netanyahu has more freedom of action
- International criticism continues
- Two-state solution dormant

## Competing Views

### "Special Relationship" Model
- Israel as uniquely valuable ally
- Shared democratic values
- Strategic and moral commitment
- Bipartisan consensus

### "Reassessment" View
- Relationship should be more conditional
- Settlement policy criticism
- Palestinian rights concerns
- Questioning unconditional support
- Growing on Democratic left

### Trump Approach
- Transactional, not sentimental
- High alignment currently
- But could shift if interests diverge
- Less constrained by traditional frameworks
