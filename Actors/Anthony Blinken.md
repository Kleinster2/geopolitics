# Anthony Blinken

## Overview

Antony ("Tony") Blinken served as US Secretary of State under President [[Joe Biden]] (January 2021 – January 2025). He previously served as Deputy Secretary of State under President [[Barack Obama]] from 2015-2017, during which time he was directly involved in the negotiation of the [[JCPOA]] (Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action — the Iran nuclear deal) and in Obama's broader Iran policy. His earlier career included Deputy National Security Advisor (2013-2015) and Staff Director of the Senate Foreign Relations Committee under then-Senator Biden.

## Key Facts

- **Born**: April 16, 1962
- **Education**: Harvard (BA), Columbia Law (JD)
- **Key positions**: Secretary of State (2021-2025), Deputy Secretary of State (2015-2017), Deputy NSA (2013-2015)
- **Family**: Stepfather [[Samuel Pisar]] — Holocaust survivor; shaped his worldview on US moral leadership
- **Relationship to Biden**: Three decades of collaboration; Biden's most trusted foreign policy adviser

## Analytical lens

Blinken is, by background and habit, a **process diplomat** — methodical, alliance-focused, allergic to freelancing by individual leaders. His analysis tends to emphasize:

- Patience and sequencing over breakthrough moments
- The guardrails (economic, military, political) that constrain what can be done
- The gap between *tactical success* and *strategic outcome*
- The cost of burning diplomatic inheritance (treaties, alliances, norms) that took years to build

## Analysis — 2026 Iran conflict

### CNN GPS Fareed Zakaria interview (April 12, 2026)

Blinken sat for an interview with [[Fareed Zakaria]] recorded Thursday April 10 and aired Sunday April 12, after Trump's morning Truth Social announcement of a US Navy Hormuz blockade and the collapse of the Islamabad talks led by [[JD Vance]] (21 hours of discussions; no lasting deal).

**On the revolutionary republic's compromise history:**

> "In the whole history of the revolutionary republic — 47 years — they've only made a fundamental compromise and come to a fundamental conclusion on something twice: ending the eight years of the war with Iraq, and then the nuclear agreement with the Obama administration, which took us two and a half years to get to."

Implication: negotiating real concessions out of Tehran is a long game by structural design. Trump's two-week ceasefire window is ahistorical.

**On Trump's off-ramp — taxonomy:**

> "President Trump has taken an off-ramp. Maybe he was tired of all that winning. The question is — is this off-ramp to a rest stop, which seems the most likely thing? Is it to some kind of destination? Or is it some kind of dead end? And the challenge he has is he's really put himself into a corner over the last few weeks that's going to be very hard to get out of."

**The two Trump guardrails — "markets and munitions":**

1. **Markets**: Trump "very sensitive" to oil, gasoline, natural gas, fertilizer, bond market, stock market.
2. **Munitions**: US offensive and defensive munitions stockpiles are "significantly depleted such that our deterrence is diminished, including with regard to China."

Both guardrails constrain the option of restarting the war.

**Tactical success, strategic failure:**

> "The military as always does a brilliant job prosecuting its target lists, doing remarkable things. But you have tactical success and strategic failure or at least strategic setback because what are we left with at the end of this?"

Blinken's inventory of what Iran retains / gains:

| Item | Status |
|------|--------|
| Regime | Still in place. "Younger albeit maybe injured Ayatollah." |
| Highly enriched uranium | Iran still holds it. |
| Centrifuges | Iran retains them; can spin up. |
| Missile capacity | Fewer but "can rebuild the production capacity." |
| Hormuz leverage | NEW — did not exist pre-war. "Break glass moment that's happened." |

**On Hormuz as the permanent strategic shift:**

> "Both Iran and the United States and others always came up to the line about the Strait of Hormuz many times in the past. There was always this question mark and we always factored in what might Iran do in the straight to make life really difficult. It never got to that. The Iranians never pulled the trigger on the straight because they thought that would invite an existential crisis and threat from the United States and others, Israel, and we always were very careful to make sure that we push things but not so far that the strait would be endangered. Now break glass moment that's happened. The net result is Iran emerges with tremendous leverage."

This aligns with [[Gideon Rachman]]'s March 16 FT thesis (see [[Strait of Hormuz#Hormuz as permanent non-nuclear deterrent]]).

**On the no-enrichment demand — the hidden compromise structure:**

Trump's formal position is zero Iranian enrichment. Blinken's experience of JCPOA and the attempted Biden revival suggests a face-saving structure is in reach:

> "My understanding is at various points over the last year and a half in the discussions that the administration was having with the Iranians, the Iranians put something like that forward. It's not clear whether the folks we had at the table fully grasped what they were getting from the Iranians. We never conceded their right to enrich, but as a practical matter, under the JCPOA, we moved out 97% of their highly enriched uranium, but they retained some capacity at a very low level. And the net was this. They could claim that they continued to be able to enrich. We could say that they didn't have a right to enrich — but as a practical matter, they were doing it. And the main thing was the uranium they were enriching was kept at such a low level in such a low quantity that had they decided to break out of the agreement it would have taken them more than a year to produce enough of the fissile material that they needed for a nuclear weapon — a weapon by the way that they didn't have and still don't have."

The structural formula: Iran keeps enrichment as political claim; US keeps non-concession as political claim; inspection / quantity / enrichment-level caps keep breakout distance >1 year.

**On why Biden did not re-enter JCPOA:**

Two blockers:

1. **Breakout window shrunk.** By 2021, Iran had run centrifuges for years and advanced them. Had the US returned to the original JCPOA, breakout time had compressed from "a year" to "six or seven months." Question: is that good enough?

2. **Iran wanted a durability guarantee Biden couldn't deliver.** "Having signed the agreement, having actually adhered to the agreement — and this is not just me saying it, it's not just the international inspectors saying it, our own intelligence concluded that — they said to us: what guarantees do we have that another administration won't come along and do exactly what President Trump did the first term and tear it up? So they wanted some kind of guarantee, maybe congressional approval of the agreement as a treaty — incredibly hard to do with our politics."

Then: midterms (2022) made continued negotiation politically unworkable. Biden administration then pivoted to a **"less for less" freeze** concept — freeze Iran's production and stockpile, freeze US sanctions (no new), as a holding pattern with a roadmap to a fuller agreement. Second term would have pursued that path.

**On Iran's negotiating character — two things to understand:**

1. **Not a monolith.** "One of the biggest mistakes one makes about Iran is to see it as especially then — maybe now a little bit different but then certainly — as a political monolith. It wasn't. There were factions, there are very different views. There are politics that were playing out in real time and making negotiations complicated."

2. **Master negotiators.** "This is a great civilization and even this heinous regime has some of the positive attributes of that civilization, which is an extraordinary track record of being able to negotiate — to be incredibly challenging to deal with." Blinken cited walk-away tactics in JCPOA endgame: US delegation (led by [[Wendy Sherman]], with [[John Kerry]]) packed bags and loaded plane — Iran said "no, no, come back."

**On Netanyahu's role:**

> "To my knowledge, he's tried to convince virtually every administration since he's been in office to do what President Trump finally did. He came to the Obama administration and tried this. He came to the Biden administration and tried this. And he finally found a willing customer in President Trump. But this was President Trump's decision. This wasn't Bibi Netanyahu's decision."

This is analytically important: Blinken *declines* the [[Trita Parsi]] frame that Netanyahu "played Trump's psychology" effectively. He places responsibility fully on Trump while noting Netanyahu's consistent 20+ year pitch.

**On how Biden handled Iranian aggression differently (April 2024, September 2024):**

Two "extraordinary moments" when Iran directly attacked Israel:

- **Defended Israel actively**: US forces on the line for the first time in such a role.
- **Rallied regional and global coalition** to do the same.
- **Cabined the Israeli response**: worked to prevent escalation to regime-threatening strikes. Rationale: "if the regime thinks you're aiming to take it down, it becomes existential. And that's when they're likely to do pretty much anything. And then it becomes a fight for life, a fight for death. Under those circumstances, the Iranians have tremendous tenacity."

Trump's 2026 war violated this principle by explicitly calling for regime overthrow on Day 1 ("cast off the yoke of tyranny") — which [[Simon Gass]] identified as the existential trigger that produced Hormuz closure.

**Blinken's advice if asked (unlikely):**

1. **Don't go back to fighting.** "Not going to be in our interest. It's going to do tremendous additional damage, tremendous additional risk and danger."
2. **Keep the pressure by other means.** Rally other countries.
3. **Engage in diplomacy for however long it takes to get a deal.** "A deal is going to involve some compromise. Decide where you're willing to compromise. Decide what you need to get."
4. **Focus the deal on Hormuz.** "The Strait of Hormuz is the most important thing because of all the ramifications that it has."
5. **Strategic patience.** "The JCPOA is probably the best example of that. It can be done but you've got to play the long game on this."

Blinken noted the irony of focusing on Hormuz: "That's a problem created by the war... it's the arsonist putting out the fire."
