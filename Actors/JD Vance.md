# JD Vance

## Overview
50th Vice President of the United States (2025-). Iraq War veteran who returned a sharp critic of foreign military interventions. Senator from Ohio (2023-2025). Author of *Hillbilly Elegy* (2016). Core figure in "America First" populist-nationalist wing of Republican Party.

## Background
- **Born**: 1984, Middletown, Ohio
- **Military**: US Marine Corps, Iraq War veteran
- **Education**: Ohio State, Yale Law School
- **Career**: Venture capital, author, Senator (Ohio 2023-2025)

## Foreign Policy Views

### Stated Position
- Iraq War critic—argues Washington policymakers lose sight of American interests in faraway wars
- Skeptical of US interventions and "forever wars"
- Aligned with restraint-oriented Republicans (Hawley, Paul wing)
- Sen. Jim Banks (R-IN): Both he and Vance "served in wars that were poorly run and managed by the military leaders of the prior administrations"

### Reported Private Reservations
Per leaked Signal chat with Trump Cabinet officials (originally about Houthi strikes):
- "I think we are making a mistake"
- "I just hate bailing Europe out again"
- "I am willing to support the consensus of the team and keep these concerns to myself"

Rebecca Lissner (former Harris deputy NSA) noted Vance was "still relitigating" after Trump had decided, rather than rallying the team.

### Analyst Assessments
Ben Freeman (Quincy Institute): "If JD Vance himself were president, would Venezuela have happened? Would we have captured Maduro? To me, without a doubt, the answer is no."

Some allies see him "struggling to thread the needle" between supporting Trump and not alienating the GOP's anti-interventionist wing.

## Venezuela Intervention (January 2026)

Despite history of intervention skepticism, Vance publicly supported [[Venezuela|Operation Absolute Resolve]]:

- Cast **tie-breaking vote** (50-50) to kill War Powers Resolution (January 14, 2026)
- Posted on X after Maduro capture: "Maduro is the newest person to find out that President Trump means what he says"
- Stated operation was justified as Maduro was wanted in US
- Reported closely involved in planning despite not attending key briefing

Pattern: Private reservations, public support for Trump's decisions.

## Iran War (2026) — internal opposition

### Haberman/Swan reporting (April 7, 2026)

[[Maggie Haberman]] and [[Jonathan Swan]] (*New York Times*) reported, based on material from their forthcoming book *Regime Change: Inside the Imperial Presidency of Donald Trump* (Simon & Schuster, June 23, 2026), that Vance was the strongest internal opponent of the Iran war:

> "Nobody in Mr. Trump's inner circle was more worried about the prospect of war with Iran, or did more to try to stop it, than the vice president."

- Vance warned Trump a war against Iran could cause "regional chaos and untold numbers of casualties" (confirmed, The Hill, Raw Story, Dnyuz, multiple sources).
- Described as the "most intense naysayer" — often going against the positions of everyone else in Trump's inner circle.
- Privately called the war a "huge distraction" from domestic priorities.
- Report notes "almost all of the president's top advisers, including Vice President JD Vance, privately had misgivings about the war."

### Framing and incentive structure

The New Republic noted the critical framing: Trump's advisers have "a strong incentive to leak their prewar misgivings since the war is going poorly." The reporting is a case study in **post-facto distancing** — the same officials who publicly supported the war are now retroactively establishing their private opposition.

The timing is notable: the excerpts published April 7 — the same day as the ceasefire announcement — allow insiders to align themselves with the war's end rather than its prosecution. [[Ian Bremmer]] (April 8) read this as a signal: "I don't see the cult of Trump persisting for people like JD Vance who are much younger and are very ambitious and want to have their own political career beyond this guy."

### Pattern deepens

This extends the Venezuela pattern:
- Venezuela: private reservations on Signal ("I think we are making a mistake"), public support (tie-breaking vote, celebratory post after Maduro capture).
- Iran: private opposition ("huge distraction," "most intense naysayer"), public silence or qualified support.

The distinction: Venezuela succeeded, so the private reservations stayed in Signal chats. Iran failed, so the private reservations became book material. The incentive to leak scales with the policy's failure — a structural feature, not a personality trait.
