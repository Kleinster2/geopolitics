# Simon Gass

Sir Simon Gass is a former British diplomat with deep Iran expertise. He served as **British ambassador to Iran** (2009-2011, present during the June 2009 protests), **NATO Senior Civilian Representative in Afghanistan** (2011-2012), then as FCO Political Director (2013-2016) where he **led the British team that negotiated the [[JCPOA]]**. He later served as **chair of the Joint Intelligence Committee** (2019-2023) — the body that synthesizes all intelligence assessments for the UK government — and as Adviser on Intelligence to successive Prime Ministers. He was also Commandant of the Royal College of Defence Studies (2018) and Ambassador to Greece (2004-2009). Now a senior adviser at SC Strategy (London-based geopolitical advisory consultancy) and Visiting Professor at King's College London.

## Key assessments (2026 Iran conflict)

Interviewed by [[Gideon Rachman]] on the FT's *Rachman Review* podcast, March 12, 2026 — Day 13 of Operation Epic Fury.

### Decapitation was never going to work

Gass was "much more surprised that the Americans might have thought" decapitation would collapse the regime — calling it contrary to "pretty much everything which people who have studied Iran over many years have come to understand about their system":

- The Iranian system is "built on a concept of defiance and opposition to the United States"
- "Long history of resistance and sacrifice" going back to the Iran-Iraq war — "the catalytic experience which shaped their worldview"
- "That sense of being prepared to suffer, to protect the Iranian state, is deeply, deeply held"
- Iran "absolutely saw this coming" — succession plans and deep bunkers were pre-positioned

### Ali Khamenei's possible martyrdom

On why Khamenei was not in a deep bunker when strikes hit: "It may say something about a man aged 86 who saw his time coming to the end and felt that the risk of death was less than the value of being seen as a martyr." Gass emphasizes this is speculative.

### Mojtaba Khamenei

- Election by Assembly of Experts (88 senior clerics, most appointed by Ali Khamenei) was "almost inevitable" to produce a hardline candidate under war conditions
- "Most of us are building our assessment of Mojtaba on a very narrow information base"
- Ali Khamenei had been "a regulator of the system" — listening to factions, keeping the regime unified, avoiding being pinned with failing decisions. Whether Mojtaba governs the same way is unknown

### Iran's three power centers

| Center | Role | Relative strength |
|--------|------|-------------------|
| Supreme Leader's office | Patronage, appointments (military, judiciary, parastatals), massive wealth | Strongest — but new leader untested |
| IRGC | Military force + economic presence (oil exploitation), wealth | Strong — "in the driving seat" during wartime |
| Civilian government (Pezeshkian) | Economy, internal administration | Weakest — "not held in great weight" by the other two |

### Strait of Hormuz — existential trigger

Iran had three means of asymmetric retaliation:
1. **Proxies** (Hezbollah, Iraq Shia militias) — "weakened considerably over the last two or three years"
2. **Ballistic missiles** — "heavily degraded last June when Israel attacked Iran and has been still more degraded"
3. **Strait of Hormuz obstruction** — reserved for use "unless it felt that it was in an existential struggle for the survival of the regime"

**The trigger**: "Once President Trump comes out on day one of the conflict and says, we are going to free the Iranian people... I think that is the moment at which the regime concludes that this is potentially a fight to the death and that therefore they have to use all of the tools at their disposal."

On methods: mining, drones at ships, limpet mines, ballistic missiles, rigid inflatable boats — "all sorts of ways in which you can threaten this." The key insight: "You don't need to physically close the strait, you simply need to be in a position to deter ship owners from wanting to put their tankers at risk."

### War duration

- Doesn't expect a long war — oil price pressure "intense"
- Military leaders will eventually tell Trump: "We've attacked pretty much all of our primary targets. We can't tell you that if we keep going for another two weeks, the regime is going to collapse... Boss, if you want us to keep going, we'll keep going. But we are not honestly sure that it's gonna have a great deal of additional effect"
- War "not popular with the MAGA base"; midterm elections in November

### Iran's war aims

1. **Regime survival** — obvious
2. **Narrative victory**: "We as a small nation took on the combined might of the United States and they could not bring us to our knees"
3. **Hormuz demonstrated capability** as future deterrent: "We have shown that we have a powerful weapon, which we could use on future occasions"
4. After a US ceasefire, Iran might fire drones "another 24, 36 hours... just to show that they are ending the war on their terms and not those of the United States"

### Nuclear program

- Pre-June 2025: Iran could produce enough HEU for weapons "in a very short space of time, a matter of a few days, a small number of weeks"
- But weaponization — turning HEU into a deliverable weapon — was "a very complicated piece of physics and engineering" and Iran was "quite a long way away from that"
- Gass believes Iran had "decided to approach threshold status — close enough that it could produce a nuclear weapon at time of need," citing Libya (gave up program, Gaddafi fell) and Ukraine (gave up weapons, invaded)
- **Fatwa lapsed**: Ali Khamenei's fatwa against nuclear weapons was "not insignificant" — but "that fatwa lapses on his death, so it is no longer enforced." (Note: this is a simplification. In Shia jurisprudence, the fatwa lost its *institutional* authority — followers already emulating Khamenei could theoretically continue observing it, but state institutions have no obligation. The Atlantic Council has argued the fatwa was never a proper fatwa at all. Mojtaba Khamenei's first public letter made no reference to the nuclear file or the fatwa.)
- Post-war reconstitution would be detected: "Israel and the United States and probably quite a lot of other countries, are directing massive intelligence capability to detect any sign"
- Centrifuges "pretty much trashed" in June 2025 — "very delicate instruments"

### Diplomacy is dead

Three sequential negotiation failures have convinced Iranian hardliners that talks with the US are futile:
1. **JCPOA (2015)** — Trump withdrew in 2018; "the United States had never filled its obligations under that agreement anyway"
2. **Pre-June 2025 talks** — "negotiations were interrupted by Israel attacking Iran and the US joining in"
3. **Pre-February 2026 talks** — "We had a process of negotiation going on and the United States just, without warning, attacked Iran"

"The hardliners are the ones making the decisions in Iran at the moment. It is the IRGC that is in the driving seat and I do not think that they will want to sit down with the United States."

### Nationalist backlash against the US

"A lot of people in Iran are saying to themselves, frankly, if this is the help that President Trump was promising us in January, we are seeing bombs landing around our residencies... even if we detest the regime, there is a rise in a tide of anti-Americanism in Iran."

This may form "more of a nationalist bond which says, OK, we don't like the ayatollahs, but frankly, we would rather them than have our future being determined by President Trump and the White House."

### Gulf states post-war

- "Extraordinarily angry at Iran" for attacks on energy infrastructure, civilian infrastructure, desalination plants
- But pragmatic: "they will know that they have to live with Iran without the United States being present at the same level of force"
- Also angry at Israel and US for wide attacks including against Qatar (a Gulf state)
- Questioning "what is the value of a US security blanket if it can't actually protect you"
- Abraham Accords: "some people in the UAE and Bahrain... will be saying, was it right for us to enter the Abraham Accords with Israel? It's not looking quite such a good bet"

### UK policy

- Starmer's legal stance consistent with longstanding UK policy — no plausible imminent threat from Iran
- Blair's Iraq precedent: stepping away from international law "didn't end at all well"
- British armed forces too depleted after "at least 15 years of underfunding" — "so few platforms to be able to deploy to the Gulf in time of need"
- If UK had joined: Starmer would face questions about oil prices, end game, war duration — "entirely powerless to achieve that result"

### JCPOA — as lead negotiator

"I led the British team that negotiated what was called the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action... Although the deal was not perfect, it was actually a very good way of ensuring that Iran did not make dangerous progress in its nuclear ambitions for a long period of time. I think that if we had stuck with that agreement and President Trump hadn't withdrawn from it in 2018, we would be in a much better position."

On the caricature of Iran: "Many people who comment on Iran who try to make out that the Iranians are fundamentally untrustworthy, that nothing they say is worth any value, that they are just hysterically opposed to the United States and are determined to bring its downfall. And that is just an oversimplistic view of a very complex and in some ways quite sophisticated country."
