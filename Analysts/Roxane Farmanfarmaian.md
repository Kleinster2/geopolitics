# Roxane Farmanfarmaian

Dr Roxane Farmanfarmaian is RUSI Senior Associate Fellow (International Security), Director of International Studies and Global Politics at the University of Cambridge Institute for Continuing Education, Senior Research Fellow at King's College London, and a non-resident fellow at the Quincy Institute. Specialist in Middle East politics, security, and energy with a particular focus on Iran.

Co-leads RUSI's "Iran in the Global Security Context" project with [[Burcu Ozcelik]].

## Key analytical contributions

### "Is Iran at a Tipping Point?" — RUSI, January 19, 2026

Co-authored with Burcu Ozcelik. Published six weeks before the war began. Key arguments:

**On the protests (December 2025-January 2026):**
- This protest cycle was "qualitatively different" — a structural, potentially existential stress test of state capacity, elite cohesion, and deterrence, not a routine cycle.
- Trigger was economic (rial volatility, >50% inflation), but rapidly became anti-government and political.
- Regime killed an estimated 2,500+ people (contested; one report of 16,500 feared dead amid internet blackout). The brutality underscored how far legitimacy claims had eroded.
- Legitimacy "does not need to be universal to sustain an autocracy, but it does need to be sufficient to reduce the everyday costs of rule." That reservoir is thinning — the regime relies more on fear and fatigue than consent.

**On security force indicators:**
- No credible indicators of high-level defections at the time of writing. No evidence that elite/special IRGC units had to be activated at scale beyond regular forces and Basij.
- The absence of these indicators "does not mean the regime is secure; it means, rather, that it may still be operating within familiar coercive parameters, containing unrest without yet revealing the deeper vulnerabilities that often precede systemic rupture."

**On external intervention dynamics:**
- Israeli political/media voices (including a far-right minister, Channel 14's Tamir Morag, and Mike Pompeo) made claims about Israeli operatives among protesters. This may have been psychological operations to rattle Tehran — but it gave the regime a rationale for its worst-ever crackdown.
- Once protests were cast as externally orchestrated, "the threshold for repression dropped and the scope for de-escalatory bargains narrowed." Dissent was reclassified from a governance problem into a national-security threat.
- Trump threatened military intervention if protesters were harmed. For the first time in Iranian protest history, the Army (Artesh) publicly stated it would protect strategic assets and stood with the government — the external threat framing worked.

**On US options (pre-war):**
- The US was "very likely assessing its options and, critically, whether limited kinetic action against elements of the regime's security apparatus can shape events inside Iran that can accelerate the conditions for political transition."
- US intervention would look like a ladder: tighter sanctions, covert/cyber activity, political support for opposition, information operations, and at the upper end, limited kinetic strikes.
- A regime-change outcome would require a large-scale, sustained campaign maintained long enough to fracture the coercive apparatus. That "sits uneasily with President Trump's apparent preference for short, demonstrably 'winnable' operations with clear endpoints."

**On regime collapse vs. transition:**
- The concept of regime collapse is "quite problematic." More likely: "some sort of transition, whether led by one individual or a constellation of leaders across the security forces and intelligence."
- A deeper contest is underway over what "Iran" is for: a state anchored in velayat-e faqih and export of revolution, or a state whose organizing principle is national renewal, dignity, and prosperity.
- Slogans like "No to Gaza; no to Lebanon; my life only for Iran" capture sharpened Iran-first nationalism that rejects the Islamic Republic's transnational revolutionary project while drawing on Iranian-Shiite cultural identity.

**On US credibility trap:**
- "If Washington ultimately opts not to act after publicly signalling support for civilian protesters, the precedent is easy to anticipate. Future US statements of solidarity will be read as non-credible."

### RUSI team wartime analysis

Ozcelik (co-author, RUSI Senior Research Fellow) published "Goldilocks" analysis in *The Observer* (March 8, 2026): the US-Israel gamble is to bring Iran to near-but-not-complete collapse — "regime transformation, something short of total state collapse, but still a decisive alteration in how state power is organised, exercised and enforced." Warned that Syria's transition from uprising to Assad's fall took 13 years including civil war and radical group emergence.

## Assessment

Farmanfarmaian's January 2026 piece is analytically distinctive because it was written in the exact window between the protest crisis and the war — mapping the conditions, incentives, and escalation dynamics that produced the strikes six weeks later. Her framework — that the regime can suppress protests but each cycle thins legitimacy, that external intervention framing hardens repression, and that US credibility was already trapped — reads as a near-blueprint for what followed. Her insistence that "collapse" is the wrong frame and that transition within security institutions is more likely aligns with [[Ali Vaez]]'s succession analysis (Larijani/Ghalibaf running the state) and challenges the regime-change expectations of [[Fred Kagan]]. Her co-author Ozcelik's "Goldilocks" framing — that US/Israel want near-but-not-total collapse — captures the strategic ambiguity that [[Sanam Vakil]] argues will drift into containment and strategic limbo.
