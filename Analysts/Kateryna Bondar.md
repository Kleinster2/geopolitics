# Kateryna Bondar

## Identity

Fellow at the [[CSIS]] **Wadhwani AI Center** (Center for Strategic and International Studies). Researches Russian and Ukrainian use of unmanned systems, AI integration in military operations, and the civil-military pipeline that turns commercial drone development into combat capability.

Ukrainian by background. Prior roles: adviser to Ukraine's Ministry of Defense and Ministry of Finance; senior project manager at PwC working with the Ukraine MoD; Stanford Ukrainian Emerging Leaders Fellow (2019-2020); financial control manager at Microsoft. Degrees from Taras Shevchenko National University of Kyiv.

Often referred to as "Kate" Bondar in podcast appearances.

## Core Argument

Russia has built a **comprehensive sovereign ecosystem** around unmanned systems and AI — spanning presidential decrees, civilian industry, talent pipelines, and military scaling — and is **ahead of NATO** in operational deployment of these capabilities, even where the underlying technology is not always superior. Western analysts routinely underestimate Russian adaptive capacity by focusing on the legacy Soviet bureaucracy at the expense of the parallel innovation track.

Her framing inverts the common Russia-vs-Ukraine drone-warfare comparison: where Ukraine has decentralized creativity (~800-1,000 manufacturers, 400 drone types in monthly use, "zoo of technology"), Russia has centralized champion-picking — fewer systems, more iterations, faster scaling, deeper civil-military integration.

## Key Claims (April 2026 Decoding Geopolitics interview)

### Russian state ecosystem
- Two of ~10 long-term goals in the **presidential decree on national development** are devoted to unmanned systems and AI integration
- National strategy targets:
  - **1 million** people in the unmanned-systems industry by 2030
  - **95%** AI-readiness of priority business/industries by 2030 (up from 12% in 2022)
  - **15,500** AI specialists graduating annually by 2030 (up from ~3,000 in 2022)
  - **130,000** large UAS produced annually by 2030; **350,000** by 2035
- Designated officials carry **personal responsibility** for hitting indicators
- Robotics courses introduced at schools and universities to seed the talent pipeline

### Civil-to-military pipeline
- Russia treats civilians as "the resource of innovation"
- Civilian industry develops dual-use technology; the state acquires/scales the winners
- Example: [[ZALA]] — civilian drone company (founded private), majority-acquired by Kalashnikov Concern in 2015, now produces top-tier loitering munitions ([[Lancet]]) and ISR systems

### "Champion picking" centralization
- Bondar paraphrasing Russian Defense Minister [[Andrei Belousov]]: satisfied with **"garage-level"** innovation; the state's job is to find what works and scale it
- Examples Bondar cites:
  - **Geran (Shahed-derived)** — Bondar tallies 30+ iterations; Geran "has almost nothing to do with the original Iranian design"
  - **Lancet FPV system** — government funding, scaled across the front
  - **Molniya** — cheap fixed-wing, plywood-grade, iterated to operational maturity
- Compare Ukraine's market-driven champion-picking via the MoD/DPA digital marketplaces (vetted vendors, aggregated unit purchases, scaled procurement)

### Regulatory asymmetry on autonomous weapons
- Bondar argues Russia's **lack of ethical/regulatory constraints** is a structural iteration-speed advantage on fully autonomous weapon systems
- Faster real-environment deployment → faster feedback loops → faster improvement
- Invokes the framing of AI-enabled autonomous weapons as **"the new nuclear bomb"** and warns NATO has no coherent response

### NATO assessment (blunt)
> "Russia is way ahead of NATO."
> — Kateryna Bondar, *Decoding Geopolitics Podcast*, April 26, 2026

She frames Russia's case as more applicable to the US military than Ukraine's, because Russia and the US share comparable institutional pathologies — large bureaucracies, dependence on legacy primes, friction between visionary younger generals and entrenched old systems.

## Caveats and Nuances

Bondar explicitly does **not** argue Russia has been transformed:
- Bureaucracy, corruption, hierarchy-driven information suppression "all exist; it's all there"
- Russian acquisition system institutionally hard to reform — they "find shortcuts" rather than fix it
- Internal generational fight between visionary new generals (who understand AI/unmanned systems) and the old guard
- Stalemate on the battlefield in technology terms — neither model definitively winning
