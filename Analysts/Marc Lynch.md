# Marc Lynch

## Overview

Political scientist at George Washington University and contributing columnist at *Foreign Policy*. One of the leading academic analysts of Arab politics, Gulf state dynamics, and US Middle East policy. Author of multiple books on Arab media and regional order. Known for institutional analysis of Gulf state decision-making and skepticism of US interventionist approaches.

## Key positions

- Professor of Political Science and International Affairs, George Washington University
- Contributing columnist, *Foreign Policy*
- Non-resident Senior Fellow, Carnegie Endowment for International Peace (previous)

## Analytical framework

Lynch emphasizes the structural fragility of US-Gulf security arrangements and the gap between Washington's assumptions about regional order and how Gulf states actually perceive their interests. He is skeptical that Arab-Israeli alignment against Iran can be sustained without resolving underlying tensions.

## 2026 Iran conflict analysis

### "The United States Could Lose the Gulf" (Foreign Policy, March 5, 2026)

Lynch's central argument: the war represents both the apotheosis and potential collapse of America's Middle East order. Washington always wanted Arab-Israeli cooperation against Iran without resolving the Palestinian issue — and now has it, precisely as the region may be collapsing.

**Iran's three-level Gulf strategy** — Lynch argues Iran's targeting of Gulf states is rational, not random:

1. **Shatter civilian immunity** — targeting civilian centers to signal unprecedented vulnerability to Gulf populations and leaders. Gulf leaders' publicized visits to shopping malls and public spaces show how seriously they take public shock.
2. **Cause global economic pain for ceasefire pressure** — closed the Strait of Hormuz "without much effort, simply by making threats that oil tankers did not care to test." Saudi refineries and Qatari LNG shut down even without direct attacks.
3. **Exhaust missile defense systems** — wave after wave of cheap drones and Shaheds while systematically targeting radar and communications. "No one should be fooled by high rates of missile defense success early on... The real test comes as interceptors run out and higher-quality missiles begin to launch."

**Why this time was different from June 2025**: During the Twelve-Day War, the Gulf states stayed on the sidelines and Iran refrained from targeting them. But with a clear US-Israeli regime-change war, "the benefits offered by the rapprochement with Saudi Arabia were already gone." The residual benefits of restraint evaporated — Iran didn't feel rewarded for holding back the last time.

**Gulf states' sense of betrayal**: Lynch argues the betrayal is acute precisely because Gulf leaders believed they had *better* relations with Trump than any previous US administration — his interest in Gulf financial opportunities, preference for autocracy, and personalistic style. This makes the unilateral launch of a war directly threatening their survival feel like a deeper breach of trust.

Specific grievances:
- War launched without serious consultation
- Uncomfortable with Israeli regime-change strategy (they want pragmatic autocrats, not state collapse)
- US impotence at protecting oil installations and shipping
- Inability or unwillingness to rapidly refresh interceptor stocks
- US military bases becoming "a source of threat rather than security"

**Saudi-Emirati split paused**: The war interrupted what Lynch calls the most significant reshuffling of regional order in years — Saudi Arabia building a new strategic alliance with Turkey, Qatar, Egypt, and Pakistan while pushing back against the UAE and, by proxy, Israel. Had Iran targeted only the UAE and Bahrain, "Saudi Arabia and its new partners might well have thrown the Abraham Accords states to the wolves." Instead, Iran's attacks on all Gulf states reunified them under existential threat — temporarily.

**Underlying issues remain**: The ferocity of Israel's war and US active participation will exacerbate Arab fears of Israeli ambitions. States that feared Israel's expanding military operations "will not be reassured watching its destruction of Iran. They will worry that they could be next."

**Gulf identity shattered**: Iran brought wealthy Gulf citizens "firmly, and likely permanently, back into geographical reality." The human price of regional power politics was supposed to be paid by Syrians, Sudanese, Lebanese, and Yemenis — not by them.

**Assessment**: Lynch brings deep institutional knowledge of Gulf politics and Arab public opinion that other analysts in this conflict (Kagan, Parsi, Nasr, Bohl) don't match in this specific domain. His most distinctive contribution is the Saudi-Emirati split analysis — none of the other analysts describe the pre-war Saudi realignment toward Turkey/Qatar/Egypt/Pakistan in such detail, and the argument that Iran's broad targeting accidentally rescued the Abraham Accords coalition (at least temporarily) is analytically novel. His framing overlaps with [[Karen Young]]'s GCC existential threat analysis but adds the political dimension Young's economic lens misses. Complements [[Vali Nasr]]'s analysis of Israeli ambitions (Netanyahu's "Sunni axis") — Lynch extends this to the Gulf states' own perception of being trapped. Compare with [[Ryan Bohl]]'s post-war Saudi-UAE divergence prediction: Bohl flags the same fault line but frames it as emerging after the war; Lynch shows it was already active before the war started.
