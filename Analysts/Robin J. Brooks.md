# Robin J. Brooks

Senior Fellow at the [[Brookings Institution]]. Former Chief Economist at the Institute of International Finance (IIF). Known for data-driven analysis using real-time indicators (AIS vessel tracking, trade flows, capital movements).

## Relevant analysis

### Strait of Hormuz blockade (March 2026)

Published AIS shipping data showing the de facto blockade's scope during Operation Epic Fury. Key contribution: quantifying the asymmetric cost problem — Iran's navy is destroyed but a few drones can freeze all traffic — and the Russia 2022 comparison (Hormuz ~3x more important for global oil than Russia, yet Brent rise still below the Russia precedent).

### Why Brent did not go to $200 (May 27, 2026)

Brooks's May 27 South Korea import-data post is the useful constraint on the chokepoint-power thesis. The [[Strait of Hormuz]] shock was real, but South Korea's April HS code 27 imports - mineral fuels, oils, refined products, LNG, and related products - fell only 8% year over year even though Saudi-origin imports fell 40%. Korea compensated with replacement supply: imports from Canada rose 174% and imports from Malaysia rose 63%.

The geopolitical point is that chokepoint leverage is powerful but not mechanically infinite. It depends on the availability, speed, and price of rerouting. In April 2026, South Korea paid up for replacement supply, but the average paid price across the import basket rose just under 20%, far below the $200-Brent demand-destruction scenario. That makes the war's durable effect more about risk premium, insurance, and route politics than about a confirmed total-supply collapse.

*Source: [Why did the Brent oil price not go to $200?](https://robinjbrooks.substack.com/p/why-did-the-brent-oil-price-not-go), May 27 2026.*
