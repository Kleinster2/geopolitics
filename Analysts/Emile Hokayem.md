# Emile Hokayem

## Overview
Director of regional security at the [[IISS|International Institute for Strategic Studies]]. Middle East conflict and security specialist.

## 2026 Iran conflict assessment (March 17, 2026)

**Source**: FT, "Who is winning the Middle East war?" (March 17, 2026)

Hokayem's assessment argues that despite stunning operational metrics, the US-Israel campaign has not produced strategic success — and Iran retains structural advantages.

**On the first military phase:**
- First phase of the US-Israel campaign — "pre-planned, highly scripted and methodical" — appears to be closing
- Over 13,000 strikes in two weeks; hundreds of US and Israeli aircraft, two aircraft carriers, cruise and ballistic missiles deployed
- Iran's leadership and infrastructure losses "staggering"
- Operational success "stunning" by the figures — but "pure metrics and technological supremacy don't tell the whole story about a war's trajectory"

**On US force posture strains:**
- Marine Expeditionary Unit rushed from Okinawa to the Gulf
- Air defense systems redeployed from the Korean peninsula
- Washington urging navies "from ambivalent if not unfriendly countries" to deploy in the Gulf
- Not panic, but "not indicators of sound forecasting and well-thought-through planning either"
- "For every gung-ho retired general on television, there is another quiet one shaking his head"

**On Iran's strategy — deliberate cost imposition:**
- Iran chose to impose costs rather than defend its airspace
- Targeting is deliberate and differentiated:
  - **Israel**: hit but deprioritized — depleted missile stocks cannot achieve decisive effect against a better-protected, hardened population
  - **UAE**: bore the brunt of retaliation
  - **Kuwait and Bahrain**: secondary targets
  - **Saudi Arabia**: comparatively spared; one-third of drones fired at the kingdom targeted the Shaybah oil site
- **Energy infrastructure** systematically targeted: production, refining, loading, and transport
- Strait of Hormuz paralysis "defied expectations that the US had gamed this war out"

**On the sustainment burden:**
- Reactive defense requires unsustainable resources
- Key questions: How many ships can escort tankers? Will any other nation help? How many aircraft and drones kept in the air at all times?
- Interception rate graphs don't reveal how many interceptors consumed or whether Iranian targeting has improved

**On phase 2:**
- **Israel**: continued destruction of military infrastructure plus greater targeting of the coercive apparatus — IRGC and Basij
- **US**: restore maritime traffic, defend Arab partners better, adapt to Iran's adaptations
- **Iran**: may deploy weapons not yet seen. The cruise missile mystery — Iran has fired relatively few cruise missiles. Optimistic explanation: destroyed on the ground. Likelier explanation: held back as "a particularly useful capability for close combat in the Strait of Hormuz"

**On Iran's structural advantages:**
- Geography: can target more countries and areas from more positions
- Time: the longer the war, the greater the cost to everyone else
- Asymmetry: can impose disproportionate costs
- Pain tolerance: "When survival is the goal, anything goes"

**Ukraine parallel:**
- Russia-Ukraine lessons apply: faulty assumptions can deny military victory to the stronger force; no single weapons system is decisive; adaptation gives temporary advantage before being matched; social resilience underpins military power

## Analytical significance

Hokayem's framing complements the [[Strategic Backfire (2026 Iran War)]] thesis: the more successful the military campaign, the more Iran resorts to asymmetric responses that the US cannot counter. His cruise missile reserve theory adds a specific operational dimension — Iran may be holding its most capable close-combat weapons for the Hormuz fight that lies ahead.

His assessment represents the IISS institutional perspective: technically informed, defense-data grounded, and cautious about equating operational metrics with strategic success.
