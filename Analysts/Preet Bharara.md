# Preet Bharara

Former US Attorney for the Southern District of New York (2009-2017); host of *Stay Tuned with Preet* and *CAFE Insider* podcasts. Not a career geopolitics analyst — legal/political — but regularly interviews national-security figures, and his interview framing adds structure to analyst perspectives captured in the vault.

## Background

- US Attorney, SDNY (2009-2017) — fired by [[Donald Trump|Trump]] March 2017
- Known for public-corruption and financial-fraud prosecutions
- Author of *Doing Justice* (2019)
- Founder of CAFE, host of *Stay Tuned with Preet* (weekly podcast)

## Relevance to this vault

Bharara appears in the vault primarily as **interviewer** of geopolitics analysts. His questions are typically structured around:
- Legal/institutional framing (war powers, statutory constraint on presidential action)
- Psychology of leaders (explicit framework he deploys with guests)
- Domestic political consequences of foreign policy

## April 2026 — Sciutto interview

Stay Tuned episode (April 10, 2026, recorded April 7): "The Psychology of Trump's War — Iran, Putin, and the Madman Theory." Interviewed [[Jim Sciutto]] from Tel Aviv on the [[2026 Iran conflict]]. Core framings introduced by Bharara:

- "A whole civilization will die tonight" — the April 7 Truth Social post
- [[Donald Trump|Trump]]'s investment horizon as CEO vs. his negotiating style as commander-in-chief
- Armchair-psychiatry theory: 79-year-old without close friends → fails to understand alliances
- "Had Trump been president at the relevant time, would there have been a Marshall Plan?"
- "Does Trump care about his legacy after he dies?"

Bharara's framing on the courage deficit: extended Sciutto's "courage shortage in Washington" observation to the professoriate, law firms, corporate leadership, and citizens — and pushed back on Sciutto's implication that politicians are categorically non-courageous ("I respect people who do it because I don't have the constitution for it").

## Analytical style

- Pushes guests toward concrete prediction rather than hedging
- Uses historical analogies deliberately (Marshall Plan, bipartisan consensus, Nixon madman theory)
- Legal/institutional gravity on presidential power questions
- Explicit framing of interviews around psychology of leaders
