# Karen Young

## Overview

Dr. Karen Young is a Senior Fellow at the [[Middle East Institute]] and professor at Columbia University, specializing in the political economy of the Gulf states and energy markets. Her analysis bridges energy economics and geopolitical risk, particularly for GCC countries.

## Key Facts
- **Affiliations**: [[Middle East Institute]] (Senior Fellow), Columbia University (Professor)
- **Focus**: Energy economics, GCC political economy, Gulf state development models

## Analysis — 2026 Iran conflict

### MEI briefing (March 8, 2026)

In a [[Middle East Institute|MEI]] virtual briefing alongside Gen. [[Joseph Votel]], Young assessed the energy and economic dimensions of the conflict approximately one week into Operation Epic Fury:

**Gulf energy disruption:**
- Iraq has shut in more than 1.5 mb/d, Kuwait following. If Gulf shutins reach 9 mb/d, that exceeds 10% of global supply.
- Saudi East-West pipeline bottleneck: 5 mb/d capacity but Yanbu loading limited to approximately 3 mb/d.
- UAE Fujairah pipeline only 400,000 bpd; tankers reluctant because Fujairah was itself an Iranian target.

**Russia benefiting:** US relaxed sanctions on Russian crude exports to India in the last 48 hours to ensure Indian supply. Russia squeezing Europe harder on gas.

**Tanker escort politics:** "Whose tankers are you escorting? Are we doing this as a favor to the Chinese?" — 80% of strait LNG goes to Asia. Politically loaded question for US domestic debate.

**GCC existential threat:** Young characterizes Iranian strikes on Gulf data centers and airports as "the greatest threat... really existential that the Gulf states are facing" — challenging their identity as international business/finance hubs. 90% of UAE/Qatar populations are non-nationals; airport disruptions are most psychologically damaging for expat populations.

**Energy diversification:** Will be THE priority for 5+ years — not just diversifying sources but accelerating renewables and domestic production.

**Source**: [MEI — Operation Epic Fury: What Comes Next?](https://www.youtube.com/live/YemEawWNxFo)

### CNN GPS energy market briefing (April 12, 2026)

On CNN's [[Fareed Zakaria]] GPS, immediately after Trump's Truth Social announcement of a US Navy Hormuz blockade, Young identified by title as senior research scholar at Columbia's **Center on Global Energy Policy** (CGEP).

**Markets vs. physical reality — the "wishful thinking" gap:**

> "The price of oil isn't as high as we might expect it to be. That's exactly right. And there's also a lot of difference between the futures market and what's being sold sort of ready-to-go barrels or the spot market. And I think there's some wishful thinking of the ability to encourage more transit of barrels through the Strait of Hormuz — that's obviously not resolved."

Young has tracked two variables throughout the crisis:

1. **Duration of the conflict** and impediment to transit
2. **Damage to oil and gas infrastructure** in the region

Her April 12 assessment: "Those two things are actually getting more serious and more detrimental" — pushing toward higher prices, not lower.

**The market deficit arithmetic (with new blockade layer):**

| Source | Volume (mb/d) | Status |
|--------|---------------|--------|
| Saudi East-West pipeline bypass | ~7 | Diverted via pipeline |
| UAE Fujairah pipeline bypass | ~1.8 | Diverted (capacity limit remains operative; peak runs higher per March data) |
| Iranian exports through strait | ~1.6 | Selective: to China via shadow fleet |
| Omani barrels | — | Bypass strait entirely |
| **Crude market shortage** | **~7 mb/d** | Not getting out |
| **Product market shortage** | **~4 mb/d** | Not getting out |

**Impact of the April 12 blockade announcement:**

The blockade adds the Iranian barrels (~1.6 mb/d) to the off-market total because "the Iranian barrels won't get through." Young predicted oil up on Monday open.

**Trump's "oil will go down when this is over" claim — Young's rebuttal:**

> "When this is over, it could be a long time from now. That entails the resumption of traffic, no real damage to oil production infrastructure or LNG, and some sort of accommodating government in Iran. Those are huge variables which are really really unsolved. So I think for now and into the end of 2026, we're looking at elevated oil prices for certain, elevated LNG prices for certain — because of the time it takes to restart, and of course the unknown of traffic flows getting in and out of the straight."

**The no-relief-before-2027 assessment:**

> "The time to restart oil production that has been shut in — that adds weeks, if not months, and assumes that we get to some sort of normal level of traffic flows through the straight. That 7 million barrel a day crude deficit, 4 million barrel a day product deficit, is not relieved until the straight is open."

Young agreed the earliest possible improvement is **2027**.

**Geography of acute pain — Asia first:**

> "Any economy that is heavily dependent on energy exports — Philippines, Malaysia, you name it down the list — those economies are hit the most, especially those also that use LPG as a cooking fuel. So it's not just in crude or in diesel and jet fuel."

Specific manifestations Zakaria cited and Young endorsed:
- **Sri Lanka**: shortened school week
- **Thailand**: government employees told not to take elevators, not to use air conditioning
- Broader: LPG cooking fuel crunch hits household level directly

**Europe next — jet fuel crunch imminent:**

> "What we'll see in the coming week or two to Europe is really this jet fuel crunch and refined products. So this is spreading and it creates inflationary pressures that the whole global economy has to deal with."

**The hoarding dynamic:**

> "It also encourages countries to think about basically hoarding or not sharing supply. And those countries that have the benefit of stockpiles — South Korea, Japan, even India — are going to be better off because they can shield their populations from some of that initial shock. Poor economies can't do that."

**The petrochemical/polymer channel — fertilizer, plastics, packaging:**

Young mapped the upstream transmission mechanism:

- **Fertilizers**: sulfur, ammonia, nitrogen — all feedstocks to phosphate/nitrogen complexes
- **Polymers / plastics / packaging**: any retail good at a big-box store has "some component that originally came from hydrocarbons"
- **Net effect**: inflationary pressure "not just in transit, but in fuel"

This reinforces the [[2026 Iran conflict#Fertilizer corridor — the overlooked transit risk|fertilizer corridor]] framing and supports [[Rana Foroohar]]'s inflation cascade mechanism.

**The US-is-not-fine caveat:**

Zakaria suggested the US is "probably fine." Young pushed back: "We're not fine. I mean these prices are global and so we are going to see inflationary pressure both on transit fuels and on food in the US."
