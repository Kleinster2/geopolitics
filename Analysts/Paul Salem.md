# Paul Salem

Senior fellow at the [[Middle East Institute]] (MEI). Lebanese-American scholar of Arab politics, previously MEI president. Longtime analyst of Iran-Arab relations, Lebanese political dynamics, and post-conflict transitions in the Arab world. Previously founding director of the Carnegie Middle East Center in Beirut. Regular US television commentator on Middle East developments.

## CNN This Morning Weekend (April 12, 2026)

Salem appeared on CNN This Morning Weekend with Victor Blackwell the morning after the 21-hour Islamabad talks collapsed, offering a structured post-failure framework that went beyond the immediate diplomatic read.

### Assessment: Iran degraded, US undamaged

Agreed with [[JD Vance]]'s framing that the failure is "worse for Iran than for the US":

- Iran "suffered tremendous losses" over nearly 6 weeks of bombing
- US and Israel had "free reign of the skies over Iran"
- Damage to Iran's military, governance structure, navies, air force, industrial base is "tremendous"
- Iran "built up all these capacities" over 40 years, now "hit back a fair distance"
- US "no weaker now than it was before"
- But: Iran "still able to throw many punches," "still surviving," still able to impose leverage through the [[Strait of Hormuz]] and by threatening Arab Gulf countries

### Two post-failure scenarios

Salem's core analytical contribution — what happens after talks fail:

1. **Quiet de-escalation.** US Navy finds a way to open the [[Strait of Hormuz]] and is "not overtly challenged by Iran." Strait quietly opens over the next few weeks. War effectively ends without a formal agreement; everyone calms down; effective ceasefire; strait open.
2. **Proactive US military campaign** to open the straits. Requires larger, more sustained operation than the current minesweeping.

Salem: "the only thing the US really needs is for those straits to open — one way or the other. Otherwise, I think they can live without an agreement for now because the US and Israel continue to have freedom of operation over Iran."

### Iranian offramp without formal agreement

Salem sketched an implicit bargain Iran could accept:

- Iran lets the strait open in exchange for no further attacks
- Gives Iran time to rebuild and regroup
- No formal agreement with Israel required
- Iran's nuclear program "has been set back many years"; ballistic missile program "still exists" but "greatly degraded"; Iran "somewhat has been deterred because of the great suffering it's had"

This framing is the **minimum viable path** — not a peace agreement, not a nuclear framework, just a de facto reopening accepted on both sides.

### Gulf-state split

Salem's reading of Arab Gulf positioning:

- Bulk of Gulf leaders "really want this war to come to an end" — impacted economies, disrupted energy exports, hit their societies
- Others in the Gulf feel normalization with Iran "did not pay off" — "the moment Iran felt threatened, it went ahead and bombed all of them and closed the Strait of Hormuz"
- Resulting hostility: some feel it's best to "degrade [Iran] further and not leave them with so much leverage"

Compatible with [[Tarek Masoud]]'s Oman-to-Bahrain spectrum but emphasizes the **normalization disillusionment** mechanism — Gulf states that tried to de-escalate with Iran before the war got bombed anyway, hardening hawkish sentiment.

### Israel and the Lebanon front

Salem: Israelis "will abide by President Trump's wishes, particularly as relates to Iran." If Trump asks attacks on Iran stop, they stop.

But Israel insisting on continuing its war against Hezbollah in Lebanon — "that war is ongoing now" and Israel has insisted that Lebanon ceasefire is "not connected to the front in Iran." US "could allow that."

Noted: Israel and Lebanon, "for the first time in their modern history," have agreed to direct talks in Washington on Tuesday (April 14, 2026).

## Analytical positioning

- Distinct from [[Tarek Masoud]]'s Gulf-political-economy lens: Salem's frame is **Iranian capacity degradation as the operative variable**
- Distinct from [[Anthony Blinken]]'s "tactical success, strategic failure" critique: Salem largely accepts the tactical success thesis but offers a **path to closure** absent a strategic deal
- Distinct from [[James Stavridis]]'s force-structure analysis: Salem assumes blockade can succeed if Iran "does not overtly challenge"
- Distinct from [[Ali Vaez]]'s (Crisis Group) diplomatic-pathway analysis: Salem sees de facto reopening without diplomacy as viable

Salem's framework implies that **"opening the strait" is a sufficient condition for Trump to declare victory** — the nuclear program, the ballistic missile program, and regional proxy issues can all be managed through continued US/Israeli freedom of operation. This is closer to a resignation thesis than a resolution thesis.
