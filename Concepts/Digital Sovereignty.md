# Digital Sovereignty

## Definition

The claim that states have the right to govern their own cyberspace - controlling data flows, regulating platforms, and building independent infrastructure. Evolved from defensive network security into a comprehensive geopolitical strategy for projecting power.

## Models

A 2024 study in *Digital Society* (Springer) identified four distinct models:

| Model | Champion | Logic |
|-------|----------|-------|
| Rights-based | EU | Sovereignty protects citizen rights (privacy, data protection) |
| Market-oriented | US (legacy) | Private sector leads, government enables |
| Centralization | China | State controls infrastructure and information |
| State-based | Russia | Sovereignty means isolation and control |

### EU Approach: Regulatory Sovereignty
The EU uses market access to globalize standards:
- **GDPR** (2018): Set global data protection baseline
- **Digital Services Act** (2022): Platform accountability
- **AI Act** (2024): Risk-based AI regulation
- **Digital Fairness Act**: Expected mid-2026
- France-Germany Digital Sovereignty Summit (November 2025) launched joint task force

The "Brussels Effect" - companies comply globally rather than maintain separate systems.

### Chinese Approach: Cyber Sovereignty
State control of the information environment:
- Great Firewall (Golden Shield Project, from 1998)
- Data localization requirements
- Platform censorship requirements
- Digital Silk Road - exporting model through infrastructure

### Russian Approach: Sovereign Internet
Isolation as security:
- Sovereign Internet Law (2019): Technical capability to disconnect from global internet
- Moving toward Iran-style dual-internet model
- "16 KB Curtain" - throttling that renders services unusable while maintaining appearance of connectivity
- Russia-Belarus joint "sovereign AI" project (June 2025)

### US Approach: Market Power as Sovereignty
Historical: Open internet promotion (Clinton-era policy)
Current: Weaponizing chokepoints:
- Semiconductor export controls
- SWIFT access
- App store bans (TikTok)
- Submarine cable restrictions — FCC barred Chinese equipment (August 2025); visa sanctions on Chilean officials for advancing China-linked cable (February 2026, see [[Chile-China Cable Sanctions]])

## Sovereign AI

A fast-growing strand of digital sovereignty: states seeking their own AI capacity — national models, data, and compute — rather than depending wholly on US or Chinese providers. Two models are emerging. The autarkic, state-built version is the Russia-Belarus joint "sovereign AI" project (June 2025, noted above). The dominant version is commercial: US chipmaker [[Nvidia]] markets a government program it calls "IA Soberana" (Sovereign AI) and reported roughly $30B in sovereign-customer revenue in FY2026, about 14% of group revenue (confirmed). Its Nemotron-class open models are positioned so any government can fine-tune without depending on a closed provider.

This sharpens the Global South bind under Infrastructure Dependency: "sovereign AI" built on a single US vendor's GPUs is a different dependency, not independence. Marcio Aguiar (NVIDIA's enterprise lead for Latin America) frames Brazil and Mexico as the region's two anchor markets, with smaller LatAm states adopting faster but lacking project critical mass, and warns that resource-rich states risk exporting critical minerals and talent rather than climbing into higher-value AI development (Aguiar's characterization, CBN Futuramente, June 2026). Brazil's existing sovereign compute is concentrated in one state actor — the oil company Petrobras, NVIDIA's largest Brazil client — whose seismic supercomputers are the country's de facto national AI compute base (see Cross-vault).

## Key Tensions

### Data Localization vs. Free Flow
- States demand data stay within borders (Russia, China, India, EU variants)
- US tech companies resist (threatens business model)
- Cloud infrastructure concentrated in US/allied hands

### Platform Regulation vs. Innovation
- EU regulates first, risks competitiveness
- US innovates first, risks harm
- China controls domestically, competes globally

### Infrastructure Dependency
- Global South reliant on foreign infrastructure (see [[Digital Colonialism]])
- Africa: 223 data centers across 38 countries, projected $9.15B market by 2029
- India building alternatives: Aadhaar, UPI, Open Network for Digital Commerce

## Critical Events

- **TikTok ban debate (2024-2025)**: Crystallized tension between open internet ideology and national security claims
- **WSIS+20 (December 2025)**: Debated whether governance remains multistakeholder or shifts to state-centric
- **Chile-China cable sanctions (February 2026)**: US revoked visas for three Chilean officials advancing a China-linked submarine cable. First personal sanctions against an allied government over telecom infrastructure. See [[Chile-China Cable Sanctions]].
- **UN Cyber Mechanism (March 2026)**: New permanent body for responsible state behavior in cyberspace
- **NDRC unwinds Meta-Manus / Singapore-washing playbook breaks (April 27, 2026)**: China's [[NDRC]] ordered the unwind of [[Meta]]'s $2B acquisition of [[Manus AI]], asserting extraterritorial reach over a Singapore-domiciled holding entity by treating the underlying [[AI agents|AI agent]] technology as substantially developed in mainland China and therefore subject to tech-export-control review. The personal-mobility leverage on co-founders Xiao Hong and Ji Yichao — barred from leaving mainland China since March 22, 2026 — was the operational binding constraint regardless of corporate posture. The April 27 unwind order is retroactive: capital had been transferred, ~100 engineers had moved to Meta's Singapore office, integration into Meta Superintelligence Labs had begun. The "[[Singapore washing]]" offshore-restructuring playbook (relocate HQ to Singapore, wind down mainland China product, restructure cap table, US-acquirer exit) — the implicit base case for [[HongShan]]'s post-2023 China-AI portfolio — failed in its first major test. Pairs with the [[NDRC]] March 2026 Panama Canal port intervention against [[Maersk]] / [[MSC]] as an earlier example of NDRC functioning as enforcement tool against foreign corporations in PRC-claimed strategic perimeters. Combined with the April 25 Seoul Economic Daily reporting that Beijing is barring Chinese tech firms from accepting US capital without state approval (inbound-side restriction), 2026 is the year the offshore corridor closes from Beijing too — symmetric with [[Pax Silica]] / outbound-EO / [[CFIUS]] from the US side.

## Critiques

- "Sovereignty" can mean citizen protection (EU) or state repression (China, Russia)
- Risk of [[Splinternet]] - fragmenting the network effects that make internet valuable
- [[Techno-Nationalism]] can lead to unrealistic autarky goals
- Digital sovereignty for Global South often means choosing between dependencies (US vs. China)
