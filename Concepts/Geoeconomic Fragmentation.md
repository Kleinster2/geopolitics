# Geoeconomic Fragmentation

Geoeconomic fragmentation is the policy-driven reversal of global economic integration along geopolitical lines — states deliberately unwinding cross-border trade, investment, technology, and financial ties on grounds of national or economic security rather than market logic. It is the economic face of the broader erosion of the [[International Order]]: where [[Geoeconomics]] is the general use of economic tools for strategic ends, fragmentation is the specific structural process the world economy has entered since roughly 2018.

The term was coined by the [[IMF]] (Aiyar et al., 2023 Staff Discussion Note) to distinguish this strategic, security-motivated reversal from the earlier market-driven slowdown in trade growth ("slowbalization"). The distinction is the whole point: slowbalization was firms re-optimizing; fragmentation is governments redrawing the map, and it is self-reinforcing once retaliation begins.

## Why states fragment

Three motives, often blended:

- **Security** — reducing dependence on a rival for critical inputs (semiconductors, energy, [[Critical Minerals]], medicines).
- **Autonomy** — insulating against another state's coercive leverage over shared networks (payments, technology standards, sea lanes).
- **Rivalry** — denying a competitor access to one's own markets, capital, or technology to slow its rise.

The IMF identifies the channels along which integration's gains once flowed and fragmentation's costs now fall: trade, technology diffusion, capital and foreign direct investment, cross-border finance and payments, and migration. Empirically (Aiyar, Malacrino, Presbitero 2024), greater geopolitical distance — measured by UN voting alignment — now measurably reduces bilateral trade, FDI, and cross-border bank claims, with investment increasingly concentrating inside aligned blocs ("friend-shoring").

## Fragmentation as statecraft

Fragmentation is not only a drift; it is actively driven by coercive economic statecraft. The mechanism is [[Weaponized Interdependence]]: states that control network hubs — dollar clearing, SWIFT, advanced-chip tooling, chokepoints like the [[Strait of Hormuz]] or [[Critical Minerals]] refining — weaponize that control, and targets respond by building parallel systems. Each act of weaponization therefore accelerates the fragmentation it is meant to exploit.

[[Pierre-Olivier Gourinchas]], the outgoing [[IMF]] chief economist, framed the danger in June 2026 as a "tit-for-tat" world in which states "continually trying to exploit the small advantages they have in a way that is ultimately self-defeating." Exploiting a chokepoint works "for a little while," he argued, but rivals build around it — so the leverage decays while the cost of the duplicated system is permanent. The strategic implication: chokepoint advantage is a wasting asset, not a durable source of power.

## The financial leg

The deeper rupture is financial. Gourinchas's sequencing claim is that a world fragmented on trade eventually fragments on finance, because weaponizing the dollar — freezing reserves, cutting off payment access — is precisely what drives states to build alternative payment rails and diversify reserves. This is the statecraft engine behind [[Dollar Dominance|de-dollarization]]: the 2022 freezing of ~$300B in Russian central-bank reserves taught every non-aligned central bank that dollar reserves are conditional on political alignment, and [[BRICS]] payment and settlement initiatives, [[China]]'s CIPS, and central-bank gold accumulation are the responses.

## Bloc formation — the constructive face

Fragmentation is reorganization, not autarky. As ties to rivals thin, states deepen ties within aligned groups — the proliferation of bilateral and pluri-lateral deals (EU-Mercosur, EU-India, [[BRICS]] enlargement, CPTPP). Gourinchas's metaphor: integrating groups "create their own gravity… like forming a new planet," pulling others in rather than leaving members isolated. For the order, this points toward a multipolar economic system organized around several gravitational centers ([[United States]]-aligned, [[China]]-aligned, and hedging middle powers like [[India]]) rather than a single integrated market under [[Pax Americana]].

## Why it matters for the order

- It hollows out the multilateral institutions (WTO, and the rules the [[IMF]] was built to steward) from within, as their principal architect — the [[United States]] — disengages.
- It rewards middle powers that can hedge between blocs and punishes small economies forced to choose.
- It makes economic interdependence a source of vulnerability rather than the peace-through-trade stabilizer liberal theory assumed — closer to a [[Security Dilemma]] in economic form.
