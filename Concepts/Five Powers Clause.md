# Five Powers Clause

## Definition

A standard exclusion in commercial war-risk insurance policies that automatically terminates coverage in any conflict involving one or more of five major powers: the [[United States]], [[United Kingdom]], [[France]], [[Russia]], and [[China]]. The clause reflects the insurance industry's assessment that great power conflict creates uninsurable risk.

## Strategic Significance

The Five Powers Clause transforms latent military tension into immediate economic disruption — without a shot being fired. If a crisis escalates to the point where markets perceive a plausible conflict between any two of the five named powers, insurers can invoke the clause and pull coverage from affected shipping lanes. This creates a self-reinforcing cycle:

1. **Escalation signal** — military demonstrations, legal declarations, exclusion zones
2. **Insurance withdrawal** — Five Powers Clause triggered or anticipated
3. **Carrier refusal** — shipping companies will not sail uninsured
4. **De facto blockade** — trade ceases through the affected route without physical interdiction

## Applications

### Strait of Hormuz (2026)

The [[2026 Iran conflict]] demonstrated the insurance mechanism in action — though Iran is not one of the five named powers, the demonstrated risk was sufficient to collapse insurer willingness. The blockade held with only ~4% of normal traffic transiting in the first two weeks, driven primarily by insurance withdrawal rather than physical obstruction.

### Taiwan Strait (potential)

[[Eyck Freymann]] (Hoover/Stanford, April 2026) identified the Five Powers Clause as the critical mechanism that would make a Chinese quasi-blockade of [[Taiwan]] self-executing. Because both the US and China are named powers, any crisis perceived as a potential US-China conflict would automatically trigger coverage termination for Taiwan Strait shipping — before any military engagement occurs.

This is structurally different from Hormuz: Iran had to *demonstrate* capability to collapse insurance confidence. For the Taiwan Strait, the clause means insurance evaporates at the mere *perception* of US-China confrontation. The threshold for economic disruption is lower.

## Implications

- Great power competition carries embedded economic tripwires through commercial insurance structures
- A state seeking to coerce through a maritime chokepoint does not need to match the adversary's naval power — it only needs to create sufficient escalation risk that insurers invoke the clause
- Efforts to provide government-backed reinsurance (as attempted during the Hormuz crisis) are the natural countermeasure, but require pre-crisis frameworks to be credible
