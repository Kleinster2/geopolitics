# Pax Silica

## Overview

**Pax Silica** is a US-led economic-security framework launched in December 2025 to coordinate trusted supply chains for [[Semiconductors]], AI infrastructure, [[Critical Minerals]], advanced manufacturing, energy, and logistics. It is non-binding, but its geopolitical significance is that it moves technology competition from individual export controls toward coalition-wide supply-chain mapping, investment coordination, and supplier qualification.

The framework is best read as [[Technology Competition]] becoming institutional architecture. The US is trying to turn its alliance network into a trusted technology stack: minerals and energy at the bottom, fabrication and manufacturing in the middle, AI compute and applications at the top.

## Strategic Logic

Pax Silica addresses the same vulnerability described in [[Supply Chains]]: the most important technology systems are assembled from globally distributed chokepoints. China remains central in mineral processing, intermediate manufacturing, electronics inputs, and logistics. The US response is not full autarky; it is coalition filtering.

The architecture has three layers:

| Layer | Geopolitical function |
|---|---|
| Supply-chain mapping | Identify Chinese or other adversary-controlled choke points before crisis |
| Joint ventures / co-investment | Build trusted replacement capacity across partner countries |
| Supplier qualification | Make sourcing posture part of access to allied contracts and markets |

This turns "friend-shoring" from a corporate resilience strategy into economic statecraft. The key question is who gets treated as inside the trusted network, who remains outside, and how quickly procurement rules harden around that boundary.

## Commercial Space Extension

The May 28, 2026 Journal of Space Commerce / Ex Terra Media excerpt applies Pax Silica to commercial space hardware. The argument is that satellite and launch-vehicle suppliers with China-anchored inputs face qualification risk before formal regulations appear, because US-aligned contracts can quietly downlist suppliers that lack diversification plans.

The exposed components are dual-use: RF systems, printed circuit board assemblies, power electronics, electro-optical sensors, carbon-fiber composites, precision-machined aluminum structures, and rare-earth permanent magnets for actuators and reaction wheels. This links Pax Silica to [[Space Geopolitics]] because commercial space infrastructure is also military, intelligence, and communications infrastructure.

Named networks in the excerpt included Terran Orbital, L3Harris, Axiom Space, Safran, and Sierra Space. In geopolitical terms, they are not just companies; they are procurement channels through which the US-led bloc can push trusted-vendor standards into sub-tier suppliers.

## Limits

Pax Silica is non-binding, so its near-term power depends on procurement behavior, diplomatic pressure, financing, and investment screening rather than treaty enforcement. It also risks fragmenting technology supply chains further: the more the US turns trusted supply into a coalition boundary, the more China has incentive to deepen alternative networks and retaliate through minerals, manufacturing inputs, or market access.

The durable signal is not the declaration itself. It is the migration of security criteria into ordinary supplier selection.
