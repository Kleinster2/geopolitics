# Traditionalism

## Overview

Capital-T Traditionalism is an esoteric anti-modern intellectual current, not simply a preference for old customs. Its modern form is usually traced to Rene Guenon in the 1920s and was later developed in different directions by figures such as Julius Evola and Frithjof Schuon. The current treats "Tradition" as a lost primordial spiritual wisdom underlying authentic religious forms, and it sees modernity as a phase of metaphysical decline rather than as progress.

Geopolitically, Traditionalism matters because parts of the contemporary populist and radical right have borrowed its language: cyclical decline, hierarchy, spiritual elite, anti-liberalism, anti-globalism, and civilizational rupture. [[Benjamin R. Teitelbaum]]'s [[War for Eternity]] is the key vault source for that modern political translation.

## Distinction

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| traditionalism | Ordinary preference for inherited customs, religion, family structures, or social norms |
| Traditionalism | Esoteric school/movement built around primordial Tradition, cyclical time, hierarchy, initiation, and anti-modern metaphysics |
| conservatism | Political disposition toward continuity, institutions, and gradual change; may overlap with traditionalism but is not the same doctrine |
| right populism | Mass political style opposing elites, immigration, globalization, or liberal institutions; may borrow Traditionalist motifs without being doctrinally Traditionalist |

## Core Ideas

### Primordial Tradition

Traditionalists posit a lost metaphysical source behind the world's major religious traditions. This makes the current different from ordinary religious conservatism: the central claim is not simply "our inherited religion is true," but that authentic traditions preserve fragments of a deeper primordial wisdom.

### Anti-Modernity

Modernity is interpreted as decline: secularism, liberalism, capitalism, communism, mass democracy, and technological materialism are often treated as symptoms of the same leveling force.

### Cyclical Time

Traditionalist history is cyclical rather than progressive. The present is often read as a late dark age or terminal phase, after which destruction can precede renewal.

### Hierarchy

Traditionalist politics, where it becomes political, tends to idealize hierarchy, caste, spiritual authority, and differentiated social roles. This makes its anti-liberalism deeper than ordinary nostalgia.

## Geopolitical Relevance

### Civilizational Politics

Traditionalist language turns politics into a struggle over civilization, spiritual order, and sacred hierarchy. In contemporary geopolitics, this can reinforce anti-liberal civilizational blocs, anti-globalist rhetoric, and hostility to universalist rights language.

### Metapolitics

The movement often matters less through parties than through intellectuals, media, schools, publishers, advisers, and cultural figures. This is why [[War for Eternity]] is useful: Teitelbaum follows networks rather than just institutions.

### Bannon-Dugin-Olavo Link

Teitelbaum's modern case is not that every populist-right figure is a Traditionalist. The more precise claim is that Steve Bannon, Aleksandr Dugin, Olavo de Carvalho, and related figures intersected with Traditionalist ideas in ways that illuminate a shared anti-modern vocabulary across the United States, Russia, Brazil, and parts of Europe.

### Anti-Progress Narratives

Traditionalism rejects the modern progress story. That makes it a useful diagnostic when a political argument treats crisis, institutional collapse, or cultural disorder not as failure to be repaired, but as evidence that the modern cycle must break before renewal.

## Caveats

- Traditionalism is not identical to fascism, nationalism, conservatism, Catholic traditionalism, or generic right populism.
- Many political actors use fragments of Traditionalist language without holding the full doctrine.
- Teitelbaum's network analysis should be attributed as interpretation unless backed by primary statements or organizational evidence.
- The same intellectual lineage contains religious, scholarly, esoteric, and political variants; avoid treating them as a single party line.
