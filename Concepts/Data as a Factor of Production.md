# Data as a Factor of Production

## Definition

China's doctrinal designation of data as a fifth factor of production — alongside land, labor, capital, and technology — and the institutional machinery built on that doctrine. The concept marks a divergence in economic statecraft: one state formally measures and prices data as a national resource; its rival, despite hosting the world's largest data markets, declines to engage the question at the standard-setting level. Mike Kuiken (Hoover Institution; vice-chair, [[US-China Economic and Security Review Commission]]) frames the asymmetry as "strategic intent versus strategic indifference" (FT, February 9, 2026).

## The Chinese stack

Three layers, built top-down (all confirmed):

| Layer | Instrument | Date |
|-------|-----------|------|
| Doctrine | CPC Central Committee / State Council "Opinions" designating data a market production factor | March 2020 |
| Accounting | Ministry of Finance Interim Provisions — enterprises may book data as inventory or intangible assets | Effective January 1, 2024 |
| Markets | Dozens of data exchanges (Shanghai, Shenzhen, Guiyang) under the National Data Administration | 2021– |

China Unicom reported Rmb204mn (~$29mn) in data assets in its first filing under the new rules (per Kuiken/FT). Adoption is thin: only ~2% of A-share listed companies (~100 firms) had disclosed data-asset recognition by April 2025 (Haynes Boone analysis) — the machinery exists, the take-up does not yet.

## Strategic stakes

- **AI feedstock**: Data is the input to AI training. A state that can measure, price, and direct data flows holds an industrial-policy lever its rival lacks. Complements China's parallel move to track AI tokens as a national economic indicator (see the investing vault's China token economy note).
- **Debt management**: The [[US-China Economic and Security Review Commission]] flagged in its 2024 report that indebted local government financing vehicles have used recognized data assets as loan collateral while the assets' market value remains "difficult to determine" — the doctrine doubles as creative accounting against a municipal debt overhang.
- **Standard-setting power**: Whoever values data first shapes how AI-era enterprise value is measured, taxed, and disclosed globally — the same first-mover dynamic as the EU's GDPR "Brussels Effect" in [[Digital Sovereignty]], applied to accounting rather than privacy.

## The US posture

The US treats data as valuable in practice — a $200bn+/yr data-broker industry, FTC intervention in the Toysmart bankruptcy (2000), litigation over RadioShack's 117mn customer records (2015) — but recognizes no internally generated data on corporate balance sheets, and neither FASB nor the SEC has a project to change that (per Kuiken). Kuiken argues the gap is a strategic choice, not a technical limit; spectrum auctions prove invisible assets can be measured when the state decides they matter.
