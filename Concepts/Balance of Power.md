# Balance of Power

## Definition
A state of equilibrium among states or alliances where no single nation or coalition is strong enough to dominate others. States act to prevent any one power from achieving hegemony.

## Key Thinkers
- Hans Morgenthau
- Kenneth Waltz
- Henry Kissinger

## Core Principles
1. States are primary actors seeking survival
2. Power is the currency of international politics
3. States balance against threats through internal buildup or alliances
4. Hegemony is naturally resisted

## Historical Examples
- Achaemenid Persia and the Greek city-states (5th-4th c. BCE) — external balancing by subsidy
- Concert of Europe (1815-1914)
- Cold War bipolarity (US vs USSR)
- Pre-WWI alliance systems

## External Balancing by Subsidy

A power outside a regional system can hold the balance without conquering it, by financing whichever party is losing so that no local hegemon emerges. The clearest ancient case is Achaemenid Persia after the Peloponnesian War: Persia funded Sparta to break Athens (412-404 BCE), then funded Athens, Thebes, Corinth, and Argos to break Sparta in the Corinthian War (395-387 BCE), and finally imposed the King's Peace (387/386 BCE) as guarantor of a divided Greece. The lever was gold, not armies — Persia recovered by subsidy and diplomacy the influence it had lost on the battlefield at Marathon, Salamis, and Plataea. The mechanism recurs wherever a wealthier outside power — the offshore balancer of realist theory, or a modern creditor or reserve-currency state — shapes a region's politics by financing division rather than by direct control.

## Contemporary Applications
- US pivot to Asia to balance China's rise
- European concerns over Russian expansion
- Middle East rivalries (Saudi Arabia vs Iran)

## Critiques
- Assumes states are rational actors
- Difficulty measuring "power" accurately
- Nuclear weapons complicate traditional balancing
