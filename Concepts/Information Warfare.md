# Information Warfare

## Overview
The use of information and communication to gain advantage over adversaries. Includes propaganda, disinformation, psychological operations, and manipulation of the information environment. Enabled by social media, now a constant feature of geopolitical competition.

## Definition

### Broad Concept
Any action to deny, exploit, corrupt, or destroy information and information systems while protecting one's own.

### Components
1. **Psychological operations (PSYOP)**: Influence target audiences
2. **Disinformation**: Deliberately false information
3. **Propaganda**: Biased information to promote cause
4. **Cyber operations**: Technical information attack
5. **Electronic warfare**: Spectrum control

## Why Information Warfare Matters

### In Modern Conflict
- Shapes perceptions before/during/after
- Cheaper than kinetic operations
- Difficult to attribute
- Can achieve objectives without fighting
- Democratic vulnerabilities

### Strategic Effects
- Undermine adversary cohesion
- Erode public trust
- Influence elections
- Create confusion
- Shape narratives

## Key Concepts

### Disinformation vs. Misinformation
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Disinformation | Deliberately false, spread intentionally |
| Misinformation | False, but spread without malice |
| Malinformation | True, but used to harm |

### Propaganda Types
- **White**: Source known, relatively accurate
- **Gray**: Source unclear
- **Black**: False source, deceptive

### Influence Operations
Coordinated efforts to affect:
- Public opinion
- Political decisions
- Social cohesion
- Trust in institutions

## Russian Information Warfare

### Doctrine
- Information as continuous battlefield
- No peace/war distinction
- "Reflexive control" (shape adversary decisions)
- Integration with military ops

### Tools
- RT, Sputnik (state media)
- Troll farms (Internet Research Agency)
- Social media manipulation
- Hack and leak operations
- Amplification networks

### Operations
| Target | Operation | Impact |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| US 2016 | Election interference | Significant |
| Ukraine | Constant disinformation | Mixed |
| Europe | Various campaigns | Variable |
| Domestic | Information control | Effective |

### Ukraine War
- "Special military operation" framing
- Atrocity denial
- NATO aggression narrative
- Domestic information control
- International: limited success

## Chinese Information Warfare

### Approach
- "United Front" influence operations
- Diaspora engagement
- Elite capture
- Media investment
- Social media (outside China)

### Focus Areas
- Taiwan narratives
- Xinjiang/Tibet deflection
- Tech reputation
- "Wolf warrior" diplomacy
- COVID-19 origins

### Differences from Russia
| Russia | China |
|--------|-------|
| Chaos, division | Image management |
| Short-term disruption | Long-term influence |
| Deniable | More attributable |
| Election interference | Elite engagement |

### Detection

- Network analysis

- Academic research

- Strategic communication

- Counter-narratives

- Free speech tensions
- Attribution difficulty
