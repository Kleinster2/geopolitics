# Madman Theory

The theory that a leader can gain leverage in coercive bargaining by appearing irrational, unpredictable, or willing to escalate beyond what observers consider rational. Originated as a [[Richard Nixon|Nixon]]-era concept; often invoked to describe [[Donald Trump|Trump]]'s negotiating posture.

## Primary source for the Trump variant

[[Jim Sciutto]]'s book ***The Madman Theory: Trump Takes On the World*** (Harper, August 2020) is the single most extensive journalistic treatment of how the theory was applied (by advisors) and observed (by adversaries) during Trump's first term. This vault treats Sciutto not as an April 2026 commentator on the framework but as its long-standing analyst — his on-the-ground interviews with foreign leaders, US officials, and intelligence personnel across 2017-2020 are the empirical base for much of the Trump-variant literature.

## Nixon / Kissinger original version

[[Richard Nixon|Nixon]] reportedly instructed [[Henry Kissinger|Kissinger]] to signal to Soviet counterparts that Nixon was capable of escalating in Vietnam beyond rational limits — to communicate "I don't know what the boss is going to do." Historical record summarized by [[Jim Sciutto]] (April 2026): behind the madman persona was "a very thoughtful, shrewd, historically educated, and strategic thinker." The irrationality was a *signal*, not a *state*.

## Core logic (rational version)

1. Adversary's expected cost of continued resistance depends on their assessment of your willingness to escalate
2. A reputation for rational restraint caps that expected cost
3. Signaling willingness to escalate irrationally raises the expected cost
4. Adversary may yield earlier or lower its demands

**Requirement for the theory to work:** the "madness" must be credible but reversible — the leader must retain the capacity to de-escalate when needed, or the threat becomes self-harming rather than coercive.

## Trump variant

Trump advisors have argued to [[Jim Sciutto]] for years that Trump's unpredictability is an asset: adversaries "give in because they're not quite sure how far he might go." Sciutto (April 2026) does not dismiss this entirely — points to examples:
- [[Qasem Soleimani]] killing (2020) — bolder than predecessors
- Bunker-buster strikes on Iran (June 2025, "12-day war") — presidents across multiple administrations had rejected this option
- Full-scale war with Iran ([[2026 Iran conflict]]) — the sequence of actual escalations gives the threat-of-further-escalation credibility

### Distinction from Nixon
Sciutto: "The madman theory only goes too far." Critical difference between Nixon and Trump versions:
- **Nixon**: irrationality was a performed signal; behind it was strategic calculation
- **Trump**: the irrationality may be genuine, not performed — decisions appear "seat of the pants," not part of a reasoned escalation ladder

When irrationality is real, not signaled, the reversibility requirement breaks. The madman can paint himself into corners ("civilization will die tonight" — see [[2026 Iran conflict]] April 7) that require either (a) following through with actions that harm the madman's own interests or (b) visible reversal that erodes the coercive reputation. Neither outcome produces the yield-earlier effect the theory predicts.

### Where it has worked for Trump
- Some trade concessions (initial tariff threats against allies)
- [[Iran]] initial diplomatic posture (got them to Islamabad talks April 10)

### Where it has failed
- **[[Denmark]]/[[Greenland]]**: positions hardened rather than yielded
- **[[Europe]]**: deepened intra-EU trade arrangements, increased independence from US
- **[[Iran]] final resolution**: 10-point plan was "available weeks ago" (Bremmer) — threats did not produce earlier yield; eventually Trump accepted terms close to what Iran had proposed before the war
- **Alliance coherence**: [[NATO]] commentary ("paper tiger") adopts adversary's preferred frame — Sciutto notes this *weakens* rather than strengthens US leverage

## Adversary learning effect

Per Sciutto: if [[Xi Jinping|Xi]], [[Vladimir Putin|Putin]], or [[Kim Jong-un]] observe a US president willing to escalate in unexpected directions, they:

1. May recalculate their own risk tolerance upward (defensive: get nuclear weapons, stockpile, harden)
2. May accelerate timelines before the US can reload
3. Paradoxically: may find it easier to predict Trump because his threats exceed his follow-through capacity, reducing the unpredictability value

**Secondary effect**: US allies ([[Japan]], [[South Korea]], [[Germany]]) also recalculate and pursue independent deterrents — see [[Alliance Credibility Crisis]].

## Cross-reference to Bremmer's "TACO" framework

[[Ian Bremmer]]'s [[Trump TACO|TACO]] ("Trump Always Chickens Out") framework is effectively an adversary's negation of the madman theory: if the pattern holds that Trump climbs down after initial threats, adversaries can discount the threats and wait for the climb-down. Bremmer's adversary pain-capacity predictor table (see [[Trump TACO]]) maps when the climb-down is likeliest. The two frameworks are duals: madman theory models the *ex ante* signal; TACO models the *ex post* outcome.

## Requirements for madman theory to function (synthesis)

1. Signaling capacity — adversary must receive the threat
2. Credibility — some prior actions must make the threat plausible
3. Reversibility — the leader must retain the ability to climb down
4. Strategic coherence — the end state achievable via the threat must be one the leader actually wants
5. Opacity — the leader's internal deliberations must not be transparent to the adversary

Trump variant satisfies (1) and (2) but has systematic issues with (3), (4), and (5):
- (3) public commitment via Truth Social often makes reversal visibly costly
- (4) end states are often incoherent or contradictory across statements
- (5) public commentary makes internal deliberation visible in real time
