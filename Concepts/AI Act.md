# AI Act

The European Union's **Artificial Intelligence Act** — the world's first comprehensive, horizontal law governing AI. It entered into force on August 1, 2024, and applies on a phased timeline through 2027. It is the EU's principal instrument for projecting regulatory power over AI globally, the AI-era successor to GDPR and a textbook Brussels Effect play (see [[Anu Bradford]]).

## Risk-based structure

The Act regulates AI by risk tier rather than by technology:

| Tier | Treatment |
|------|-----------|
| Unacceptable risk | Prohibited (e.g., social scoring, certain biometric surveillance) |
| High risk | Strict obligations (conformity assessment, documentation, human oversight) |
| Limited risk | Transparency obligations (e.g., disclosure that content is AI-generated) |
| Minimal risk | Largely unregulated |

General-purpose AI (GPAI) models — including large language models — carry their own transparency, copyright, and safety obligations.

## Phased timeline

| Date | What applies |
|------|--------------|
| Aug 1, 2024 | Entry into force |
| Feb 2, 2025 | Prohibited practices banned; AI-literacy duties |
| Aug 2, 2025 | GPAI model obligations begin; GPAI Code of Practice (finalized July 2025) offers a presumption of conformity |
| Aug 2, 2026 | Most high-risk requirements, financial penalties, and Commission enforcement (including GPAI fines) |
| Aug 2, 2027 | AI embedded in regulated products; pre-Aug-2025 GPAI models must comply |

## Geopolitical significance

The AI Act is the clearest current case of the EU setting a global standard through regulation rather than invention. Lacking dominant AI firms, the EU writes the rules that US and Chinese model-makers must follow to sell into Europe — the regulation-led column of the [[Technical Standards]] three-model framework, and the EU's bid in the broader contest over who governs AI.
