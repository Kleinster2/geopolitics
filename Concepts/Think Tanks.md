# Think Tanks

## Overview
Research organizations that conduct policy analysis and seek to influence public discourse and government decisions. Range from academic institutions to advocacy organizations. Central to how geopolitical ideas develop, spread, and influence policy. Understanding their biases and funding is essential for consuming their output.

## What Think Tanks Do

### Functions
1. **Research**: Policy analysis
2. **Convening**: Bringing experts together
3. **Publishing**: Reports, articles, books
4. **Advising**: Government consultation
5. **Media**: Public commentary
6. **Training**: Fellowships, exchanges

### Value
- Long-form analysis
- Expert networks
- Institutional memory
- Policy development
- Revolving door (government)

## Major US Think Tanks

### Council on Foreign Relations (CFR)
- **Founded**: 1921
- **Orientation**: Establishment centrist
- **Publication**: *Foreign Affairs* (influential)
- **Membership**: Invitation-only elite
- **Influence**: Very high

### Brookings Institution
- **Founded**: 1916
- **Orientation**: Center-left
- **Scope**: Domestic + foreign policy
- **Reputation**: Academic rigor
- **Influence**: High

### RAND Corporation
- **Founded**: 1948
- **Origin**: Air Force spinoff
- **Focus**: Defense, national security
- **Method**: Quantitative analysis
- **Influence**: Defense community

### Carnegie Endowment
- **Founded**: 1910
- **Focus**: International affairs
- **Global**: Offices worldwide
- **Orientation**: Liberal internationalist

### Center for Strategic and International Studies (CSIS)
- **Founded**: 1962
- **Focus**: Security, defense
- **Orientation**: Bipartisan
- **Convening**: Major conferences

### Heritage Foundation
- **Founded**: 1973
- **Orientation**: Conservative
- **Influence**: Republican administrations
- **Style**: Advocacy-oriented

### American Enterprise Institute (AEI)
- **Founded**: 1938
- **Orientation**: Conservative
- **Focus**: Economics, foreign policy
- **Influence**: Policy development

### Center for a New American Security (CNAS)
- **Founded**: 2007
- **Focus**: National security
- **Orientation**: Bipartisan (center)
- **Pipeline**: Obama/Biden officials

## How to Read Think Tank Output

### Consider
1. **Funding**: Who pays?
2. **Ideology**: What orientation?
3. **Author**: Background, incentives
4. **Timing**: Policy relevance
5. **Track record**: Past accuracy

### Biases to Note
- Donor influence
- Revolving door
- Ideological priors
- Institutional positions
- Foreign government funding

### Foreign Funding Concerns
- Transparency varies
- Gulf state money
- China-linked funding
- Disclosure debates

## Revolving Door

### How It Works
- Think tank → Government → Think tank
- Policy development
- Network maintenance
- Influence mechanism

### Examples
- NSC staff from think tanks
- Ambassadors returning to think tanks
- Defense officials
- Policy continuity

### Pros/Cons
- **Pro**: Expertise, practical knowledge
- **Con**: Capture, narrow perspectives
