# Iran transnational repression

The pattern of [[Iran]]-orchestrated covert activity directed at dissidents, journalists, former senior US officials, and Israel-linked targets on Western soil. Operationally distinct from the conventional kinetic phase of the [[2026 Iran conflict]]: a longer-running, lower-amplitude channel that operates through assassination, kidnapping, intimidation, surveillance, sabotage, and recruitment of foreign criminal networks. The investing dimension — political-risk premia, executive-protection markets, insurance-withdrawal patterns — is treated separately at `obsidian://open?vault=investing&file=Concepts%2FIran%20transnational%20repression`. This note is the geopolitics-primary lens: institutional structure, named operations, attribution chains, doctrinal context.

## Overview

Two structural facts shape the channel. First, transnational repression is not a contingent feature of the post-war environment: it has been continuous Islamic Republic policy since 1979, with operational tempo that varies by political phase but never goes to zero. Second, Western counter-intelligence services treat it as an active and *expanding* threat into 2025-26 — UK MI5 director [[Ken McCallum]] (October 2025 public address) tracked more than twenty potentially lethal Iran-backed plots in the UK during 2025, the highest annual count on public record. The post-war state question is whether the March 2026 strikes that killed senior Iranian command and intelligence figures *delay* or *eliminate* this channel.

Per FT-quoted experts (John Raine of [[IISS]]; Jonathan Hackett, former US Marine SOF), the consensus is *delay only*. The Soleimani precedent is the load-bearing analogy: when Qassem Soleimani was killed in January 2020, [[Quds Force]] external-operations capacity was demonstrably constrained for months while a new operational network re-formed under Esmail Qaani; it did not disappear. The 2026 senior decapitation has the same shape. John Raine's framing — "revenge is as much a religious obligation as an emotional response" — implies a multi-quarter, possibly multi-year horizon.

## Operational structure

Western intelligence (Israeli Mossad terminology, adopted by FT and other Western reporting) divides Iranian transnational special operations into two main organisations:

| Unit | Parent | Purview | Operational since | Notable public attributions |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Unit 400 | [[IRGC]] Intelligence | Iranian targets only — dissidents, exiles, journalists | Long-running; pre-dates 2010s reorganisation | UK / Europe arson and intimidation campaigns; Iran International journalist targeting |
| Unit 840 | [[Quds Force]] Special Operations Division | Foreign nationals — Israeli diplomats, intelligence officers, businesspeople; Western political figures | 2012 (Mossad dating) | Cyprus, Ethiopia, Kenya, UAE, Colombia (Israeli diplomats / intelligence officers); Ghana, Tanzania, Senegal (Israeli businesspeople) |

The doctrinal distinction is consequential. Unit 400 operations are formally framed as state security against perceived domestic opponents (extra-territorial dissident targeting); Unit 840 operations are formally framed as foreign-policy retaliation. The legal posture for the targeted state and the diplomatic posture for the state-of-residence differ accordingly. In practice, both are conducted under the same operational doctrine and frequently use the same supporting infrastructure (recruited criminal networks, false-flag cover, Iranian embassy logistical support).

## Recurring operational patterns

Iranian transnational operations across the 2018-2026 window display recurring features:

- *Recruitment of foreign criminal networks.* Operations against dissidents and former officials have repeatedly been outsourced through Eastern European and Middle Eastern criminal organisations, providing operational distance from official Iranian institutions. The 2022 Brian Hook plot used Iran-linked recruitment of a Russian-network operative; the 2024 indictment of Shahram Poursafi (alleged IRGC) for the Bolton plot followed the same pattern, with a $300,000 contract offer and a $1 million completion bonus per the FBI affidavit (US Treasury / DoJ have placed a $20 million standing reward on Poursafi).
- *Diplomatic-mission overlap.* Iranian embassies abroad have repeatedly featured in operational chains as logistical or cover infrastructure. The mid-April 2026 [[Iran|Iranian]] embassy London Telegram channel "sacrifice for the homeland" recruitment campaign (which prompted a formal UK demarche) is the most recent public episode; UK and EU services have publicly characterised this as a continuation of long-running practice.
- *Multi-target, multi-phase plots.* The Masih Alinejad case (Iranian-American journalist; multiple kidnap attempts on US soil 2018-2022) and the Pouria Zeraati case (Iran International journalist stabbed in Wimbledon, March 2024) both featured multi-phase planning, multiple operational vectors, and persistence after early disruption.
- *Continuity through ceasefire phases.* The post-Apr-12 [[2026 Iran conflict|conflict]] ceasefire produced a public reduction in conventional military exchanges but no documented reduction in covert tempo; the April 2026 embassy-London Telegram episode and the Western arson campaigns provisionally attributed to Iran fall within the ceasefire window.

## Post-war capacity constraints

The March 2026 [[Israel|Israeli]] strikes that killed Supreme Leader [[Ali Khamenei]] also killed two figures who would have coordinated large transnational operations:

- *Ali Larijani*, head of the Supreme National Security Council. Larijani's operational influence ran through both diplomatic and intelligence chains; his death removes an interlocutor with the institutional history to coordinate cross-agency external operations.
- *Esmail Khatib*, intelligence minister (MOIS). Khatib's death removes the lead institutional figure responsible for non-IRGC intelligence operations abroad.

The [[Quds Force]] external chain is technically separate (under the IRGC, not the MOIS), but historically these chains have coordinated, and combined senior loss raises the practical coordination cost of any large operation.

The Soleimani 2020 precedent informs the timeline. Public reporting and post-action analysis indicate that:
- Quds Force Special Operations Division (Unit 840) tempo was visibly reduced for 6-12 months after January 2020;
- A new operational network re-formed under Qaani by mid-2021;
- High-amplitude attacks (Brian Hook 2022, Bolton 2024) resumed within 2-4 years of the original constraint event.

By analogy, the March 2026 senior decapitation produces a delay of months-to-quarters, not years. The full reconstitution may take longer if the post-conflict succession itself is contested.

## Cross-targeting: former senior US officials

A persistent feature of the 2018-2026 window is Iranian targeting of former senior US officials directly involved in the [[Trump I|first Trump administration]]'s [[2018 JCPOA withdrawal|2018 JCPOA withdrawal]] and the January 2020 Soleimani strike. Public US protective-intelligence baseline (per FBI and DSS public reporting) covers:

| Former official | Role at time of plot | Plot disposition |
|---|---|---|
| John Bolton | National Security Advisor (2018-2019); architect of "maximum pressure" | August 2022 indictment of Shahram Poursafi (alleged IRGC); $300k offer, $1mn completion; $20mn US standing reward |
| Mike Pompeo | Secretary of State (2018-2021) | Continuing 24-hour DSS / private security baseline; multiple disrupted plots reported |
| Brian Hook | Special Representative for Iran (2018-2020) | 2022 plot disrupted; FBI public statement |
| Robert O'Brien | National Security Advisor (2019-2021) | Reported as protected category |
| Other [[2018 JCPOA withdrawal|JCPOA withdrawal]] - era principals | Various | Multiple lower-profile disruptions in public record |

The pattern is durable enough that "Iran threat" has been a standing US Diplomatic Security and FBI category since 2020. The [[2026 Iran conflict|2026 conflict]] adds operational urgency to the standing posture but does not create the category.

## Counter-intelligence response

Western services are increasingly public about attribution:

| Service | Public posture (2024-26) |
|---|---|
| US FBI | Standing FTO designation for IRGC (2019); Iran-threat advisory updates 2024-26 |
| UK MI5 | [[Ken McCallum]] October 2025 address: >20 potentially lethal plots tracked in 2025; Iran framed as joint top-tier threat alongside Russia |
| Israeli Mossad | Routine public attribution of Unit 400 / Unit 840 operations |
| EU INTCEN / member-state services | Coordinated demarches; April 2026 UK demarche on London embassy Telegram episode |
| Five Eyes | Coordinated intelligence sharing on Iranian recruitment networks |

Public attribution serves a deterrent function — it raises the diplomatic and operational cost of subsequent attempts — but does not substitute for the underlying counter-intelligence work.

## Strategic implications

Three implications shape Western policy posture into the post-war phase:

- *No expectation of channel closure.* Even with senior decapitation, Western services are operating under the assumption of continued covert tempo into 2027-28 minimum. Counter-intelligence resourcing remains at conflict-period elevation, not pre-conflict baseline.
- *Diplomatic-mission status as ongoing tension point.* The April 2026 UK demarche on the London Iranian embassy Telegram episode signals continued active Western pressure on diplomatic-mission operational use; persistence of this practice is a recurring driver of expulsions and embassy-status downgrades.
- *Diaspora-protection as an institutional commitment.* The Alinejad and Zeraati cases have established a Western institutional commitment to diaspora-target protection that operates independently of bilateral conflict tempo. This commitment carries staffing and political costs and is unlikely to be reversed regardless of post-conflict détente.
