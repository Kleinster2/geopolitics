# Constructive strategic stability

## Definition

Constructive strategic stability is the May 2026 Chinese framing for the post-summit US-China relationship. It presents the bilateral relationship as stable enough to avoid great-power conflict, constructive enough to expand cooperation, and strategic enough to manage competition across trade, technology, Taiwan, regional crises, and global governance.

The phrase is not just generic "stability." In the Chinese account, it is a positioning formula for the next several years of US-China relations after the May 13-15, 2026 Trump-Xi state visit to Beijing.

## Formula

Gao Fei's People's Daily article, translated and framed by [[Manoj Kewalramani]] in Tracking People's Daily, gives the internal structure of the phrase:

| Component | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| Positive stability | Cooperation remains the mainstay |
| Benign stability | Competition stays within limits |
| Normalized stability | Differences are controllable |
| Durable stability | Peace remains foreseeable |

The implied bargain is managed competition rather than partnership. Beijing is not saying structural contradictions disappear. It is saying they should be bounded by summit-level political guidance, dialogue channels, and issue-specific councils.

## Why Beijing is using it now

The important shift is rhetorical direction. In 2022-2023, Washington was the side emphasizing guardrails. By May 2026, Beijing is the side using the language of boundary management, constructive competition, and strategic stability. Kewalramani's read is that Beijing now sees the United States as declining but still dangerous, so prudence is useful even after China believes it has resisted US pressure.

Gao's article makes the power claim explicit: dialogue, in this telling, follows from China having pushed back against containment and suppression. That is not concession language. It is victory-with-restraint language: Beijing presents talks as the result of Chinese capability, while also arguing that conflict would damage both economies and global supply chains.

## Policy channels

The source names practical channels from the May 2026 summit:

| Channel | Function |
|---------|----------|
| Trade Council | Bilateral trade management |
| Investment Council | Investment access and dispute-management channel |
| Agricultural market access | Specific tariff / market-access concerns |
| Reciprocal tariff reductions | Mechanism for expanding two-way trade |
| Regional crisis talks | Middle East, Ukraine, Korean Peninsula |
| Global challenge cooperation | Climate, public health, AI, supply chains, global economic order |

The stabilizing claim is broad, but the test is narrow: whether these channels keep working when Taiwan, sanctions, export controls, Iran-war aftershocks, or domestic politics force either side to reprice the relationship.

## New York follow-through

The May 27-29 New York sequence shows Beijing trying to connect bilateral stabilization with its broader order campaign. In the official Foreign Ministry readout, [[Wang Yi]] told US strategic and business representatives that reviving the UN requires Security Council coordination, and that Security Council coordination depends heavily on the direction of China-US relations. The same trip also included the Group of Friends of Global Governance meeting, where Wang outlined nine reform directions ranging from Security Council authority and peacekeeping to AI, cyber, outer space, human rights and economic-financial governance.

This matters because constructive strategic stability is not only a bilateral guardrail formula. Beijing is using it as the great-power-management layer beneath a wider global-governance project. The US-China relationship is framed as the stabilizing hinge; the desired output is a UN-centered order less tolerant of unilateral sanctions, selective human-rights pressure, and US-led coalition rule-setting.

## Relation to older Chinese frameworks

Constructive strategic stability revives part of the older "new type of major-country relationship" language that Xi Jinping proposed to Barack Obama at Sunnylands in 2013. The US side resisted that phrase because it was read as asking Washington to recognize a Chinese sphere of influence or equal major-power status on Beijing's terms.

In the 2026 version, the older destination reappears through a more modest pathway. "Constructive strategic stability" is the road; a new type of major-country relationship is the horizon. The concept also points back to [[Community with a Shared Future]], because Gao closes the argument by tying US-China coexistence to a more "just and rational" global-governance order and the shared-future formula.

## What to watch

- Whether Wang Yi / Foreign Ministry messaging keeps repeating the phrase after the May summit cycle.
- Whether the Trade Council and Investment Council produce concrete tariff, agriculture, or market-access steps.
- Whether Taiwan language hardens even as economic language softens.
- Whether AI appears as cooperation, strategic-risk management, or competitive control in follow-up meetings.
- Whether US officials adopt the phrase or leave it as a Chinese-side formula.
