# Little Giants Program (小巨人)

China's state-run program to certify and support small and medium-sized manufacturers in high-tech sectors. Over 17,000 firms hold the designation. The program bundles capital, R&D, regulatory, and supply chain support into a single certification — an architecture the United States has no equivalent for.

## Program Structure

### Origins

| Year | Milestone |
|------|-----------|
| 2011 | "Specialized, Refined, Unique, Innovative" (专精特新) program launched for SME support |
| 2015 | [[Made in China 2025]] — industrial policy framework targeting Western technological parity |
| 2018 | MIIT upgraded to "Little Giants" (小巨人) initiative — elite tier within 专精特新 |

### What the Certification Delivers

The Little Giants designation unlocks a coordinated package:

- **Preferential loans and direct subsidies** — below-market financing
- **State equity investment** — government takes stakes in certified firms
- **University research partnerships** — structured R&D collaboration
- **Fast-tracked patent processing** — reduced IP timelines
- **Guaranteed integration into SOE supply chains** — demand certainty from state-owned enterprises
- **Streamlined stock market listings** — faster path to public capital

The critical design feature: these supports are **bundled and coordinated**, not delivered through separate, unconnected programs. The certification itself functions as a state credibility signal that unlocks private capital.

### Scale

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Firms certified | **17,000+** |
| In high-tech manufacturing | ~90% |
| Private capital raised | **$125B+** (with state credentialing as catalyst) |
| Example firm | [[Unitree Robotics]] (humanoid robotics) |

## US Comparison

Rush Doshi (former Biden NSC China director, now Georgetown) testified before the House Small Business Committee in April 2026. His core argument: the US has individual tools that could approximate China's approach, but no architecture to deploy them together.

| Dimension | China | United States |
|-----------|-------|---------------|
| Certification program | Little Giants (17,000 firms) | Nothing equivalent |
| Private capital unlocked | $125B+ | SBIC Critical Tech: ~$4B |
| Annual industrial policy spend | ~$400B/year | CHIPS Act: $50B over multiple years |
| Technical assistance | University partnerships at scale | MEP — just defunded |
| Early-stage R&D | State research institutes | SBIR/STTR — programs lapsed |
| Manufacturer credit cap | Scaled to need | $5M ceiling |

### Manufacturing Share Inversion

Over approximately 25 years:

| Country | 2000 | 2025 | Change |
|---------|------|------|--------|
| United States | ~30% global manufacturing | ~15% | Halved |
| [[China]] | ~6% | ~30% | Quintupled |

China now exceeds the next 9 countries combined in manufacturing output.

## US Policy Gap

Doshi's diagnosis: the US is not just behind — it is actively dismantling the closest equivalents to China's program:

1. **SBIR/STTR** (early-stage small business research): lapsed mid-competition with China
2. **Manufacturing Extension Partnership (MEP)**: defunded — was the closest US equivalent to China's university technical assistance
3. **SBIC Critical Technologies Initiative**: $4B is a non-serious answer to $125B
4. **SBA Manufacturer's Credit**: $5M cap inadequate for global-scale manufacturing
5. **No coordination architecture**: SBA has loans, equity vehicles, R&D programs — but runs them in isolation

### Doshi's Prescriptions

1. Reauthorize SBIR/STTR immediately
2. Create an American one-stop-shop certification (SBA-led, modeled on Little Giants bundling)
3. Scale SBIC Critical Technologies to $125B+ range
4. Raise manufacturer credit caps beyond $5M
5. Restore MEP funding
6. Give SBA mandate to coordinate all tools together

## Strategic Significance

The Little Giants program reflects a core CCP insight: small manufacturers are the connective tissue of industrial ecosystems. 70% of Boeing's Dreamliner comes from smaller suppliers. 60% of US aerospace/defense employment is in SMEs. China views these firms as the path to replacing foreign suppliers, eliminating supply chain chokepoints, and building globally competitive companies from the bottom up.

The program is part of the broader [[Made in China 2025]] framework and connects to China's approach to [[Technology Competition]] — industrial dominance built through systematic institutional support rather than individual firm champions.
