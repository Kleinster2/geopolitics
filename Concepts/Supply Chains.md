# Supply Chains (Geopolitics of)

## Overview
The networks that produce and deliver goods have become a central arena of geopolitical competition. COVID-19 and US-China tensions revealed dangerous dependencies. "Supply chain security" is now a national security priority.

## Why Supply Chains Are Geopolitical

### Vulnerabilities Exposed
- **COVID-19**: PPE shortages, vaccine production
- **Chip shortage**: Auto industry paralysis
- **Ever Given**: Suez blockage impact
- **Ukraine war**: Food, energy, neon gas

### Strategic Dependencies
- Single points of failure
- Adversary control of critical inputs
- Weaponization potential
- Resilience vs. efficiency trade-off

## Key Concepts

### Chokepoints
Concentrated control points in supply chains:
- Geographic (Suez, Malacca)
- Production (Taiwan chips)
- Processing (China rare earths)
- Technology (ASML lithography)

### Fragility vs. Resilience
| Efficient (fragile) | Resilient (costly) |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| Just-in-time | Just-in-case |
| Single source | Multi-source |
| Lowest cost | Secure supply |
| Global optimization | Regional buffers |

### Friend-shoring
Relocating supply chains to allied countries:
- Trusted partners
- Values-aligned
- Reduces adversary leverage
- Trade-off: Less efficient

## Critical Supply Chains

### Semiconductors
**The most critical supply chain**

| Stage | Dominant Player |
|-------|-----------------|
| Design | US (Nvidia, AMD, Qualcomm) |
| Manufacturing (advanced) | Taiwan (TSMC 90%+) |
| Equipment | Netherlands (ASML monopoly) |
| Memory | South Korea (Samsung, SK) |
| Assembly/Test | China, Southeast Asia |

**Vulnerabilities:**
- Taiwan concentration (China threat)
- Single equipment supplier
- Long lead times
- Massive capital requirements

**Responses:**
- CHIPS Act (US)
- EU Chips Act
- Fab construction (Arizona, Ohio, Germany)
- Export controls on China

### Critical Minerals
See: [[Critical Minerals]]
- Rare earths: China 60% mining, 90% processing
- Lithium: Australia, Chile, China processing
- Cobalt: DRC 70% mining, China processing
- Tantalum: Rubaya in eastern DRC produces around 15% of world coltan/tantalum and has been under AFC/M23 rebel control since 2024, turning mineral security into conflict diplomacy

### Pharmaceuticals
- Active ingredients: China, India dominant
- Generic drugs: India
- Medical devices: Global
- Revealed by COVID-19

### Energy
- Oil: OPEC, Russia
- Natural gas: Pipeline dependencies
- LNG: New flexibility
- Renewables: China manufacturing

## US/Western Response

### Reshoring
Bringing production home:
- CHIPS Act subsidies
- IRA manufacturing incentives
- "Made in America"
- Costly but strategic

### Friend-shoring
Diversifying to allies:
- Mexico (nearshoring)
- India (China alternative)
- Vietnam (manufacturing shift)
- "China+1" strategy

### Stockpiling
Building reserves:
- Strategic petroleum reserve
- Medical supplies
- Critical minerals
- Chips (proposed)

### Export Controls
Denying adversaries:
- Semiconductor equipment
- Advanced chips
- AI technology
- "Small yard, high fence"

### Pax Silica

[[Pax Silica]] is the coalition version of supply-chain security. Instead of only subsidizing domestic production or blocking exports to China, it tries to coordinate trusted partners across the full technology stack: minerals, energy, manufacturing, semiconductors, AI infrastructure, and logistics.

The geopolitical shift is supplier qualification. A company can be exposed even when it is not directly sanctioned if its sub-tier inputs remain anchored in China and it cannot document trusted alternatives. The May 2026 commercial-space read-through shows how this logic can reach RF systems, printed circuit boards, power electronics, sensors, composites, precision structures, and rare-earth magnets before a formal export-control rule appears.

### Strategic infrastructure as supply-chain control

The May 2026 EU-China debate highlights a wider definition of supply-chain security. Control is not only at the factory or refinery. It can sit in ports, telecom towers, cable networks, data centers, municipal bus fleets, and the data generated by those systems.

This is why European China policy is moving beyond product tariffs. If the infrastructure carrying goods, data, passengers, or power is controlled by a strategic competitor, trade dependency becomes operational dependency. The policy toolkit then shifts toward procurement rules, investment screening, ownership caps, anti-circumvention rules, and sector-specific security reviews.

## Trade-offs

### Efficiency vs. Security
```
              Security
                 ↑
                 │    Autarky
                 │    (North Korea)
                 │
                 │         Friend-shoring
                 │         (current trend)
                 │
                 │              Pre-2020
                 │              Globalization
                 └───────────────────────→ Efficiency
```

### Costs of Reshoring
- Higher prices
- Less specialization
- Slower growth
- Trade fragmentation

### Costs of Not Reshoring
- Strategic vulnerability
- Coercion risk
- Crisis fragility
- Adversary leverage

## Future Trends

2. **Redundancy**: Multiple suppliers standard
3. **Visibility**: Supply chain mapping
4. **Stockpiles**: Strategic reserves expand
5. **Industrial policy**: States shape production
6. **Technology**: Automation enables reshoring
