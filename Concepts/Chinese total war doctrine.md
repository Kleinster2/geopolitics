# Chinese Total War Doctrine

## Overview
Chinese "total war" as discussed in recent CCP/PLA writing is not the classic English-language idea of scorched earth, unconditional surrender, or unlimited destruction. Howard Wang's 2026 RAND-linked study defines it as national mobilization to develop civilian institutional advantages in peacetime that can be converted into warfighting capabilities in conflict.

## Core Logic

The doctrine starts from a PLA view that future war is systems confrontation. A strong enemy such as the [[United States]] does not fight only with military units. It fights with finance, alliances, sanctions, intelligence disclosure, commercial space, cyber operations, information campaigns, industrial capacity, and domestic political cohesion.

China cannot copy the US model directly. It lacks US alliance density, dollar-clearing dominance, global financial chokepoints, and the same strategic-messaging reach. The compensating asset is party control: Beijing believes the CCP can operate the country as "one chessboard" and direct civilian systems in ways market democracies cannot.

## Civilian Systems as Combat Effects

Total war directs civilian institutions to keep developing normally in peacetime while also identifying how their capabilities can become wartime effects. The relevant civilian systems include:
- financial networks and sanctions-evasion channels
- commercial satellite communications and ISR
- cyber and electronic-warfare capacity
- social-media and cognitive-domain operations
- industrial production and logistics
- food, energy, and strategic stockpiles

The point is sequencing and harmonization: civilian effects should be synchronized with PLA effects to disrupt the adversary's system of systems.

## Ukraine Lessons

PLA analysts read the Russia-Ukraine war as a case in which the United States supplemented Ukraine's war system with civilian systems. Their examples include sanctions, Starlink, public intelligence declassification, strategic messaging, cyber capability, and coalition management. Whether the PLA overreads US coordination is less important than the doctrine it extracts: the "strong enemy" uses civilian systems to increase lethality and disrupt decision cycles.

## Priority Sectors

Wang identified China SatNet as the clearest practical example. Created in 2021, China SatNet is a state-owned vehicle tasked with a proliferated low-earth-orbit satellite communications constellation similar to Starlink. Officially it is civilian broadband infrastructure; PLA writing discusses how such a constellation could suppress Starlink, support resilient ISR, and become useful in war.

The weaker example is cryptocurrency: some Chinese writing imagines blockchain channels as sanctions-evasion infrastructure for wartime financing. Wang treats this as conceptually revealing but technically less convincing.
