# China soft power

The cultural-attraction dimension of [[China]]'s rising power, distinct from its hard-power instruments ([[PLA]], rare-earth and processing chokepoints) and its economic instruments ([[Belt and Road]], [[China-ASEAN FTA]], state-bank lending). The framing originates with Joseph Nye (1990): the ability to shape preferences through cultural appeal, ideological resonance, and institutional legitimacy rather than payment or coercion. For most of the post-1978 reform period, modern Chinese soft power was widely treated as elusive — Japan had manga, South Korea had K-pop, the US had Hollywood, and China seemed structurally constrained from generating comparable cultural output by one-party limits on civil society. By 2025-26, that diagnosis is incomplete: a measurable Chinese soft-power surface has emerged, propagating through algorithmic platforms, open-source frontier technology, and consumer goods rather than through state-directed cultural production. The concurrent collapse of US soft-power capital under [[Trump II]] policy choices means the bilateral balance is shifting more clearly in this dimension than in any other.

## Overview

The 2026 inflection has two structural drivers, neither of which fits the older "Beijing must produce its own Hollywood" expectation.

First, *algorithmic platforms generate cultural reach without a state-directed narrative*. [[TikTok]]'s recommendation engine — not the [[Cyberspace Administration of China]], not [[Xi Jinping Thought]] propaganda — is what decides what reaches global audiences at scale. The platform-as-infrastructure vector means Chinese soft-power output looks exactly like organic cultural exchange and is harder to discount as state messaging. Examples that surface in current Western reporting: the Chongqing cyberpunk-aesthetic meme cluster; the "very Chinese time" labour-discipline humour wave; user-generated reaction-video coverage of Wu Yize and Zhao Xintong's rise as world snooker champions.

Second, *open-source release of frontier-quality technology* generates global developer and adopter pull. [[DeepSeek]]'s open-weight large language models, released free for any developer to download and run, bypass both US export-control architecture (which targets compute, not weights) and Western platform capture (no Anthropic/OpenAI API dependency). The mechanism is reciprocal credibility: open weights demonstrate transparency to developers, while developer adoption embeds Chinese AI defaults across every vertical that uses LLMs.

Both vectors share a property that earlier expectations missed: they are *state-tolerated* rather than *state-directed*, and that distinction is what avoids the credibility deficit of explicit CCP propaganda.

## US soft-power inventory drawdown

The other half of the story is US-side. Trump-era policy choices are running down decades of accumulated US soft-power capital faster than China is building its own:

| Mechanism | Recent manifestation | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Tariff escalation against allies | 25% EU vehicle tariff threat (May 2026); Section 232 extensions | Collapses the historical asymmetry where allies were tariffed less than rivals |
| Personal-image collapse of US ambassadors | [[Elon Musk]]–[[Donald Trump]] alignment; Tesla European sales decline 2025-26 | Brand-association discount on US flag-ship companies in markets where consumer politics matter |
| Operational-trust degradation | EU officials reportedly carry burner phones to US visits (FT, May 2026) | Treatment historically reserved for high-surveillance environments |
| Alienation of swing states | Trump tariff escalation against [[India]]; public praise for [[Pakistan]]; "hellhole" reference | March 2026 Indian fast-track for minority-Chinese tech investments — first quantitative reversal of post-Galwan India-China capital closure |
| USAID and public-diplomacy retreat | 2025 USAID dismantlement | Removes longstanding development-assistance soft-power instrument |
| Visa-restrictiveness | Tightened student / business visa regimes 2025-26 | Reduces inbound flow of foreign students/researchers — a historical pillar of US soft-power transmission |

The pattern is that US soft-power assets do not require explicit Chinese countermoves to erode. Each US action — a tariff, a visa restriction, a public alienation of an ally — directly creates the comparative advantage Beijing exploits.

These are *opinion* signals, not behavioural signals. But they are leading indicators for the structural decisions — tech-stack adoption, supplier diversification, security alignment, multilateral-vote alignment — that *do* express into behaviour over 5-10 year contracts and policy choices.

## What is and is not yet soft power

The 2020s shift is real but bounded. Three things have *not* happened, and the constraint is both genuine and the opening for US recovery if conditions change:

1. *No K-pop / Hollywood equivalent.* China still lacks a globally dominant entertainment-content franchise. [[TikTok]] is a *platform*, not a content production engine; the cultural memes reaching global audiences are user-generated, not exports of Chinese cultural production. Hollywood-equivalent box-office reach for Chinese cinema has not materialised, and *Wandering Earth* / *Nezha* remain domestic-market phenomena rather than global ones.
2. *No diaspora-driven loyalty premium.* Chinese diaspora communities in the West remain politically wary of mainland positioning, in contrast to the cultural diplomacy that Korean, Japanese, and Indian diasporas exercise in Western markets. Lunar New Year cultural events lack the political salience of Diwali receptions or K-pop festivals.
3. *Trust gap on sensitive infrastructure.* The original US argument against [[Huawei]] 5G — that adopting Chinese network technology exposes adopters to surveillance and economic-coercion risk — has not lost intellectual force. What has happened is that US conduct has degraded the credibility of *the messenger*, not the underlying message. A sovereign weighing Huawei vs. Western 5G in 2026 may now perceive the security trade-offs as more symmetric, not as more favourable to China.

## Strategic implications

The soft-power dimension is the area where the US-[[China]] competitive balance shifts most clearly toward Beijing in 2025-26 — not because Chinese soft-power generation has accelerated dramatically, but because US soft-power inventory is being drawn down faster than it is being replenished. This has three downstream consequences for strategy:

- *Alliance management.* Atlantic-ally polling shifts compound through 5-10 year decisions: defence-procurement, semiconductor-fab siting, AI-infrastructure dependency. The sequence makes the [[Alliance Credibility Crisis]] and soft-power drawdown structurally linked rather than independent.
- *Swing-state alignment.* India is the leading example. [[Narendra Modi]] government's March 2026 Chinese-minority-investment fast-track is the first quantitative reversal of the post-Galwan India-China capital closure. Other swing states ([[ASEAN]] members, Gulf, Africa) face similar pressures and have lower reversal costs than India.
- *Asymmetry with [[China retaliatory toolkit]].* Beijing is running both *attraction* and *coercion* in parallel — the soft-power surface alongside rare-earth export-control instruments and dysprosium / [[Antimony]] licensing. Both reshape the cost of US confrontation: punishment side raises the cost; attraction side raises the value of Chinese partnership.
