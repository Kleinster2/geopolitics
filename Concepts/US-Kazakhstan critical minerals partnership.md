# US-Kazakhstan critical minerals partnership

The US-Kazakhstan critical minerals partnership is the bilateral arm of the [[Trump|second Trump administration]]'s strategy to source defense-relevant minerals from non-China-aligned jurisdictions. Its keystone is the [[Tungsten]] joint venture between the US private mining company [[Cove Kaz Capital]] (70%) and Kazakhstan's state mining company [[Tau-Ken Samruk]] (30%) on the Northern Katpar and Upper Kairakty deposits in central [[Kazakhstan]]. By May 2026 the project carries up to $1.6 billion in concurrent US federal letters of interest from the [[EXIM Bank]] ($900M) and [[DFC]] ($700M), making it the largest single critical-minerals federal-financing commitment of the second term outside the rare-earths cluster.

The partnership is the bilateral instantiation of the [[C5+1]] Critical Minerals Dialogue inaugurated May 22, 2025 and elevated to head-of-state level at the November 6, 2025 C5+1 Presidential Summit in Washington — the first such summit. Per *The Diplomat*, the Trump administration's preferred operating mode is "five bilateral C1+1 relationships" rather than continued multilateral C5+1 engagement; the Kazakhstan tungsten deal is the first high-profile test of that bilateral pivot.

## Structural logic

[[Tungsten]]'s strategic relevance follows the same pattern as the broader [[Critical Minerals]] dependency map but with sharper concentration:
- [[China]] produces ~83% of mined tungsten globally (per 2024 USGS data)
- The US has no commercial-scale tungsten mining; domestic supply has historically come from secondary recovery, recycling, and small specialty operations
- Tungsten carbide is the core material for armor-piercing kinetic-energy ammunition, mining drill bits, machine-tool inserts, and high-temperature defense components
- Tungsten was on the USGS critical minerals list at the start of the second Trump term and is a target of the [[Project Vault]] strategic stockpiling program ($10B EXIM commitment)

The Northern Katpar and Upper Kairakty deposits — originally Soviet-explored and held by [[Tau-Ken Samruk]] — are described by their sponsors as the world's largest known undeveloped tungsten resource. Combined planned production is ~12,000 metric tonnes per annum (~15% of current global output) at full ramp.

## Joint-venture structure

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Operating entity | Severniy Katpar LLP (registered in [[Kazakhstan]]) |
| Equity split | [[Cove Kaz Capital]] 70% / [[Tau-Ken Samruk]] 30% |
| Definitive JV agreements executed | February 13, 2026 |
| Northern Katpar planned production | ~5,000 mtpa |
| Upper Kairakty planned production | ~7,000 mtpa |
| Total development cost | ~$1.1 billion |

[[Tau-Ken Samruk]] is a subsidiary of the Kazakh sovereign wealth fund [[Samruk-Kazyna]]. The 30% / 70% split — Kazakh state minority, Western-led majority and operatorship — matches the broader Kazakh pattern of inviting Western JV partners into resource-development projects while retaining state equity participation.

## US federal financing

| Agency | Instrument | Up-to amount | Issued |
|--------|------------|--------------|--------|
| [[EXIM Bank]] | Letter of interest | $900M | Concurrent with merger announcement, April 30, 2026 |
| [[DFC]] | Letter of interest | $700M | Concurrent with merger announcement, April 30, 2026 |

Letters of interest are non-binding and do not commit the agencies to fund — they signal eligibility for further diligence and willingness to consider commitment. The combined $1.6bn ceiling sits above the ~$1.1bn estimated development cost, providing comfortable coverage if diligence converts.

The EXIM letter sits alongside the [[Project Vault]] $10B EXIM commitment for critical-minerals stockpiling. The second-Trump-term EXIM has been the primary federal vehicle for critical-minerals project finance, complementing the IRA-era processing incentives the prior administration used.

## US capital-markets vehicle

The US-listed vehicle for the JV is the reverse merger of [[Cove Kaz Capital]] into [[Skyline Builders]] (Nasdaq: SKBL) — a Hong Kong-domiciled construction shell — announced April 30, 2026. The [[Sponsored reverse merger pipeline]] mechanic is documented in the investing-vault concept note of the same name; the geopolitics-relevant facts are:

1. The pipeline operator ([[Dominari Holdings]] / [[American Ventures]]) is the same broker-dealer / SPV-factory pair that [[Donald Trump Jr.]] and [[Eric Trump]] anchor as ~12.56% combined Dominari shareholders and as anchor LPs in the Series XIX SKBL and Series XXXII SKBL II vehicles.
2. The federal LoIs were issued the same day as the merger announcement.
3. The listed shell ([[Skyline Builders]]) was named in the [[House Select Committee on China]]'s March 8, 2026 letter requesting information on Dominari-underwritten Chinese small-cap IPOs implicated in possible ramp-and-dump activity.

The structural observation, per the *Financial Times* (George Steer, May 8, 2026): the federal-policy event landed on the same day as the merger announcement. There is no public suggestion the Trump brothers had advance knowledge of or influence on the EXIM/DFC issuance.

## Strategic context — counter-China supply chain

The partnership sits inside three converging policy frames:

| Frame | Mechanism | Relevance to Cove Kaz |
|-------|-----------|------------------------|
| [[Critical Minerals]] sourcing diversification | USGS list, IRA processing incentives, DFC equity, EXIM project finance | Direct: tungsten on USGS list, EXIM/DFC funded |
| [[C5+1]] / Central Asia bilateralization | Inaugural C5+1 minerals dialogue (May 2025), Presidential Summit (Nov 2025), Trump invitation of Kazakhstan to 2026 G20 | Direct: first major bilateral deal post-summit |
| [[Project Vault]] strategic stockpiling | $10B EXIM ceiling, off-take guarantees | Indirect: tungsten is a covered mineral |

Per [[Carnegie]] (January 2025) and [[CSIS]] (Ten Years of C5+1 Minerals Cooperation), [[Kazakhstan]] holds reserves of 21 of 34 EU-listed critical raw materials — including the world's largest chromium reserves, plus titanium, silver, lead, copper, and uranium. Kazakhstan supplies ~24% of US reactor uranium and ~21% of global primary uranium output through [[Kazatomprom]]. The tungsten JV is therefore the leading edge of a much wider US-Kazakh minerals relationship.

The Kazakhstan deal is the largest single-project commitment so far in the federal-financing critical-minerals book outside the rare-earths cluster. The closest structural analogue is the Australia rare-earths book, which has been built up over multiple Lynas, Iluka, and Australian Strategic Materials deals; Kazakhstan reaches comparable scale in a single transaction.

## Russia and China overlay

Kazakhstan's broader strategic position — see [[Central Asia Economic Boom]] — sits between three powers:
- [[Russia]] (declining; CSTO base in [[Kazakhstan]] retained but influence eroding since February 2022)
- [[China]] (rising; $23B in Kazakh investment in H1 2025 alone, $12B aluminium complex, $7.5B in copper; trade deficit with China quadrupled to $1.8B in H1 2025)
- US / EU (rising; EU Global Gateway €12B Central Asia package; US C5+1 Critical Minerals Dialogue)

The tungsten deal does not displace China — Chinese Kazakh mineral investment remains larger in aggregate — but it represents the first US position of comparable single-deal magnitude in the Kazakh mining stack. The political calculus from Astana is the diversification logic [[Oliver Stuenkel]] frames for [[Brazil]]: refusal to swap one dependency for another. Kazakhstan can take Western capital on tungsten while continuing Chinese capital on aluminium and copper.

## Risks and open questions

| Risk | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Diligence-to-commitment conversion | EXIM and DFC letters of interest are non-binding; no comparable LoI has yet converted to a full commitment under the second-term EXIM at this scale |
| Operator concentration | [[Cove Kaz Capital]] has no current production; the pipeline operator ([[Dominari Holdings]] / [[American Ventures]]) carries broker-dealer reputational issues from the [[House Select Committee on China]] letter |
| Geological execution | The 12,000 mtpa target is a step-change in global supply from a single new operating site; ramp timing and capex containment are unverified |
| Russian transit | Kazakh exports to Western markets historically transited Russia; the [[Middle Corridor]] (Trans-Caspian) is the alternative route but capacity is constrained |
| Chinese counter-response | China holds processing capacity even where it does not hold mining capacity; tungsten concentrate from Kazakhstan may still need to transit Chinese refining if non-China processing capacity is not built in parallel |
| Kazakh political risk | Tokayev-era reforms assessed by [[Yale Journal of International Affairs]] / [[Foreign Policy Centre]] as largely symbolic; concentration of executive authority creates discontinuity risk |

The final risk — processing dependency — is the structural analogue of the rare-earths trap. Mining diversification without processing diversification merely changes the geography of the upstream dependency, not the bottleneck. No public US tungsten processing facility is currently in advanced permitting at the scale the Cove Kaz volumes would require.

## Why this matters

The deal is the cleanest current example of three converging Trump-2.0 policy instruments operating at scale on a single transaction:
1. Federal financing apparatus ([[EXIM Bank]] + [[DFC]]) issuing concurrent letters at the merger announcement
2. Bilateral [[C5+1]]-derived minerals diplomacy translated into a specific deposit-level commitment
3. A US capital-markets vehicle ([[Sponsored reverse merger pipeline]]) connected to the [[Trump family investment portfolio]] anchor-LP structure

Whether the partnership becomes the model for further C1+1 minerals deals or remains a one-off depends on EXIM/DFC conversion of the LoIs and on whether the Cove Kaz development phase delivers on the announced production targets without recurrence of the ramp-and-dump pattern flagged by the House China Committee letter. Either outcome will inform how Brazil, Greenland, and the remaining four C5 states ([[Uzbekistan]], [[Kyrgyzstan]], [[Tajikistan]], [[Turkmenistan]]) approach US capital in 2026-2027.
