# Alliance Entrapment (US-Israel)

## Overview

The argument that Israel systematically escalated its confrontation with Iran in a pattern designed to draw the United States into a war that primarily serves Israeli — not American — strategic interests. Rooted in Glenn Snyder's entrapment framework from [[Alliance Theory]], this thesis treats the [[2026 Iran conflict]] (Operation Epic Fury) as its central case study.

Advanced most explicitly by [[Paul Pillar]] (Georgetown/Quincy, ex-CIA NIO), [[Trita Parsi]] (Quincy Institute), [[John Mearsheimer]] (Chicago), and [[Stephen Walt]] (Harvard). Richard Haass (former CFR president) provides a centrist version. Contested by hawkish analysts who argue Iranian threats required preemptive action regardless of Israeli preferences.

**Key distinction**: The US-Israel relationship is an *informal* alliance — no mutual defense treaty exists. Entrapment therefore operates through political mechanisms (donor pressure, media framing, shared intelligence dependency, the "special relationship" narrative) rather than legal obligation. This makes it harder to identify and harder to resist than treaty-based entrapment.

## The Mechanism

### Pillar's entrapment sequence

[[Paul Pillar]] (28-year CIA career, NIO Near East/South Asia) describes a recurring cycle:

1. **Israel initiates** — strikes on Iranian targets, assassinations, sabotage
2. **Iran responds** — retaliatory operations, calibrated and often telegraphed
3. **Israel frames response as aggression** — the retaliation is presented as unprovoked Iranian hostility
4. **US drawn in** — political and military pressure to "defend Israel" against the "aggression"

Pillar: Israeli operations are "designed at least in part to goad Iran into entering a wider war, preferably one that also involves the United States." (*Responsible Statecraft*, June 2025)

### The evidentiary base

The [[Israel-Iran Escalation]] ledger documents the pattern quantitatively:

- **Israeli offensive actions**: ~30+ major operations across 16 years (assassinations, sabotage, cyber, air campaigns, wars)
- **Iranian direct actions against Israel**: 3 total (True Promise I, True Promise II, 2026 retaliation) — all explicitly retaliatory
- **Post-October 7 shift**: 18 IRGC commanders killed in ~6 months against a baseline of deliberately avoiding IRGC casualties
- **Qualitative escalation**: systematically ascending the seniority chain until striking sovereign diplomatic territory ([[2024 Damascus consulate strike]])

### Rubio's confirmation

Secretary of State [[Marco Rubio]] stated on March 2, 2026 that Israel was planning to attack Iran unilaterally, and the US went first to avoid being caught in Iranian retaliation: "if we waited for them to hit us first after they were attacked by someone else... we would suffer more casualties" (confirmed, State Department transcript). The stated US objective — ballistic missiles and naval threat to shipping — was exactly the Israeli priority, not the nuclear program. IAEA head Grossi confirmed Iran had no active nuclear weapons program (confirmed, CNN/IAEA, March 3-4, 2026).

### The blank check analogy

Farah Jan (War on the Rocks, March 2026) formalized the parallel to 1914: Israel's position vis-a-vis the United States mirrors Austria-Hungary's "blank check" from Germany. "Washington chose war over diplomacy not because diplomacy failed, but because the junior partner's objectives required more than any deal could deliver."

In both cases: a junior alliance partner with local objectives drew a senior partner into a war the senior partner did not need, through a combination of shared threat perception, political entanglement, and the senior partner's unwillingness to restrain.

### The Mearsheimer-Walt thesis

[[John Mearsheimer]] (Chicago) explicitly connects the 2026 war to the Israel Lobby framework he and [[Stephen Walt]] developed in their 2006 *London Review of Books* article and 2007 book. The 20th anniversary of the article fell during the war itself.

Mearsheimer (Substack, ~March 3, 2026): The "Trump administration was dragged into this war by Israel and its enormously powerful lobby in the US." In "Bound to Lose" (~March 11): "The United States has already lost the war against Iran, and there is no credible path to victory." On the Grayzone (March 12, 2026, with Blumenthal/Mate): Drew direct parallels to the Iraq War chapter of the 2007 book — "there is absolutely no question that Israel and the lobby played a central role" in both wars.

Walt (*Foreign Policy*, March 2, 2026): Frames the war as part of a broader American "addiction" to military conflict rather than using the entrapment framework specifically. Since 1992, presidents from both parties promise peace but cannot resist military intervention. "The use of force has become too easy and seemingly risk-free."

### The centrist critique: Haass

Richard Haass (former CFR president) provides the centrist version, notable because he is not from the restrainer school:

- "A Questionable War of Choice" (Substack, February 28, 2026): Called it a *preventive* war — not preemptive — because "Iran posed no imminent threat" and "was not on the verge of becoming a nuclear weapons state."
- "Epic Folly" (Substack, March 12, 2026): The war "weakens the global norm against preventive war." Called for reviving diplomacy. Argued both rationales for continuing (degrading capabilities, regime change) are infeasible.

The preventive vs. preemptive distinction matters: preemptive war (against imminent attack) has some international law support; preventive war (against a future possibility) does not.

### Academic entrapment literature

The theoretical framework draws on established IR scholarship:

- **Glenn Snyder**, "The Security Dilemma in Alliance Politics" (*World Politics*, 1984) and *Alliance Politics* (Cornell UP, 1997) — the foundational entrapment concept
- **Christensen & Snyder**, "Chain Gangs and Passed Bucks" (*International Organization*, 1990) — coined "chain-ganging" to describe how tight alliances and perceptions of offensive advantage drive preemptive interventions. WWI is the paradigm case.
- **Edelstein & Shifrinson** (Georgetown), "Entrapment Revisited" — allies have "long entrapped the United States into costly foreign adventures by manipulating the United States' perception of foreign threats, exacerbating tensions with other states, and occasionally provoking crises"
- **TNSR** (2018), "Restraining an Ally: Israel, the United States, and Iran's Nuclear Program, 2011-2012" — directly examines the case where Israel "led its principal strategic ally to infer that a unilateral Israeli attack on Iran could be imminent" to enhance leverage. The US "determined not to get 'chain-ganged' into a conflict." The 2011-12 episode shows the dynamic has deep roots — but in that case, restraint succeeded.
- **Michael Beckley** (*International Security*, 2015), "The Myth of Entangling Alliances" — the counter-argument. Found only 5 debatable cases of US entanglement since 1945. Argues the US usually restrains allies. Published before the 2026 war.

## Costs to US Interests

### Economic
- [[Strait of Hormuz]] closed — Brent crude at ~$100/bbl, recession risk
- SPR already depleted from Ukraine-era releases; IEA authorized record 400M barrel release
- Gulf sovereign wealth fund investment in US may slow (Ryan Bohl, RANE, assessment)
- Inflationary shock compounding existing pressures

### Military
- US forces deployed across Gulf at risk from Iranian retaliation across 9 countries
- Interceptor stocks depleted — [[Fred Kagan]] (AEI): "Why are we short of interceptors four years into supporting Ukraine?" Production gap undermines credibility against China over Taiwan
- Heritage Foundation 2007 wargame showed the US was unprepared for exactly this Hormuz scenario; planning failures confirmed (minesweeper decommissioning, drone delays, helium gap)

### Diplomatic
- Bombed during active negotiations — the Omani proposal (confirmed, Omani FM Albusaidi on CBS, February 28) was reportedly stronger than the JCPOA. [[Rob Malley]] assessed it would have been easy for Trump to claim as a diplomatic victory (reported, Parsi account)
- China positioned as mediator; Beijing sent special envoy while the US was bombing
- Parsi: "if America's adversaries refuse to talk to the United States," that serves Israeli interests by foreclosing diplomacy permanently

### Nuclear counterproductivity
- Pillar: "No event is more likely to lead Iranian policymakers to take the decision they had not so far taken — to build a nuclear weapon — than an armed attack on their sovereign territory"
- Centrifuge cascades can be reconstructed; inspections derailed; activities move underground
- Bohl's "nuclear breakout" scenario: new Iranian leadership concludes "they attacked anyway, so why not get the bomb?"

### Strategic
- Distraction from stated US priority: great power competition with China
- Iran conflict absorbs military assets, diplomatic bandwidth, and political capital the administration's own NSS directed toward the Indo-Pacific
- Parsi: remaining escalation rungs (nuclear breakout, great power pivot to China/Russia) all make the US position worse

## The Structural Argument

Matt Duss (Center for International Policy, former Sanders foreign policy adviser) argues entrapment is not simply Israel manipulating the US but reflects a shared power structure:

"Just putting it all on Israel is analytically wrong" — the real driver is "a faction in the United States and in Israel that share a vision of a U.S.-Israel relationship that supports continuing U.S. military hegemony in the Middle East." This faction "sees Israel as essentially the U.S.'s sheriff in the region." (J Street conference, March 1, 2026)

In this framing, entrapment is not a foreign policy failure but the intended outcome of a domestic political alignment. The war serves not "Israel" abstractly but a specific US-Israeli hawkish coalition whose interests diverge from both the broad US national interest and from Israeli civilians' interests.

**Evidence cited**: Washington Post reported Israeli and Saudi officials conducted "a weeks-long lobbying effort" to persuade Trump (reported). Harrison Berger (American Conservative) identified pro-Israel megadonors (Miriam Adelson) as vectors of influence. Netanyahu told press that Trump had helped accomplish "what I have yearned to do for 40 years" (confirmed, multiple outlets).

## The Counter-Argument

The hawkish position — advanced by [[Fred Kagan]] (AEI), Matthew Kroenig (Atlantic Council), and the Israeli security establishment — is that the war serves US interests because:

1. **Existential proxy threat**: Iran's proxy network (Hezbollah's 150,000+ rockets, Hamas's October 7, Houthi shipping attacks, Iraqi militia strikes on US forces) constituted a permanent offensive threat to US allies and US personnel
2. **Nuclear latency**: Iran's enrichment to 60% created threshold capability — a political decision away from weapons
3. **Deterrence restoration**: Years of Iranian proxy escalation and US restraint had eroded deterrence across the region
4. **Opportunity**: Iran at its weakest since 1979 — Axis of Resistance stripped, protests, air defenses destroyed in June 2025
5. **October 7 lesson**: Threats left unaddressed become catastrophic

**Kagan's framing** (School of War, March 3, 2026): The coalition is targeting organs of internal suppression alongside military infrastructure — a regime change approach justified by the argument that only structural change in Iran eliminates the threat permanently. The interceptor shortage is a fixable procurement failure, not an argument against the war.

**Kroenig's framing** (*Foreign Policy* debate with Parsi, March 12, 2026): Iran has "violated key principles of the rules-based international order for decades" — threatening neighbors, sponsoring terrorism, pursuing nuclear weapons. A nuclear-armed Iran would provide "a nuclear umbrella to its proxies" and trigger Saudi/Turkish/Egyptian proliferation. "It falls on the United States to enforce the rules."

**FDD (Foundation for Defense of Democracies)** directly contested the entrapment thesis: "Israel Did Not Drag the US Into War" (March 5, 2026) called the narrative "a dangerous lie." A follow-up (March 8) framed it as a "combined effort" between "the two most powerful air forces in the world" — operational parity, shared burdens and risks.

**Michael Oren** (former Israeli ambassador to US, Fox News, ~March 9, 2026): "Iran has waged war on America for 47 years." Key argument: "While Israel has an interest in defending itself from Iranian attacks, that interest is consonant with, not superior to, America's, which is independent and critical." Oren's framing directly rejects the interest divergence premise — the US has its own reasons for the war, independent of Israel.

## Assessment

The entrapment thesis is strongest on three points:

1. **The ledger is lopsided**: Israel conducted the overwhelming majority of offensive operations. Iran's direct military actions were exclusively retaliatory. Documented in [[Israel-Iran Escalation#Escalation Ledger]].

2. **Rubio said it**: The Secretary of State confirmed the US attacked because Israel was going to attack — and the stated objectives were Israeli priorities (missiles), not US priorities (the nuclear program, which the IAEA said wasn't weaponized).

3. **The costs are measurable**: Hormuz closure, oil shock, SPR depletion, interceptor shortage, diplomatic credibility loss, China's mediator positioning — concrete harms to US interests, not theoretical risks.

The thesis is weakest on:

1. **October 7**: Hamas's attack killed 1,200 Israelis. Framing all Israeli operations as "provocation" requires minimizing a genuine catastrophic threat that was the product of decades of Iranian backing.

2. **Proxy network**: Hezbollah, Houthis, and Iraqi militias attacked US allies and US forces directly. The threat was real, not manufactured.

3. **Agency**: The entrapment frame risks denying American agency — the US chose this war through its own political process. Duss's structural argument is more honest: a shared faction wanted it, and the faction won.

**Synthesis**: Israel drove the kinetic escalation that created the conditions for war. A US domestic faction aligned with Israeli hawks enabled the decision. The war's costs fall disproportionately on the United States. The benefits — if any — accrue primarily to Israel's regional position. Whether that constitutes "entrapment" or "shared strategic choice by an aligned faction" is the key analytical divide between Pillar and Duss.
