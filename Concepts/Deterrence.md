# Deterrence

## Definition
A strategy to prevent adversary action by threatening unacceptable costs. The goal is to convince an opponent that the costs of aggression outweigh any potential gains.

## Key Thinkers
- Bernard Brodie
- Thomas Schelling
- Herman Kahn
- Albert Wohlstetter

## Core Principles
1. **Capability**: Must possess means to inflict unacceptable damage
2. **Credibility**: Adversary must believe you will use it
3. **Communication**: Threat must be clearly conveyed
4. **Rationality**: Assumes adversary makes cost-benefit calculations

## Types of Deterrence

### By Method
- **Deterrence by punishment**: Threaten retaliation (nuclear weapons)
- **Deterrence by denial**: Convince adversary they cannot achieve objectives

### By Scope
- **General deterrence**: Ongoing peacetime posture
- **Immediate deterrence**: Response to specific crisis
- **Extended deterrence**: Protecting allies (NATO Article 5)

### Nuclear Concepts
- **Mutually Assured Destruction (MAD)**: Both sides can devastate the other
- **Second-strike capability**: Ability to retaliate after absorbing first strike
- **Nuclear triad**: Land, sea, air delivery systems

## Silo-field air defense (China, May 2026)

Reuters' May 2026 satellite-imagery report on Chinese launch pads near ICBM silo fields is a deterrence-by-survivability signal. The point is not to threaten first use; it is to make a counterforce strike less credible by surrounding fixed nuclear infrastructure with active defenses. That supports second-strike credibility while also making crisis interpretation harder for adversaries.

## Historical Examples
- Cold War nuclear standoff
- NATO deterrence of Soviet invasion
- Israeli ambiguous nuclear posture

## Challenges
- Deterring non-state actors (terrorists)
- Cyber domain deterrence
- Credibility of extended deterrence
- Inadvertent escalation
