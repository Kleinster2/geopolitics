# China energy security strategy

[[China]]'s multi-layered approach to reducing vulnerability to seaborne energy import disruption. Combines strategic stockpiling, infrastructure diversification, unconventional gas development, and a long-term transition to domestically produced renewable energy — all shaped by historical experience with energy coercion and the structural reality that ~75% of China's oil demand is met by imports, ~90% of which arrive by sea.

---

## Historical driver: Sino-Soviet energy cutoff

China has firsthand experience with energy weaponization. In the 1960s, as the Sino-Soviet relationship collapsed, China was heavily dependent on the [[Soviet Union]] for refined products — particularly jet fuel for its military. The USSR cut back refined oil product exports as leverage. This remains a formative reference point in Chinese energy policy — the determination to never again depend on a single adversary for critical energy inputs.

[[Erica Downs]] (Columbia CGEP, March 4 2026): *"China has firsthand experience of being in a tense relationship with another major power and having that major power squeeze the country with respect to imported oil products."*

---

## Pillar 1: Strategic petroleum reserves

China's combined strategic and commercial oil stockpiles provide ~120 days of net crude oil import coverage at 2025 import levels (Downs estimate). This exceeds the [[IEA]]'s 90-day requirement for member states (China is not an IEA member). The program began over 20 years ago; early internal debates questioned whether the cost was justified given rapidly growing imports.

Additional buffers: Iranian and Russian crude in floating storage off the coast of China and [[Malaysia]], plus Iranian oil in bonded storage at Chinese ports. These are available for drawdown during disruptions.

Unlike oil, China has no strategic gas reserve of comparable scale — a significant gap exposed by the [[2026 Iran conflict|Strait of Hormuz closure]], which cut ~1/3 of China's LNG imports (almost entirely [[Qatar]]-sourced).

---

## Pillar 2: Overland pipeline diversification

To reduce seaborne import vulnerability, China built overland oil and gas pipelines from [[Russia]] and [[Kazakhstan]]. These provide supply that cannot be interdicted by naval blockade — a direct hedge against the scenario where a hostile navy (specifically the [[United States]]) cuts China's maritime energy lifeline.

Key routes:
- [[ESPO pipeline]] (Russia → Daqing) — crude oil
- [[Power of Siberia]] (Russia → northeast China) — natural gas
- Central Asia-China pipeline (Kazakhstan/Turkmenistan → Xinjiang) — natural gas
- Myanmar-China pipeline (Indian Ocean bypass) — crude oil and gas

---

## Pillar 3: Unconventional gas development

43% of China's natural gas production came from unconventional sources in 2025 (including shale and tight gas). On paper, China's shale resources rival the United States. But structural factors prevented a US-style revolution: state-owned enterprises (not nimble independents) control upstream assets; no private landowner compensation mechanism; and Beijing prioritized renewables over shale.

Downs: *"It's going to be more of a shale evolution"* — a gradual buildup rather than a transformative boom.

---

## Pillar 4: Green technology as geopolitical tool

China's renewable energy transition serves three goals simultaneously:

1. **Decarbonization** — meeting climate commitments
2. **Energy security** — reducing import dependency by generating power domestically
3. **Geopolitical influence** — positioning China as the global supplier of green technology

The strategic calculus: countries that spend heavily on energy imports can instead buy Chinese solar panels, EVs, and batteries. This reduces their foreign exchange burden while creating dependency on Chinese manufacturing — a softer form of energy influence than the US "energy dominance" model (LNG/oil exports as leverage).

Downs frames the contrast: the US approach is "more of a stick," while China offers something "quite appealing to a lot of countries in the world" — affordable, reliable, domestically generated energy via Chinese technology.

**Pakistan as proof of concept:** individual households and businesses buying Chinese rooftop solar panels to replace expensive fuel oil imports. Downs, who studied Pakistan's energy sector circa 2018-2019, noted the country was spending significant foreign exchange on imported fuel oil for power generation. Chinese solar is now reducing that bill.

## Peak oil demand — the structural endgame

China's oil demand is peaking faster than expected, further reducing long-term energy vulnerability:

| Fuel | Status | Driver |
|------|--------|--------|
| Diesel | Already peaked | Property/construction collapse |
| Gasoline | Peaking now | Rapid EV uptake |
| Overall oil demand | Peak forecast: **2027** | IEA, Chinese national oil companies |

[[Sinopec]] at one point projected the peak as early as 2025. China will still need oil for petrochemicals — feedstocks for EVs, solar panels, lithium-ion batteries. But transport fuel demand is structurally declining. Each year of declining demand reduces the leverage that energy-exporting states and naval powers hold over China.

---

## Vulnerability: LNG

The major gap in China's energy security architecture. China imports ~1/3 of its LNG from the [[Middle East]] (mostly Qatar), and has no strategic gas reserve equivalent to its oil stockpile. The [[2026 Iran conflict|Hormuz closure]] exposed this: Qatari and UAE LNG flows halted entirely, and Chinese state traders refused spot market purchases at elevated prices. Short-term options are limited to demand destruction, increased domestic production, and sourcing from non-Hormuz suppliers (Australia, US, Russia).

---

## 2026 Iran conflict — stress test

The [[2026 Iran conflict]] provided the first real-world stress test of China's energy security architecture. Key findings:

- **SPR adequate:** 120 days of import cover means China could sustain a total import shutoff for four months — but this scenario is hypothetical; Russian and other non-Hormuz crude continues flowing.
- **Teapot refineries exposed:** The [[Chinese teapot refineries|teapots]] — small independent refineries that buy sanctioned crude at discount — were hit by a double supply shock: [[Venezuela]] disrupted by US takeover of crude marketing, then [[Iran]] disrupted by the conflict. But floating/bonded storage provides a short-term buffer.
- **LNG is the weak point:** No strategic gas reserve, no quick substitute for Qatari volumes, and demand destruction via price is the primary mechanism.
- **Diplomatic response calibrated:** Beijing sent a special envoy for mediation, expressed concern about attacks on the [[UAE]] (where China has economic assets), but avoided military involvement. China walks a tightrope between [[Iran]] and the Gulf states — it has no formal treaty allies and no interest in getting drawn into a regional war.
