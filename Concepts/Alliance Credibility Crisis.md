# Alliance Credibility Crisis

The hypothesis that US allies' confidence in Article 5-type security guarantees has degraded materially during [[Donald Trump|Trump]]'s second term — and that this degradation may be **irreversible** under any future US president because the decisions allies are making now (independent defense budgets, nuclear deterrents, alternative alliances) operate on multi-year horizons that span US electoral cycles.

## The core thesis

Articulated by [[Jim Sciutto]] (April 2026, based on direct interviews with high-level officials in Asia, Europe, and Middle East during 2025-2026 book research):

1. Allies no longer trust the US to come to their defense the way they did pre-2025
2. They cannot bet that [[Donald Trump|Trump]] was an aberration — a future Democratic president cannot credibly guarantee the pattern won't return
3. Therefore, fiduciary duty requires decisions that protect their peoples independently
4. Those decisions (defense budgets, nuclear programs, alternative alliances) take years to execute
5. By the time they come online, the US-centric security architecture has structurally changed — the crisis is locked in even if Trump-style politics recede

## Drivers

### Rhetoric
- Trump's "[[NATO]] is a paper tiger" (April 2026, post-Iran war)
- Trump blaming allies for not joining the Iran war (South Korea, Japan, Australia)
- Trump territorial threats against [[Denmark]]/[[Greenland]] and [[Canada]]
- "I'm done with you guys — your problem"

### Actions
- [[Greenland Crisis]] — US threatening NATO ally to force territorial concession
- Tariffs on allies
- Rubio's non-defense of NATO in Iran-war spat
- [[Marco Rubio]]'s prior bipartisan [[NATO]]-withdrawal prohibition law now quiet in public discourse

### Operational signals
- Uniformed officer departures from the Pentagon (unclear if dissent-driven or routine)
- F-15E rescue press conference tone suggesting executive self-congratulation over doctrinal sobriety

## Downstream effects

### Nuclear proliferation — verified 2025-2026 developments

Per [[Jim Sciutto]] (April 2026 podcast), compounded by independent reporting:

**[[Japan]]** — elite-led debate, PM policy walk-back (grassroots less active than Sciutto framing implies):
- PM [[Sanae Takaichi]] (Nov 2025): sidestepped firm commitment to [[Three Non-Nuclear Principles]] remaining in strategic documents under review
- Dec 2025: unnamed senior PMO security advisor told reporters Japan "should possess nuclear weapons" (personal view); LDP official Onodera called for debate
- **Takaichi Feb 24, 2026 red line**: rejected NATO-style nuclear sharing with US, reaffirmed principles — so debate but not policy shift
- **Asahi April 2025 poll**: only 15% believe US would protect Japan in crisis; 77% say it would not. University of Tokyo ROLES confidence in US nuclear umbrella fell 5.5 points YoY
- Takaichi plans to skip April 2026 NPT review conference
- Per The Diplomat (Feb 2026): "public debate lags behind" elite/policy discussion

**[[South Korea]]** — strongest grassroots/mainstream signal:
- **Asan Institute 2025 Poll**: record 76.2% support for indigenous nuclear weapons (up 5.3 pts YoY, all-time high since 2010); 66.3% support redeployment of US tactical nukes
- Defense News (Nov 2025): "South Korea's nuclear debate is no longer taboo" — mainstream across generations, elites, parties
- **Oct 2025 Trump–Lee Jae Myung summit**: Trump agreed to support ROK nuclear submarines, civilian uranium enrichment, spent fuel reprocessing — formally opens the [[Nuclear Latency]] pathway

**[[Germany]]-[[France]] nuclear arrangement** — formalized March 2, 2026:
- **Macron [[Île-Longue Speech]] (March 2, 2026)**: announced France will increase warheads above ~290 current level (first increase since 1992); unveiled "forward deterrence" / "advanced deterrence" strategy; France will allow "circumstantial deployments" of nuclear-capable aircraft to allied territory
- **[[Macron-Merz Joint Declaration 2026]]** (March 2): established bilateral nuclear steering group for doctrinal dialogue; German conventional participation in French nuclear exercises starting 2026
- **Key limit**: Macron insisted "no sharing of the final decision" — adds to, does not replace, NATO nuclear sharing; French sovereignty preserved
- **8 participating countries**: UK, Germany, Poland, Netherlands, Belgium, Greece, Sweden, Denmark
- Merz has not abandoned US extended deterrence — this is hedging, not substitution

**[[Poland]]** — strongest single statement:
- **PM [[Donald Tusk]] March 2026**: "Poland will eventually seek its own nuclear weapons"; entered talks with France on advanced deterrence umbrella (Bloomberg, Japan Times, Atlantic Council)
- Opposition President [[Karol Nawrocki]] favors US-only path — domestic political split on direction

**[[Australia]]** — early 2026 Strategic Defence Partnership with EU (hedging beyond [[AUKUS]]); no independent nuclear debate found

**Sciutto thesis qualification** based on verification:
- "Multi-year nuclear-hedging decisions that cannot easily be undone" — analytically defensible but predictive. Verified steps so far (subs, enrichment, exercises, steering groups) are **reversible**, not weapons acquisition
- Thesis strength depends on whether these precursors harden into weapons programs, or get rolled back under a different US administration

Previously, US extended deterrence was the non-proliferation pillar — "we've got you covered" removed the strategic need for allies to build their own. If that pillar weakens, [[Nuclear Proliferation|proliferation]] becomes rational even for countries that signed [[NPT|NPT]] in good faith.

### Alternative alliance structures
- EU intra-bloc defense arrangements accelerating
- [[France]]-[[UK]] troop deployment to [[Ukraine]] — acting without explicit US coordination
- [[Japan]]-[[South Korea]]-[[Australia]] Pacific coordination strengthening

### Economic de-risking
- EU trade arrangements that reduce dependence on US market access
- Reserve diversification away from USD (separate driver but compounding)

### Reversibility obstacle
Even a future pro-alliance US president faces:
- Decisions already made (German nuclear sharing with France, Japanese defense industrial base)
- Sunk political costs in ally countries that opted for independent posture
- Structural trust deficit: allies have learned the US is a politically variable partner on multi-year horizons

## Distinguishing features vs. prior alliance strains

Previous US foreign-policy shocks (Iraq 2003, Trump 1.0 trade war, Afghanistan 2021) damaged specific alliances or specific programs. What distinguishes the 2025-2026 episode, per Sciutto's thesis:

- **Scope**: simultaneous across Europe, Asia, Middle East
- **Duration**: two Trump terms bracketing a single Biden term produces a decade of instability rather than a four-year outlier
- **Substance**: territorial threats to allies (Greenland, Canada) are qualitatively different from trade disputes
- **Paper-tiger adoption**: when the US president adopts the adversary's preferred description of the alliance, the alliance's deterrent value erodes with or without formal withdrawal

## Forecasting implications

**Sciutto's assessment**: damage is "deeply and possibly irreparably damaged."

**Counterargument (weak)**: A post-2028 Democratic president could rebuild trust through rapid ally outreach, personnel signaling (former diplomats returning), and legislative constraints on future unilateral action.

**Counterargument (strong)**: Even if trust can be rebuilt, the multi-year capability decisions (nuclear programs, industrial base, pre-positioned alliances) can't be un-decided without massive sunk-cost waste. Allies would rather maintain redundancy than re-trust.

**What to watch**:
- Whether [[Japan]] and [[South Korea]] move from debate to concrete nuclear infrastructure decisions
- Whether [[Germany]] formalizes nuclear-sharing arrangement with [[France]]
- Whether [[Kevin Rudd]] or successor Australian diplomats re-pitch [[AUKUS]] on an altered basis
- Whether [[NATO]] formally revises Article 5 interpretation (unlikely but possible)
