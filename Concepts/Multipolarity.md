# Multipolarity

## Definition
An international system with three or more great powers of roughly comparable strength. Contrasts with unipolarity (one dominant power) and bipolarity (two dominant powers).

## System Types Compared

| System | Powers | Stability | Examples |
|--------|--------|-----------|----------|
| Unipolar | 1 | Debated | US post-1991 |
| Bipolar | 2 | Stable? | Cold War |
| Multipolar | 3+ | Unstable? | 19th century Europe, emerging? |

## Historical Examples

### Concert of Europe (1815-1914)
- Five great powers: Britain, France, Russia, Prussia/Germany, Austria
- Managed through diplomacy, balance of power
- Eventually collapsed into WWI

### Interwar Period (1919-1939)
- Britain, France, US, Germany, Japan, USSR
- Failed multipolarity: economic nationalism, revisionism
- Collapsed into WWII

## Theoretical Debates

### Realist Views
- **Waltz**: Bipolarity more stable (clarity, fewer miscalculations)
- **Mearsheimer**: Multipolarity dangerous (uncertainty, shifting alliances)
- **Classical realists**: Balance of power can manage multipolarity

### Liberal Views
- Institutions can stabilize multipolar systems
- Economic interdependence constrains conflict
- Democratic peace within multipolar orders
